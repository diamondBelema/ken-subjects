## c-ant-001

# Thorax Overview

The thorax is the region of the body between the neck and the abdomen, enclosed by the rib cage. It contains all thoracic viscera except the lungs in the mediastinum, with the lungs occupying the pleural cavities on either side.

The thoracic cavity is bounded superiorly by the thoracic inlet (superior thoracic aperture), inferiorly by the diaphragm, anteriorly by the sternum and costal cartilages, posteriorly by the thoracic vertebrae and ribs, and laterally by the ribs and intercostal muscles.

Key contents include: the heart and great vessels, trachea and main bronchi, esophagus, thymus, phrenic and vagus nerves, thoracic duct, and lymph nodes.

## c-ant-001:summary

The thorax is the body's central compartment housing vital cardiovascular and respiratory structures, protected by the bony thoracic cage and divided by the mediastinum.

---

## c-ant-002

# Pleura

The pleurae are serous membranes composed of a single layer of simple squamous mesothelial cells supported by connective tissue. They line the lungs and thoracic cavity, permitting effective and easy respiration.

Each pleura is divided into two continuous parts: the **visceral pleura** covering the lung surfaces and the **parietal pleura** lining the thoracic wall, mediastinum, and diaphragm. The two layers are continuous at the lung hilum.

The **pleural cavity** is the potential space between the visceral and parietal pleurae, containing approximately 25 mL of serous fluid per lung. This fluid serves two functions: (1) lubricating the pleural surfaces to allow sliding during respiration, and (2) producing surface tension that couples the parietal and visceral pleurae, ensuring the lungs expand when the thorax expands.

## c-ant-002:summary

The pleurae are bilateral serous membranes with visceral and parietal layers, creating a fluid-filled potential space that enables frictionless lung movement and couples lung expansion to chest wall movement.

---

## c-ant-003

# Lungs

The lungs are paired, spongy, expandable organs occupying the thoracic cavity, separated by the heart and mediastinal structures. Each lung is conical, with an apex, base, three surfaces (costal, diaphragmatic, mediastinal), and three borders (anterior, posterior, inferior).

The right lung is larger (~625 g), shorter, and wider with three lobes (superior, middle, inferior) divided by two fissures (oblique and horizontal). The left lung (~565 g) has two lobes (superior and inferior) separated by one oblique fissure, and features a cardiac notch with the lingula.

The lungs are suspended freely within the pleural membrane, anchored only at the hilum where structures (bronchi, pulmonary vessels, bronchial vessels, nerves, lymphatics) enter and exit.

## c-ant-003:summary

The lungs are paired respiratory organs with the right having three lobes and the left two lobes. They are connected to the mediastinum at the hilum and enclosed by the pleural membranes.

---

## c-ant-004

# Tracheobronchial Tree

The tracheobronchial tree is the branching system of air passages conducting air from the trachea to the terminal bronchioles where gas exchange begins. It forms the conducting portion of the respiratory system.

The hierarchy proceeds: trachea → main (primary) bronchi → lobar (secondary) bronchi → segmental (tertiary) bronchi → smaller bronchi → bronchioles → terminal bronchioles → respiratory bronchioles → alveolar ducts → alveolar sacs → alveoli.

Functions include conducting, filtering, humidifying, and warming inspired air, protecting against foreign particles, and distributing air evenly throughout the lungs.

## c-ant-004:summary

The tracheobronchial tree is an inverted-tree branching system that conducts and conditions inspired air from the trachea to the alveoli through progressively smaller airways.

---

## c-ant-005

# Diaphragm

The diaphragm is a double-domed musculotendinous partition separating the thoracic and abdominal cavities, and is the chief muscle of respiration.

It has two surfaces: a superior (convex, facing thoracic cavity, covered by pleura and pericardium) and an inferior (concave, facing abdominal cavity, covered by peritoneum).

The diaphragm consists of a peripheral muscular part (attached to thoracic wall) and a central tendinous part (non-contractile aponeurosis). The right dome is higher (~5th rib) due to the liver; the left dome is lower due to the heart and stomach.

## c-ant-005:summary

The diaphragm is the primary muscle of respiration, a dome-shaped partition with muscular periphery and central tendon, separating the thoracic and abdominal cavities.

---

## c-ant-006

# Mediastinum

The mediastinum is the central compartment of the thoracic cavity, the space between the pleural cavities. It extends from the superior to inferior thoracic aperture and contains all thoracic viscera except the lungs.

Key contents include the heart and great vessels, esophagus, trachea and its bifurcation, thymus, thoracic duct, lymph nodes, phrenic and vagus nerves. A loose connective tissue holds these structures together.

The mediastinum is divided by the **transverse thoracic plane** (passing from the 2nd costal cartilage to the lower border of T4 vertebra) into:
- **Superior mediastinum** (above the plane)
- **Inferior mediastinum** (below the plane), further divided by the pericardium into:
  - Anterior mediastinum
  - Middle mediastinum
  - Posterior mediastinum

## c-ant-006:summary

The mediastinum is the central thoracic compartment containing vital structures, divided by the transverse thoracic plane into superior and inferior (anterior, middle, posterior) divisions.

---

## c-ant-007

# Histology of Respiratory System

The respiratory system is divided into the conducting portion (nasal cavities, pharynx, trachea, bronchi, bronchioles) and the respiratory portion (lungs, alveoli).

The passages are lined predominantly by **pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium** (respiratory epithelium), kept moist by serous, goblet, and mucous glands. Mucoid secretions trap dust particles, cilia transport them toward the pharynx, and deep blood vessels warm inspired air.

The respiratory epithelium contains multiple cell types: goblet cells (mucus production), ciliated cells (mucociliary clearance), basal cells (progenitor cells), brush cells, and neuroendocrine (Kulchitsky) cells.

## c-ant-007:summary

The respiratory system histology features respiratory epithelium (pseudostratified ciliated columnar) with specialized cell types for protection, filtration, and gas exchange, supported by glands, cartilage, and smooth muscle.

---

## c-ant-008

# Development of Respiratory System

The respiratory system develops from the **respiratory diverticulum (lung bud)**, an outgrowth from the ventral wall of the foregut appearing at approximately the 4th week of development.

Fibroblast growth factors (FGFs) signal the endoderm, making the epithelia of the larynx, trachea, bronchi, and lungs of endodermal origin, while cartilaginous, muscular, and connective tissues derive from splanchnic mesoderm.

The **tracheoesophageal ridges** separate the lung bud from the foregut, and their fusion forms the tracheoesophageal septum, dividing the foregut into the esophagus (dorsally) and trachea with lung buds (ventrally).

## c-ant-008:summary

The respiratory system develops from a foregut outgrowth (lung bud) at week 4, with endodermal epithelia and mesodermal support tissues, separated from the esophagus by the tracheoesophageal septum.

---

## c-ant-009

# Parietal Pleura

The parietal pleura lines the internal surface of the thoracic cavity, adhering to the thoracic wall (ribs), mediastinum, and diaphragm. It is thicker than the visceral pleura.

The parietal pleura is divided into four parts based on the surface it contacts:
- **Costal pleura** - covers the internal thoracic wall
- **Mediastinal pleura** - covers the lateral mediastinum
- **Diaphragmatic pleura** - covers the superior diaphragmatic surface
- **Cervical pleura (cupula)** - covers the lung apex extending into the root of the neck

The parietal pleura is innervated by somatic nerves (intercostal and phrenic), making it sensitive to pressure, pain, temperature, and touch. Blood supply comes from intercostal, internal thoracic, and musculophrenic arteries. Venous drainage is to the azygos system.

## c-ant-009:summary

The parietal pleura is the thicker outer pleural layer lining the thoracic cavity, divided into costal, mediastinal, diaphragmatic, and cervical parts, with somatic innervation providing pain and sensory awareness.

---

## c-ant-010

# Visceral Pleura

The visceral (pulmonary) pleura covers all surfaces of the lungs, including the horizontal and oblique fissures. In dissection, it cannot be separated from the lung surface.

It provides the lung with a smooth slippery surface, enabling free movement on the parietal pleura. Its sensory fibers are only sensitive to stretch (not pain).

Nerve supply is via autonomic innervation from the pulmonary plexus (sympathetic trunk and vagus nerve). Arterial supply is from the bronchial arteries (branches of the descending aorta).

## c-ant-010:summary

The visceral pleura is the thin inner pleural layer adherent to the lung surface, providing a smooth gliding surface, innervated by autonomic fibers (sensitive only to stretch), and supplied by bronchial arteries.

---

## c-ant-011

# Pleural Cavity

The pleural cavity is a potential space between the parietal and visceral pleurae. It normally contains a small volume of serous fluid (~25 mL per lung).

Two major functions:
1. **Lubrication** - allows the pleural surfaces to slide over each other during respiration
2. **Surface tension** - couples the parietal and visceral pleurae together, ensuring that when the thorax expands, the lungs also expand and fill with air

The pleural cavity is not completely filled by the lungs anteriorly and posteroinferiorly, giving rise to recesses where opposing parietal pleural surfaces touch.

## c-ant-011:summary

The pleural cavity is a fluid-filled potential space between pleural layers that lubricates and couples lung-chest wall movement, with recesses where the lungs do not fully fill the space.

---

## c-ant-012

# Pleural Recesses

Pleural recesses are areas where the opposing surfaces of the parietal pleura come into contact because the lungs do not completely fill the pleural cavity. They are clinically important as sites where fluid can collect (pleural effusion).

Two main recesses:
1. **Costodiaphragmatic recess** - between the costal pleura and the diaphragmatic pleura, the most dependent part of the pleural cavity
2. **Costomediastinal recess** - between the costal pleura and the mediastinal pleura, behind the sternum

The left costomediastinal recess is larger because the cardiac notch in the left lung is more pronounced.

## c-ant-012:summary

Pleural recesses (costodiaphragmatic and costomediastinal) are potential spaces where parietal pleural surfaces meet, serving as common sites for fluid accumulation in pleural effusion.

---

## c-ant-013

# Costal Pleura

The costal (costovertebral) pleura covers the internal surfaces of the thoracic wall, separated from the ribs by the endothoracic fascia (extra-pleural layer). It covers the inner surface of the sternum, ribs and costal cartilages, intercostal muscles and membranes, and sides of thoracic vertebrae.

The extra-pleural layer forms a natural cleavage plane for surgical separation of the costal pleura from the thoracic wall.

Innervated by intercostal nerves.

## c-ant-013:summary

The costal pleura lines the internal thoracic wall, separated from ribs by endothoracic fascia which provides a surgical cleavage plane, innervated by intercostal nerves.

---

## c-ant-014

# Mediastinal Pleura

The mediastinal pleura covers the lateral aspect of the mediastinum. It continues superiorly into the root of the neck as the cervical pleura, and is continuous with the costal pleura antero-posteriorly and with the diaphragmatic pleura inferiorly.

Innervated by the phrenic nerve.

## c-ant-014:summary

The mediastinal pleura covers the lateral mediastinum, continuous with costal pleura and diaphragmatic pleura, and extends superiorly as cervical pleura.

---

## c-ant-015

# Diaphragmatic Pleura

The diaphragmatic pleura covers the thoracic (superior) surface of the diaphragm on each side of the mediastinum, except along its costal attachments and where the diaphragm is fused to the pericardium.

A thin layer of endothoracic fascia (phrenicopleural fascia) connects the diaphragmatic pleura with the muscular fibers of the diaphragm.

Innervated by the phrenic nerve over the domes and intercostal nerves around the periphery.

## c-ant-015:summary

The diaphragmatic pleura covers the superior diaphragmatic surface, connected by phrenicopleural fascia, innervated by phrenic nerve centrally and intercostal nerves peripherally.

---

## c-ant-016

# Cervical Pleura

The cervical pleura (cupula) covers the apex of the lung, the part extending through the superior thoracic aperture into the root of the neck. It forms a cup-like dome over the lung apex.

The cervical pleura is reinforced by the suprapleural membrane (Sibson's fascia), which separates it from the subclavian artery and vein that groove the anterior surface of the lung apex.

The apex extends 3-4 cm above the level of the anterior end of the 1st rib, and about 2.5 cm above the medial third of the clavicle.

## c-ant-016:summary

The cervical pleura is the dome-shaped uppermost pleural reflection over the lung apex, reinforced by Sibson's fascia, extending above the 1st rib into the root of the neck.

---

## c-ant-017

# Lungs - External Features

At birth the lungs are pinkish. In young adults they are brown or grey. In smokers and those in industrialized areas, the lungs become mottled black due to carbon particle deposition.

The lungs weigh approximately: right lung ~625 g, left lung ~565 g. When empty of blood, the right weighs about 240 g, left less. Male lungs are heavier than female lungs, though this depends on height.

Each lung presents an apex (rounded superior end extending into the root of the neck), a base (resting on the diaphragm), three surfaces, and three borders.

## c-ant-017:summary

The lungs are paired spongy organs, right ~625 g and left ~565 g, with color varying from pink (birth) to brown/grey (adult) to black (smokers), each having an apex, base, three surfaces, and three borders.

---

## c-ant-018

# Lungs - Surfaces

Each lung has three surfaces:

1. **Costal surface** - smooth and convex, facing the internal chest wall, bearing rib marks, lined by costal pleura and endothoracic fascia.

2. **Diaphragmatic (basal) surface** - concave, resting on the dome of the diaphragm. The right has a deeper concavity due to the higher right hemidiaphragm (liver). On the right it separates the lung from the liver; on the left from the liver, stomach fundus, and spleen.

3. **Medial (mediastinal) surface** - has two parts:
   - Vertebral part (posterior 1/3) - rounded, lies in the paravertebral gutter, related to vertebral bodies, intervertebral discs, posterior intercostal vessels, and splanchnic nerve
   - Mediastinal part (anterior 2/3) - overlies the heart, pericardium, and mediastinal structures, featuring the hilum and cardiac impression (deeper on the left)

## c-ant-018:summary

Each lung has a convex costal surface against the chest wall, a concave diaphragmatic base, and a medial mediastinal surface bearing the hilum and cardiac impression (deeper on the left).

---

## c-ant-019

# Lungs - Lobes and Fissures

**Right lung** has three lobes and two fissures:
- **Oblique fissure** - begins posteriorly at T3, runs downward and forward to end at the 6th costochondral junction
- **Horizontal fissure** - runs from the 4th costal cartilage posteriorly to meet the oblique fissure near the 5th rib at the midaxillary line
- Creates: superior lobe (above horizontal fissure), middle lobe (smallest, between horizontal and oblique fissures), and inferior lobe (behind oblique fissure, largest)

**Left lung** has two lobes and one fissure:
- **Oblique fissure** only (no horizontal fissure)
- Creates: superior lobe and inferior lobe
- The **lingula** on the superior lobe corresponds to the right middle lobe, located below the cardiac notch

## c-ant-019:summary

The right lung has 3 lobes (superior, middle, inferior) separated by oblique and horizontal fissures; the left lung has 2 lobes (superior, inferior) with only an oblique fissure and a lingula corresponding to the right middle lobe.

---

## c-ant-020

# Bronchopulmonary Segments

A bronchopulmonary segment is a pyramidal-shaped functional and surgical unit of the lung. Each is supplied by a segmental (tertiary) bronchus and a segmental branch of the pulmonary artery, but drained by intersegmental pulmonary veins.

There are **10 segments in the right lung** and **8-10 segments in the left lung**.

Right lung segments: Apical (S1), Posterior (S2), Anterior (S3) — Upper lobe; Lateral (S4), Medial (S5) — Middle lobe; Superior (S6), Medial basal (S7), Anterior basal (S8), Lateral basal (S9), Posterior basal (S10) — Lower lobe.

Surgical relevance: It is the smallest area of lung that can be isolated and removed without affecting adjacent regions.

## c-ant-020:summary

Bronchopulmonary segments are the smallest functional-surgical units of the lung, each with its own segmental bronchus and pulmonary artery branch, with 10 on the right and 8-10 on the left.

---

## c-ant-021

# Hilum of the Lung

The hilum is a large depressed, wedge-shaped area on the medial surface of each lung, lying near the center of the mediastinal surface. It is located approximately behind the 3rd and 4th costal cartilages at the sternal margin, or at the level of T5-T7 vertebrae.

Various structures enter and leave the lungs via the hilum: principal bronchus, pulmonary arteries, superior and inferior pulmonary veins, bronchial arteries and veins, pulmonary lymph nodes and vessels, and autonomic nerve plexuses.

From anterior to posterior, the arrangement of structures in the hilum is: **Vein - Artery - Bronchus (VAB)**.

## c-ant-021:summary

The hilum is the wedge-shaped mediastinal surface depression where bronchi, pulmonary vessels, bronchial vessels, nerves, and lymphatics enter/exit the lung, arranged anterior-to-posterior as Vein-Artery-Bronchus.

---

## c-ant-022

# Root of the Lung

The root of the lung is a short tubular collection of structures that together attach the lungs to the mediastinum. It consists of all structures entering and leaving the lung at the hilum.

It is wrapped by a sleeve of mediastinal pleura that reflects onto the lung surface as visceral pleura. The pleura extends distally as the **pulmonary ligament**.

Components include: principal bronchus (left) or eparterial and hyparterial bronchi (right), pulmonary arteries, superior and inferior pulmonary veins, bronchial arteries (2 left, 1 right), bronchial veins, pulmonary lymph nodes and vessels, and anterior and posterior pulmonary autonomic nerve plexuses.

## c-ant-022:summary

The lung root is the pedicle of structures (bronchi, vessels, nerves, lymphatics) connecting each lung to the mediastinum, wrapped in a pleural sleeve extending as the pulmonary ligament.

---

## c-ant-023

# Trachea

The trachea is a wide, flexible tubular structure approximately 10-12 cm long and 2 cm in diameter. It begins at the lower border of the cricoid cartilage (C6) and extends to the carina (T4-T5) where it bifurcates into the left and right main bronchi.

It is composed of **16-20 C-shaped rings of hyaline cartilage** which keep the lumen open. The cartilage is deficient posteriorly, where the gap is bridged by the **trachealis muscle** (smooth muscle) and fibroelastic tissue, allowing flexibility during inspiration and expiration.

The trachea divides outside the lungs at the level of the lower border of T4 into the left and right primary bronchi. At the bifurcation, the lowest tracheal cartilage has a hook-shaped process called the **carina**.

## c-ant-023:summary

The trachea is a 10-12 cm cartilaginous tube from C6 to T4, supported by 16-20 C-shaped hyaline cartilage rings with the trachealis muscle posteriorly, bifurcating at the carina into main bronchi.

---

## c-ant-024

# Main Bronchi

The trachea bifurcates at the sternal angle (of Louis) at T4-T5 into:

**Right main bronchus**: ~2.5 cm long, wider, shorter, and more vertical — a direct continuation of the trachea. Foreign bodies are more likely to lodge here. Before entering the lung at T5, it gives off the eparterial bronchus (for the superior lobe), then divides into three secondary (lobar) bronchi: upper lobe, middle lobe, and lower lobe bronchi.

**Left main bronchus**: ~5 cm long, narrower, longer, and more horizontal due to the position of the heart. It divides into two secondary bronchi: upper lobe and lower lobe bronchi.

## c-ant-024:summary

The right main bronchus is short, wide, and vertical (2.5 cm); the left is long, narrow, and horizontal (5 cm). The right gives 3 lobar bronchi, the left gives 2.

---

## c-ant-025

# Bronchioles

Bronchioles are airways less than 1 mm in diameter. They have **no cartilage or glands** and their wall is predominantly smooth muscle.

**Terminal bronchioles**: lined by columnar epithelium, no cartilage, contain smooth muscle, and contain Clara cells (which replace goblet cells). These represent the end of the conducting zone.

**Respiratory bronchioles**: lined by cuboidal epithelium, no mucous glands, represent the transition zone between conducting airways and gas-exchange portions. They give rise to alveolar ducts, which open into alveolar sacs and alveoli.

Neuroendocrine cells in bronchioles secrete serotonin and bombesin that regulate muscle tone. Bronchospasm in allergic conditions results from spasm of the smooth muscle in bronchiolar walls.

## c-ant-025:summary

Bronchioles are small airways (<1 mm) lacking cartilage and glands, consisting mainly of smooth muscle. Terminal bronchioles end the conducting zone; respiratory bronchioles begin gas exchange.

---

## c-ant-026

# Diaphragm - Structure

The diaphragm is dome-shaped when relaxed. The **right dome** is higher (at the 5th rib) due to the liver; the **left dome** is at the lower border of the 5th rib due to the heart and stomach.

The **central tendon** is at the level of the xiphisternal joint. In full expiration, the right dome can be found at the level of the 4th intercostal space (nipple level). Posteriorly, the central tendon can be traced to the intervertebral disc between T8-T9.

**Origin** of the muscular part has three components:
- **Sternal part**: two muscular slips from the posterior surface of the xiphoid process
- **Costal part**: inner surfaces of lower six ribs (ribs 7-12) and their costal cartilages
- **Lumbar (vertebral) part**: via right and left crura from lumbar vertebrae — right crus from L1-L3 and their discs, left crus from L1-L2 and discs

## c-ant-026:summary

The diaphragm has a dome shape with right dome higher than left, central tendon at xiphisternal level, and three origins: sternal, costal (ribs 7-12), and lumbar (crura from L1-L3).

---

## c-ant-027

# Diaphragm - Openings

The diaphragm has three major openings:

1. **Vena caval opening (T8)**: located in the central tendon, transmits the inferior vena cava and terminal branches of the right phrenic nerve.

2. **Esophageal hiatus (T10)**: located in the muscular part (right crus), transmits the esophagus, anterior (left) and posterior (right) vagal trunks, and esophageal branches of the left gastric artery and vein.

3. **Aortic hiatus (T12)**: located behind the diaphragm between the crura, transmits the descending aorta, azygos vein, and thoracic duct.

## c-ant-027:summary

The diaphragm has three openings at different vertebral levels: vena caval (T8), esophageal (T10), and aortic (T12), transmitting major neurovascular structures and the esophagus between thorax and abdomen.

---

## c-ant-028

# Diaphragm - Nerve Supply

**Motor innervation**: Phrenic nerves (C3, C4, C5) — "C3, 4, 5 keep the diaphragm alive."

**Sensory innervation (pain and proprioception)**:
- Central part: phrenic nerve
- Peripheral part: lower intercostal nerves (T5-T11) and subcostal nerve (T12)

**Functions**:
- Muscle of inspiration: contraction pulls central tendon down, increasing vertical thoracic diameter
- Muscle of abdominal straining: assists anterior abdominal wall muscles in raising intra-abdominal pressure for micturition, defecation, and parturition
- Thoracoabdominal pump: descent decreases intrathoracic pressure and increases intra-abdominal pressure, compressing IVC blood upward into the right atrium

## c-ant-028:summary

The diaphragm is motor-innervated by phrenic nerves (C3-5), with pain sensation from phrenic (central) and intercostal nerves (peripheral), serving as the primary inspiratory and straining muscle.

---

## c-ant-029

# Superior Mediastinum

The superior mediastinum is wedge-shaped, lying above the transverse thoracic plane (T4/5 level).

**Boundaries**:
- Superior: thoracic inlet
- Anterior: dorsal surface of the manubrium
- Posterior: ventral surfaces of T1-T4 vertebral bodies
- Inferior: continuous with inferior mediastinum at the transverse thoracic plane
- Lateral: medial borders of the pleural sacs (mediastinal pleura)

**Contents**: Arch of aorta and its branches (brachiocephalic trunk, left common carotid, left subclavian), superior vena cava, brachiocephalic veins, azygos system, right and left vagus nerves, left recurrent laryngeal nerve, phrenic nerves, cardiac nerves and plexus, sympathetic trunk, trachea, esophagus, thoracic duct, thymus, sternohyoid and sternothyroid muscles.

**Key relationships**: The thymus is most anterior. The brachiocephalic veins cross anterior to the arteries. The trachea is anterior to the esophagus. The thoracic duct is right posterior to the esophagus.

## c-ant-029:summary

The superior mediastinum is the wedge-shaped compartment above T4/5 containing the arch of aorta, great vessels, trachea, esophagus, thymus, and major nerves, continuous with the neck above and inferior mediastinum below.

---

## c-ant-030

# Anterior Mediastinum

The anterior mediastinum lies in front of the heart and pericardium, inferior to the manubrium, and is continuous superiorly with the inferior part of the superior mediastinum.

**Contents**: Internal thoracic artery and veins, pre-pericardial lymph nodes, parasternal and anterior mediastinal lymph nodes, sternopericardial ligaments, fat with loose connective tissue, small branches of internal thoracic arteries, and thymus in infants and children.

**Clinical relevance**: Common site for lymphadenopathy or retrosternal thyroid goiters extending inferiorly.

## c-ant-030:summary

The anterior mediastinum is the space anterior to the pericardium containing thymus (in children), internal thoracic vessels, lymph nodes, fat, and sternopericardial ligaments.

---

## c-ant-031

# Middle Mediastinum

The middle mediastinum is the central division of the inferior mediastinum, containing the heart and great vessels.

**Boundaries**: Anterior mediastinum (pericardium) anteriorly, posterior mediastinum posteriorly, transverse thoracic plane superiorly, diaphragm inferiorly, mediastinal pleura laterally.

**Contents**: Heart and pericardium, ascending aorta, pulmonary trunk and arteries, superior vena cava (lower part), azygos vein (arches over right lung root), pulmonary veins, main bronchi (right and left), phrenic nerves, pericardiacophrenic vessels, and lymph nodes (tracheobronchial, hilar).

**Key relationships**: Ascending aorta is anterior and to the right of the pulmonary trunk. SVC lies to the right of the ascending aorta. Phrenic nerves descend anterior to the lung roots. Main bronchi lie posterior to the pulmonary vessels and SVC.

## c-ant-031:summary

The middle mediastinum contains the heart, pericardium, ascending aorta, pulmonary trunk and veins, SVC, main bronchi, and phrenic nerves, forming the central cardiovascular compartment.

---

## c-ant-032

# Posterior Mediastinum

The posterior mediastinum lies deep to the heart, supporting the longitudinal pathway of thoracic structures to the abdomen.

**Boundaries**: Pericardium and diaphragm anteriorly, T5-T12 vertebral bodies posteriorly, transverse thoracic plane superiorly, diaphragm inferiorly, mediastinal pleura laterally.

**Contents**: Descending thoracic aorta, esophagus, azygos vein, hemiazygos and accessory hemiazygos veins, thoracic duct, sympathetic trunk, splanchnic nerves, esophageal nerve plexus, posterior mediastinal lymph nodes.

**Key relationships**: The esophagus lies anterior to the thoracic aorta and slightly to the right in the upper thorax, shifting left as it descends. The thoracic duct lies between the thoracic aorta (left) and azygos vein (right), posterior to the esophagus.

## c-ant-032:summary

The posterior mediastinum contains the descending aorta, esophagus, azygos system, thoracic duct, and sympathetic chain, forming the posterior neurovascular pathway between thorax and abdomen.

---

## c-ant-033

# Azygos System of Veins

The azygos system drains venous blood from the body walls and mediastinal viscera to the superior vena cava.

**Azygos vein**: begins as the union of the right lumbar and right subcostal veins, passes through the aortic opening in the diaphragm, ascends on the posterior chest wall to T4, then arches over the right lung root to enter the SVC. Tributaries include the lower eight posterior intercostal veins, right superior intercostal vein, and hemiazygos veins.

**Hemiazygos vein**: arises on the left (similar origin to azygos), passes through the aortic opening, extends to T9, then crosses diagonally behind the aorta and thoracic duct to drain into the azygos vein at T8. Tributaries: lower four left posterior intercostal veins.

**Accessory hemiazygos vein**: crosses to the right to drain into the azygos vein at T7. Tributaries: middle posterior intercostal veins, bronchial veins, esophageal veins.

## c-ant-033:summary

The azygos system comprises the azygos (right), hemiazygos (left lower), and accessory hemiazygos (left upper) veins, draining posterior intercostal and mediastinal venous blood to the SVC.

---

## c-ant-034

# Thoracic Aorta

The thoracic aorta is the descending part of the aorta, beginning at the lower border of T4, lying slightly left of the midline on the vertebral column, passing through the aortic hiatus of the diaphragm at T12 to become the abdominal aorta.

**Branches**:
- **Unpaired (from anterior surface)**: bronchial arteries, esophageal arteries, mediastinal branches
- **Paired (from posterolateral aspect)**: posterior intercostal arteries, subcostal arteries, superior phrenic arteries

The thoracic aorta also gives rise to the vertebral artery, thyrocervical trunk, and internal thoracic (mammary) artery from its first part. The internal thoracic artery gives anterior intercostal arteries, branches to the thymus, and perforating branches.

## c-ant-034:summary

The thoracic aorta descends from T4 to T12 along the left vertebral column, giving bronchial, esophageal, intercostal, and phrenic branches before passing through the diaphragm.

---

## c-ant-035

# Respiratory Epithelium

Respiratory epithelium is a **pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium** lining most of the conducting airways (nasal cavity respiratory region, trachea, bronchi).

It contains several cell types:
- **Ciliated columnar cells** - the most numerous, with motile cilia that move mucus toward the pharynx
- **Goblet cells** - scattered, produce mucous that traps dust particles
- **Basal cells** - lie near the basal lamina, give rise to ciliated cells to replace those lost
- **Non-ciliated columnar cells** with microvilli - probably secrete serous fluid keeping mucosa moist
- **Brush cells** and **Kulchitsky (neuroendocrine) cells** - secrete serotonin and bombesin

The epithelium becomes less tall with fewer goblet cells in the bronchi, transitions to simple ciliated columnar in smaller bronchi, and becomes cuboidal in the respiratory bronchioles. Cilia are more abundant in the lower trachea.

## c-ant-035:summary

Respiratory epithelium is pseudostratified ciliated columnar with goblet cells, basal cells, and neuroendocrine cells, progressively simplifying from trachea to bronchioles, providing mucociliary clearance and air conditioning.

---

## c-ant-036

# Goblet Cells

Goblet cells are numerous in the respiratory epithelium and produce mucous. The mucous traps dust particles from inhaled air and is moved by ciliary action toward the pharynx.

They are most abundant in the upper trachea and become progressively fewer distally. They are absent in the bronchioles, where they are replaced by Clara cells.

In allergic conditions, goblet cell hyperplasia can occur, leading to excessive mucus production.

## c-ant-036:summary

Goblet cells are unicellular mucous glands in respiratory epithelium that trap particulates, most numerous in the trachea and absent in bronchioles (replaced by Clara cells).

---

## c-ant-037

# Clara Cells

Clara cells (Club cells) are non-ciliated cells predominantly seen in terminal bronchioles, where they replace goblet cells.

Functions:
- Produce one component of surfactant
- Secrete a fluid that spreads over the alveolar surface, reducing surface tension
- Protection against development of emphysema
- Act as stem cells for the bronchiolar epithelium
- Protection against inhaled harmful substances

## c-ant-037:summary

Clara (Club) cells are non-ciliated bronchiolar cells replacing goblet cells, producing surfactant components, reducing surface tension, acting as progenitor cells, and providing protection against inhaled toxins.

---

## c-ant-038

# Type I Pneumocytes

Type I pneumocytes (Type I alveolar cells or squamous epithelial cells) form the lining of **90% of the alveolar surface**.

They are the most numerous alveolar cells, squamous in shape, with extremely thin cytoplasm (0.05-0.2 μm thickness). The edges of adjacent cells overlap and are united by tight junctions.

They form the major structural component of the **blood-air barrier**, together with the capillary endothelial cells and their basement membranes.

## c-ant-038:summary

Type I pneumocytes are thin squamous cells lining 90% of the alveolar surface, forming the primary structural component of the blood-air barrier for gas exchange.

---

## c-ant-039

# Type II Pneumocytes

Type II pneumocytes (also called septal cells) are rounded or cuboidal secretory cells with microvilli, covering **5% of the alveolar surface**.

They contain secretory granules called **multilamellar bodies** (composed of several layers) that produce **pulmonary surfactant**.

They can differentiate into Type I pneumocytes, serving as progenitor cells for the alveolar epithelium. They develop at the end of the 6th month of fetal life.

## c-ant-039:summary

Type II pneumocytes (septal cells) are cuboidal secretory cells covering 5% of alveolar surface, producing surfactant from multilamellar bodies, and capable of differentiating into Type I pneumocytes.

---

## c-ant-040

# Blood-Air Barrier

The blood-air barrier (respiratory membrane) is the structure across which gas exchange occurs between alveolar air and pulmonary capillary blood. It consists of:

1. A thin layer of **surfactant** on the alveolar surface
2. **Cytoplasm of Type I pneumocytes** (alveolar epithelial cells)
3. **Basement membrane of pneumocytes** (fused in places with capillary BM)
4. Intervening **connective tissue** (collagen, elastic fibers)
5. **Basement membrane of endothelial cells**
6. **Cytoplasm of capillary endothelial cells**

The endothelial cells of alveolar capillaries are thin with numerous projections increasing the surface area for gas exchange. At places, the two basement membranes are fused, reducing barrier thickness.

## c-ant-040:summary

The blood-air barrier consists of surfactant, Type I pneumocyte cytoplasm and basement membrane, intervening connective tissue, and capillary endothelial basement membrane and cytoplasm, forming an ultra-thin membrane for gas exchange.

---

## c-ant-041

# Pulmonary Surfactant

Pulmonary surfactant is a fluid that spreads over the alveolar surface, containing **phospholipids, proteins, and glycosaminoglycans**.

Functions:
- **Reduces surface tension** and prevents collapse of alveoli during expiration (atelectasis)
- **Stabilizes alveolar diameters** and facilitates their expansion
- **Bactericidal properties**
- Prevents development of an air-blood interface with high surface tension

Surfactant is produced by Type II pneumocytes, constantly renewed, and removed from the surface by Type I pneumocytes and macrophages.

In fetal life, surfactant amount increases particularly during the last 2 weeks before birth. Before birth, lungs are full of fluid containing surfactant from Type II cells. After birth, most lung fluid is rapidly reabsorbed, leaving surfactant as a thin phospholipid coat.

## c-ant-041:summary

Pulmonary surfactant is a phospholipid-protein mixture from Type II pneumocytes that reduces alveolar surface tension, preventing atelectasis, stabilizing alveolar size, and providing bactericidal protection.

---

## c-ant-042

# Alveolar Macrophages

Alveolar macrophages (also called dust cells) are the third cell type found in alveoli.

They enter the connective tissue from blood and migrate into the alveolar space. Dust particles phagocytosed by them are seen in their cytoplasm — hence the name "dust cells."

They phagocytose and remove inhaled particles, pathogens, and surfactant from the alveolar surface, playing a crucial role in pulmonary defense.

## c-ant-042:summary

Alveolar macrophages (dust cells) are blood-derived phagocytic cells in alveoli that engulf dust, pathogens, and debris, serving as the primary defense mechanism of the alveolar space.

---

## c-ant-043

# Lung Bud Formation

The respiratory diverticulum (lung bud) appears as an outgrowth from the ventral wall of the foregut at approximately the **4th week** of development.

The location of the bud is signaled by **fibroblast growth factors (FGFs)** which instruct the endoderm. Thus the epithelia of the larynx, trachea, bronchi, and lungs are of **endodermal origin**, while the cartilaginous, muscular, and connective tissues derive from **splanchnic mesoderm**.

The lung bud initially has open communication with the foregut. As it expands caudally, two longitudinal ridges (**tracheoesophageal ridges**) separate it from the foregut.

## c-ant-043:summary

The lung bud forms at week 4 as a foregut outgrowth signaled by FGFs, with endodermal epithelia and mesodermal support tissues, separated from the foregut by tracheoesophageal ridges.

---

## c-ant-044

# Tracheoesophageal Septum

When the tracheoesophageal ridges fuse, they form the **tracheoesophageal septum**, dividing the foregut into two structures:
- **Esophagus** (dorsally)
- **Trachea and lung buds** (ventrally)

Abnormalities in partitioning result in **esophageal atresia** with or without **tracheoesophageal fistulas**. 85-90% result in the upper esophagus ending in a blind pouch with the lower segment forming a fistula with the trachea.

Other developmental abnormalities include ectopic lung lobes, congenital cysts, and respiratory distress syndrome (hyaline membrane disease).

## c-ant-044:summary

The tracheoesophageal septum divides the foregut into esophagus (dorsal) and trachea/lung buds (ventral); its malformation causes esophageal atresia and tracheoesophageal fistulas.

---

## c-ant-045

# Lung Maturation

Lung maturation proceeds through several phases:

By the **5th week**, the 2 bronchial buds enlarge to form right (3 secondary bronchi) and left (2 secondary bronchi) main bronchi, representing the future lobes.

By the **6th week**, about 17 generations of subdivisions are formed, with 10 tertiary/segmental bronchi in the right lung and 8 in the left, forming the bronchopulmonary segments.

Bronchioles continue to divide until the **7th month** (canalicular phase). At the **7th month**, sufficient capillaries are present for gas exchange, and premature infants can survive.

At the end of the **6th month**, Type II pneumocytes develop (produce surfactant). Type I pneumocytes form the blood-air barrier.

About **1/6** of the adult number of alveoli are present at birth; the remaining alveoli form during the first **10 years** of postnatal life.

## c-ant-045:summary

Lung maturation progresses from bronchial budding (week 5) through segmental branching (week 6), canalicular phase (month 7, viability), with only 1/6 of alveoli present at birth, completing development over 10 years postnatally.

---

## c-ant-046

# Alveoli

Alveoli are thin-walled sac-like evaginations at the terminal end of the bronchial tree, forming the spongy parenchyma of the lung.

There are approximately **200 million** alveoli in a normal lung with a total surface area of **75 square meters**. The total capillary surface area available for exchange is **125 square meters**.

In section, alveoli resemble a honeycomb. They are separated by the **interalveolar septum**, which contains a network of capillaries supported by reticular and elastic fibers, fibroblasts, macrophages, and mast cells.

The septum contains **pores of Kohn** (alveolar pores) that allow passage of air between alveoli, equalizing pressure. Elastic fibers enable expansion during inspiration and passive contraction during expiration. Reticular fibers prevent over-distention.

Three cell types are found in alveoli: Type I pneumocytes, Type II pneumocytes, and alveolar macrophages (dust cells).

## c-ant-046:summary

Alveoli are ~200 million thin-walled gas-exchange sacs with 75 m² surface area, separated by capillary-rich septa with pores of Kohn, lined by Type I and II pneumocytes, and containing elastic/reticular fibers.

---

## c-ant-047

# Vascular Supply of Lungs

The lungs have a **dual blood supply**:

**Pulmonary circulation**: Right and left pulmonary arteries carry deoxygenated blood from the pulmonary trunk to the lungs. Oxygenated blood returns via four pulmonary veins (superior and inferior on each side) to the left atrium.

**Bronchial circulation**: The bronchial arteries (2 on the left, 1 on the right) arise from the descending thoracic aorta and supply the bronchi, lung roots, visceral pleura, and supporting lung tissue with oxygenated nutritive blood.

**Venous drainage**: Right bronchial vein drains into the azygos vein; left bronchial vein drains into the accessory hemiazygos vein or left superior intercostal vein.

## c-ant-047:summary

The lungs receive deoxygenated blood via pulmonary arteries for gas exchange and oxygenated blood via bronchial arteries for nutrition, with venous drainage to the azygos system.

---

## c-ant-048

# Lymphatic Drainage of Lungs

The superficial and deep lymphatic plexuses of the lung drain into the **bronchopulmonary lymph nodes**, which then drain into the superior and inferior **tracheobronchial lymph nodes**, and finally into the right and left **bronchiomediastinal lymph trunks**.

Parietal pleural lymphatics pass to the intercostal, parasternal, diaphragmatic, and posterior mediastinal nodes.

The parasternal nodes are situated at the anterior ends of the upper five intercostal spaces along the internal thoracic artery; their efferents drain to the bronchiomediastinal trunk. These lymphatic vessels may become involved in cancer of the breast.

## c-ant-048:summary

Lung lymphatics drain via bronchopulmonary nodes to tracheobronchial nodes to bronchiomediastinal trunks; parietal pleural lymphatics drain to intercostal, parasternal, and mediastinal nodes.

---

## c-ant-049

# Nerve Supply of Lungs

The nerves of the lungs are derived from the **pulmonary plexuses**, containing sympathetic, parasympathetic, and visceral afferent fibers.

**Parasympathetic** (from vagus nerve):
- Stimulate secretion from bronchial glands
- Cause contraction of bronchial smooth muscle
- Cause vasodilation of pulmonary vessels

**Sympathetic** (from sympathetic trunks):
- Stimulate relaxation of bronchial smooth muscle
- Cause vasoconstriction of pulmonary vessels

**Visceral afferent**: Conduct stretch impulses to the sensory ganglion of the vagus nerve.

## c-ant-049:summary

Lung innervation comes from pulmonary plexuses: parasympathetic (vagus) causes bronchoconstriction and secretion; sympathetic causes bronchodilation; visceral afferents conduct stretch sensations.

---

## c-ant-050

# Trachealis Muscle

The trachealis muscle is a band of smooth muscle that bridges the open posterior ends of the C-shaped tracheal cartilage rings.

It connects the ends of the cartilaginous rings and allows flexibility of the trachea during inspiration and expiration. Contraction can narrow the tracheal lumen.

It is innervated by the autonomic nervous system and plays a role in coughing reflexes by narrowing the airway to increase expiratory velocity.

## c-ant-050:summary

The trachealis muscle is smooth muscle bridging the posterior gap of C-shaped tracheal cartilage rings, allowing tracheal flexibility and lumen narrowing during coughing.
