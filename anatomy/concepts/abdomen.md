## c-abdomen

The abdomen is the region of the body between the thorax and pelvis. It is flexible, expansible, and houses vital organs including the spleen, liver, stomach, intestines, kidneys, pancreas, and gallbladder.

**Boundaries:**
- **Anterolaterally:** muscles forming the anterolateral abdominal wall
- **Posteriorly:** lumbar vertebrae and paravertebral muscles
- **Superiorly:** diaphragm
- **Inferiorly:** pelvic inlet/pelvic diaphragm

## c-abdomen:summary

The abdomen lies between thorax and pelvis, bounded by the diaphragm superiorly, pelvic inlet inferiorly, abdominal wall muscles anterolaterally, and lumbar vertebrae posteriorly.

---

## c-abdominal-wall

The anterolateral abdominal wall is a muscular and fascial structure that protects abdominal viscera, assists in posture, micturition, defecation, and parturition. Its integrity is critical for preventing herniation of abdominal contents.

## c-abdominal-wall:summary

The anterolateral abdominal wall is a muscular wall protecting abdominal viscera and assisting in functions like defecation and parturition.

---

## c-abdominal-wall-layers

The abdominal wall has the following layers from superficial to deep:

1. **Skin** - thin anteriorly, thick posteriorly, with horizontal tension lines
2. **Superficial fascia** - two layers:
   - **Camper's fascia** (superficial fatty layer)
   - **Scarpa's fascia** (deep membranous layer) - continuous with Colles fascia in the perineum, dartos fascia in the scrotum, and fascia lata in the thigh
3. **Deep fascia** - thin layer over muscles
4. **Muscles** - three flat muscles and rectus abdominis
5. **Transversalis fascia** - deep to the muscles
6. **Extraperitoneal fat** - variable layer
7. **Parietal peritoneum** - innermost layer

**Clinical Note:** Extravasation of fluid below the umbilicus can track into the perineum via Scarpa's fascia continuity with Colles fascia.

## c-abdominal-wall-layers:summary

From superficial to deep: skin, superficial fascia (Camper's + Scarpa's), deep fascia, muscles, transversalis fascia, extraperitoneal fat, and parietal peritoneum. Scarpa's fascia is continuous with Colles fascia in the perineum.

---

## c-abdominal-wall-muscles

Five muscles form the anterolateral abdominal wall:

1. **External oblique** - largest and most superficial
   - Origin: external surfaces of last 8 ribs
   - Insertion: linea alba, pubic tubercle, iliac crest
   - Nerve supply: lower 6 intercostal nerves + subcostal nerve
   - Lower free border forms the inguinal ligament (of Poupart)

2. **Internal oblique** - lies deep to external oblique, fibers run at right angles
   - Origin: thoracolumbar fascia, anterior 2/3 of iliac crest, lateral half of inguinal ligament
   - Insertion: inferior borders of ribs 10-12, linea alba, pecten pubis
   - Nerve supply: lower 6 intercostal nerves + L1

3. **Transversus abdominis** - innermost flat muscle
   - Origin: lateral surfaces of costal cartilages 7-12, thoracolumbar fascia, iliac crest, lateral 1/3 of inguinal ligament
   - Insertion: linea alba, pubic crest, pecten pubis

4. **Rectus abdominis** - strap-like muscle enclosed in rectus sheath
   - Origin: pubic symphysis and pubic crest (two heads)
   - Insertion: costal cartilages 5-7 and xiphoid process
   - Three tendinous intersections at umbilicus, xiphisternum, and midway between

5. **Pyramidalis** - small triangular muscle, absent in 20% of population
   - Tenses the linea alba

## c-abdominal-wall-muscles:summary

Five muscles: external oblique (superficial, fibers inferomedially), internal oblique (fibers superomedially), transversus abdominis (deepest flat muscle), rectus abdominis (vertical, in rectus sheath), and pyramidalis (small, absent in 20%). All flat muscles are supplied by lower 6 intercostal nerves.

---

## c-rectus-sheath

The rectus sheath is a strong fibrous envelope enclosing the rectus abdominis and pyramidalis muscles, formed by aponeuroses of the three flat muscles.

**Formation varies in three regions:**
- **Above costal margin:** posterior wall absent; sheath formed by external oblique only; rectus rests on costal cartilages
- **Between costal margin and arcuate line:** internal oblique splits into anterior and posterior layers; external oblique fuses with anterior layer; transversus fuses with posterior layer
- **Below arcuate line (midway between umbilicus and pubic symphysis):** all three aponeuroses pass anterior to rectus; posterior wall absent; rectus lies on transversalis fascia

**Linea alba:** strong fibrous midline structure from xiphoid process to pubic symphysis, formed by all three aponeuroses.

**Contents of rectus sheath:** rectus abdominis, pyramidalis, superior and inferior epigastric arteries, lower 6 intercostal nerves.

## c-rectus-sheath:summary

The rectus sheath encloses rectus abdominis and varies in formation: above costal margin (no posterior wall), between costal margin and arcuate line (anterior and posterior walls), and below arcuate line (no posterior wall, all aponeuroses anterior).

---

## c-inguinal-canal

The inguinal canal is a ~4 cm oblique passage through the anterior abdominal wall, connecting the extraperitoneal space to the scrotum or labia majora.

**Boundaries:**
- **Anterior:** aponeuroses of external and internal oblique
- **Posterior:** transversalis fascia medially, conjoint tendon
- **Roof:** internal oblique and transversus abdominis aponeuroses
- **Floor:** inguinal ligament, lacunar ligament medially

**Rings:**
- **Deep inguinal ring:** oval opening in transversalis fascia, 1.25 cm above midpoint of inguinal ligament
- **Superficial inguinal ring:** triangular opening in external oblique aponeurosis

**Contents:** spermatic cord and ilioinguinal nerve (male); round ligament of uterus and ilioinguinal nerve (female)

**Hesselbach's Triangle:** bounded laterally by inferior epigastric artery, medially by lateral edge of rectus abdominis, inferiorly by inguinal ligament. Hernias medial to inferior epigastric artery are direct; lateral are indirect.

## c-inguinal-canal:summary

The inguinal canal is a 4 cm oblique passage with deep ring (transversalis fascia) and superficial ring (external oblique). Contains spermatic cord (male) or round ligament (female). Direct hernias pass medial to inferior epigastric artery (through Hesselbach's triangle); indirect hernias pass lateral to it (through deep ring).

---

## c-abdominal-regions

The abdomen is divided into 9 regions by four imaginary lines:

**Lines:**
- **Two vertical:** right and left midclavicular lines (midpoint of clavicle to midinguinal point)
- **Two horizontal:** transpyloric line (midway between jugular notch and pubic symphysis = L2 level) and intertubercular line (across iliac tubercles)

**Regions:**
- **Upper row:** right hypochondriac, epigastric, left hypochondriac
- **Middle row:** right lumbar (flank), umbilical, left lumbar (flank)
- **Lower row:** right iliac (inguinal), hypogastric (pubic), left iliac (inguinal)

**Transpyloric plane (L2) passes through:** pylorus, tip of 9th costal cartilage, conus medullaris, horizontal part of duodenum, neck of pancreas, fundus of gallbladder, portal vein formation, SMA origin.

## c-abdominal-regions:summary

Nine regions defined by two vertical (midclavicular) and two horizontal (transpyloric at L2 and intertubercular) lines. The transpyloric plane at L2 is a key anatomical landmark passing through pylorus, portal vein formation, and SMA origin.

---

## c-peritoneum

The peritoneum is a double-layered serous membrane lining the abdominal cavity and covering abdominal organs. Both layers are composed of simple squamous mesothelium.

**Two layers:**
1. **Parietal peritoneum** - lines the abdominal wall
   - Derived from somatic mesoderm
   - Somatic innervation (well-localized pain)
   - Sensitive to pressure, pain, temperature, laceration

2. **Visceral peritoneum** - covers abdominal organs
   - Derived from splanchnic mesoderm
   - Autonomic innervation (poorly localized, referred pain)
   - Only sensitive to stretch and chemical irritation

## c-peritoneum:summary

The peritoneum is a double-layered serous membrane: parietal (lines abdominal wall, somatic innervation, well-localized pain) and visceral (covers organs, autonomic innervation, poorly localized pain).

---

## c-peritoneal-cavity

The peritoneal cavity is the potential space between parietal and visceral peritoneum, normally containing a small amount of lubricating serous fluid.

**Two divisions:**
1. **Greater sac** - main peritoneal cavity
   - **Supracolic compartment** (above transverse mesocolon): stomach, liver, spleen
   - **Infracolic compartment** (below transverse mesocolon): small and large intestines

2. **Lesser sac (omental bursa)** - lies posterior to stomach and lesser omentum
   - Communicates with greater sac through the **epiploic foramen (of Winslow)**

**Organ classification by peritoneal relationship:**
- **Intraperitoneal:** stomach, liver, spleen, jejunum, ileum, transverse colon, sigmoid colon (covered by visceral peritoneum)
- **Retroperitoneal (SAD PUCKER):** Suprarenal glands, Aorta/IVC, Duodenum (2nd-4th parts), Pancreas (except tail), Ureters, Colon (ascending/descending), Kidneys, Esophagus, Rectum
  - *Primarily retroperitoneal:* kidneys, ureters, suprarenal glands, duodenum (2nd-4th), pancreas (except tail)
  - *Secondarily retroperitoneal:* ascending and descending colon

## c-peritoneal-cavity:summary

The peritoneal cavity has a greater sac (supracolic and infracolic compartments) and lesser sac (omental bursa), communicating via epiploic foramen of Winslow. Retroperitoneal organs remembered by SAD PUCKER mnemonic.

---

## c-peritoneal-reflections

The peritoneum forms several important folds connecting organs:

**Omenta:**
- **Greater omentum:** 4 layers of visceral peritoneum hanging from greater curvature of stomach; attaches to transverse colon; called "abdominal policeman" for its immune role
- **Lesser omentum:** double layer from lesser curvature of stomach and proximal duodenum to liver; contains hepatogastric ligament and hepatoduodenal ligament (free edge containing portal triad)

**Mesentery:**
- Double layer of visceral peritoneum connecting intraperitoneal organs to posterior abdominal wall
- Provides pathway for nerves, blood vessels, and lymphatics
- **Mesentery proper:** attaches jejunum and ileum to posterior wall
- **Transverse mesocolon:** attaches transverse colon
- **Sigmoid mesocolon:** attaches sigmoid colon

**Ligaments:**
- **Gastrosplenic ligament:** stomach to spleen (carries short gastric and left gastroepiploic vessels)
- **Lienorenal (splenorenal) ligament:** spleen to left kidney (carries splenic vessels and pancreatic tail)
- **Falciform ligament:** liver to anterior abdominal wall (carries ligamentum teres)

**Pouches (pelvic):**
- **Male:** rectovesical pouch (between rectum and bladder)
- **Female:** vesicouterine pouch (between bladder and uterus) and rectouterine pouch (of Douglas, between rectum and uterus)

## c-peritoneal-reflections:summary

Peritoneal reflections include greater omentum (4 layers, "abdominal policeman"), lesser omentum (contains portal triad), mesentery (conveys neurovascular structures), and various ligaments. The rectouterine pouch (of Douglas) is the lowest peritoneal point in females.

---

## c-abdominal-vessels

The abdominal vasculature includes the abdominal aorta (systemic arterial supply) and two venous systems: the systemic (IVC) and the portal venous system.

**Abdominal aorta:** continuation of thoracic aorta at T12, ~13 cm long, bifurcates into common iliac arteries at L4.

**Branches grouped as:**
- **Unpaired (anterior):** celiac trunk, SMA, IMA, median sacral artery
- **Paired (lateral):** inferior phrenic, middle suprarenal, renal, gonadal, lumbar arteries

## c-abdominal-vessels:summary

The abdominal aorta begins at T12, gives unpaired anterior branches (celiac trunk, SMA, IMA) and paired lateral branches (renal, gonadal, lumbar), and bifurcates into common iliac arteries at L4.

---

## c-celiac-trunk

The celiac trunk is the first major anterior branch of the abdominal aorta, arising at T12 (aortic hiatus of diaphragm). It is ~1 cm long and divides into three branches:

1. **Left gastric artery** - smallest branch; ascends to supply lesser curvature of stomach and esophagus; anastomoses with right gastric artery

2. **Splenic artery** - largest branch; tortuous course along superior border of pancreas; passes through lienorenal ligament; gives off:
   - Short gastric arteries (to fundus of stomach)
   - Left gastroepiploic artery (to greater curvature)
   - Pancreatic branches

3. **Common hepatic artery** - passes to the right; divides into:
   - **Proper hepatic artery** - supplies liver via right and left hepatic arteries; gives off cystic artery to gallbladder and right gastric artery
   - **Gastroduodenal artery** - descends behind duodenum; gives off right gastroepiploic artery and superior pancreaticoduodenal artery

**Clinical:** Peptic ulcers can erode the gastroduodenal artery causing severe GI bleeding. Coeliac trunk compression syndrome occurs when the median arcuate ligament compresses the trunk.

## c-celiac-trunk:summary

The celiac trunk (T12) divides into left gastric, splenic (largest, tortuous), and common hepatic arteries. It supplies foregut structures. The stomach uniquely receives blood from all three branches via anastomoses along both curvatures.

---

## c-mesenteric-vessels

**Superior Mesenteric Artery (SMA):**
- Arises at L1, immediately inferior to celiac trunk
- Supplies midgut (from major duodenal papilla to proximal 2/3 of transverse colon)
- **Branches:** inferior pancreaticoduodenal, jejunal and ileal (arcades with vasa recta), middle colic, right colic, ileocolic arteries
- **Relations:** anterior to left renal vein, uncinate process of pancreas, and 3rd part of duodenum

**Inferior Mesenteric Artery (IMA):**
- Arises at L3, near inferior border of duodenum
- Supplies hindgut (distal 1/3 of transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid colon, rectum)
- **Branches:** left colic, sigmoid arteries (2-4), superior rectal artery (terminal branch)

**Anastomoses:**
- **Marginal artery (of Drummond):** continuous arterial circle along inner border of colon, formed by SMA and IMA branches
- **Arc of Riolan:** anastomosis between middle colic (SMA) and left colic (IMA) arteries

**Watershed area:** splenic flexure is vulnerable to ischemia during systemic hypoperfusion.

## c-mesenteric-vessels:summary

The SMA (L1) supplies midgut with jejunal/ileal arcades and colic branches. The IMA (L3) supplies hindgut. They anastomose via the marginal artery of Drummond. The splenic flexure is a watershed area vulnerable to ischemia.

---

## c-portal-system

**Portal vein** is formed posterior to the neck of the pancreas at L2 by the union of the splenic vein and superior mesenteric vein. It carries nutrient-rich blood from the GI tract to the liver.

**Tributaries:** splenic vein, SMV, inferior mesenteric vein, left gastric (coronary) vein, right gastric vein, cystic vein, paraumbilical veins, pancreaticoduodenal veins.

**Portal-systemic anastomoses** (sites of portocaval shunting):
1. **Esophageal:** left gastric vein to azygous system (esophageal varices in portal hypertension)
2. **Rectal:** superior rectal vein to inferior rectal veins (hemorrhoids)
3. **Paraumbilical:** portal veins to anterior abdominal wall veins (caput medusae)
4. **Retroperitoneal:** mesenteric veins to retroperitoneal veins

**Clinical:** Portal hypertension (from liver cirrhosis) causes dilation at anastomosis sites - esophageal varices (potentially fatal bleeding), caput medusae, hemorrhoids. Treatment: portacaval or splenorenal shunts.

**Inferior Vena Cava (IVC):** formed at L5 by common iliac veins; ascends to right of aorta; pierces diaphragm at T8 (caval hiatus); drains into right atrium. Key tributaries: renal veins, hepatic veins, right gonadal vein, lumbar veins, suprarenal veins.

## c-portal-system:summary

The portal vein (formed at L2 behind pancreas neck) drains nutrient-rich blood to the liver. Portal-systemic anastomoses at esophagus, rectum, paraumbilical region, and retroperitoneum dilate in portal hypertension, causing varices, caput medusae, and hemorrhoids. The IVC drains systemic venous blood to the right atrium.

---

## c-small-intestine

The small intestine is the longest part of the GI tract (5-7 m), responsible for 90% of nutrient absorption and most enzymatic digestion. It extends from the pyloric sphincter to the ileocecal valve.

**Three parts:**
1. **Duodenum** (~25 cm) - shortest, receives chyme, bile, and pancreatic secretions
2. **Jejunum** (~2.5 m) - major site of nutrient absorption
3. **Ileum** (~3.5 m) - longest; absorbs vitamin B12 and bile salts; contains Peyer's patches

## c-small-intestine:summary

The small intestine (5-7 m) has three parts: duodenum (25 cm, receives bile/pancreatic juice), jejunum (2.5 m, nutrient absorption), and ileum (3.5 m, B12/bile salt absorption, Peyer's patches).

---

## c-duodenum

The duodenum is the first and shortest part (~25 cm) of the small intestine, receiving chyme from the stomach, bile from the liver/gallbladder, and pancreatic enzymes.

**Histological features:**
- Simple columnar epithelium with enterocytes and goblet cells
- **Brunner's glands** in the submucosa (most distinctive feature) - secrete alkaline mucus to neutralize gastric acid
- Broad or leaf-shaped villi
- Plicae circulares present but few and small
- Fewer goblet cells compared to distal segments
- Crypts of Lieberkühn present
- No Peyer's patches

**Parts:** superior (first part, contains Brunner's glands, site of peptic ulcers), descending (second part, receives common bile duct and pancreatic duct at major duodenal papilla), horizontal (third part, crosses over IVC and aorta), ascending (fourth part, ends at duodenojejunal flexure).

**Developmental origin:** proximal half from foregut (celiac trunk supply), distal half from midgut (SMA supply).

## c-duodenum:summary

The duodenum (25 cm) is characterized by Brunner's glands in the submucosa that secrete alkaline mucus. It has broad villi, few goblet cells, and no Peyer's patches. The major duodenal papilla receives the common bile and pancreatic ducts.

---

## c-jejunum-ileum

**Jejunum (~2.5 m):**
- Major site of nutrient absorption
- Tall, finger-like villi
- Numerous prominent plicae circulares (Valves of Kerckring)
- No Brunner's glands, no Peyer's patches
- Moderate number of goblet cells
- Rich in capillaries for rapid nutrient transport
- Fewer arterial arcades with longer vasa recta

**Ileum (~3.5 m):**
- Absorbs vitamin B12, bile salts, remaining nutrients
- Shorter villi, fewer plicae circulares
- **Peyer's patches** (large aggregations of 30-50 lymphoid nodules) - immune surveillance
- Highest density of goblet cells
- No Brunner's glands
- More arterial arcades with shorter vasa recta

## c-jejunum-ileum:summary

Jejunum has tall villi, prominent plicae, fewer goblet cells, no Peyer's patches. Ileum has shorter villi, sparse plicae, most goblet cells, and Peyer's patches (lymphoid aggregates for immune surveillance).

---

## c-small-intestine-histology

**General histological layers (all three segments):**
1. **Mucosa:**
   - Simple columnar epithelium with enterocytes (brush border of microvilli) and goblet cells
   - Villi: finger-like projections increasing surface area; each contains capillaries and a central lacteal
   - Crypts of Lieberkühn (intestinal glands): between villi bases; contain Paneth cells (antimicrobial), stem cells, enteroendocrine cells
   - Lamina propria: loose CT with capillaries, lacteals, immune cells, GALT
   - Muscularis mucosae: thin smooth muscle layer

2. **Submucosa:** dense irregular CT with blood vessels, lymphatics, Meissner's plexus; Brunner's glands in duodenum only

3. **Muscularis externa:** inner circular + outer longitudinal smooth muscle layers; Auerbach's (myenteric) plexus between them

4. **Serosa or adventitia:** serosa (visceral peritoneum) for intraperitoneal portions; adventitia for retroperitoneal parts

**Cell types of mucosa:**
- **Enterocytes:** absorptive cells with microvilli (brush border), enzymes for final digestion
- **Goblet cells:** mucus-secreting; increase in number from duodenum to ileum
- **Paneth cells:** at crypt bases; secrete lysozyme, defensins (antimicrobial)
- **Enteroendocrine cells:** secrete hormones (secretin, CCK, GIP)
- **Stem cells:** at crypt bases; replace all epithelial types every 3-5 days
- **M cells:** over Peyer's patches; antigen transport for immune surveillance

**Surface modifications increase absorptive area:** villi, microvilli (brush border), plicae circulares

## c-small-intestine-histology:summary

The small intestine has villi, crypts of Lieberkühn, and a brush border of microvilli for maximum absorption. Key cell types: enterocytes (absorption), goblet cells (mucus), Paneth cells (antimicrobial at crypt bases), enteroendocrine cells (hormones), stem cells (renewal), and M cells (immune surveillance over Peyer's patches).

---

## c-large-intestine

The large intestine is approximately 1.5 m long, forming the terminal part of the GI tract from the ileocecal junction to the anus.

**Components:**
1. **Cecum** - pouch-like structure receiving chyme from ileum via ileocecal valve; attached vermiform appendix
2. **Colon** - ascending, transverse, descending, sigmoid
3. **Rectum** - straight segment storing feces
4. **Anal canal** - terminal segment with internal (smooth, involuntary) and external (skeletal, voluntary) sphincters

**Functions:** water and electrolyte absorption, mucus secretion for lubrication, fecal formation and storage, microbial fermentation (vitamin K, biotin production).

**Structural features:** teniae coli (3 bands of longitudinal muscle forming haustra), appendices epiploicae (fat-filled pouches), and haustra (sacculations).

## c-large-intestine:summary

The large intestine (1.5 m) has cecum, colon (ascending/transverse/descending/sigmoid), rectum, and anal canal. Unique features include teniae coli, haustra, and appendices epiploicae. Functions: water absorption, mucus secretion, fecal storage.

---

## c-cecum-colon

**Cecum:** blind-ended pouch receiving chyme from ileum via ileocecal valve. The vermiform appendix projects from its posteromedial wall.

**Colon parts:**
- **Ascending colon** (right side) - secondarily retroperitoneal
- **Transverse colon** (across abdomen) - intraperitoneal, suspended by transverse mesocolon
- **Descending colon** (left side) - secondarily retroperitoneal
- **Sigmoid colon** (S-shaped) - intraperitoneal, suspended by sigmoid mesocolon

**Blood supply:** SMA supplies cecum, ascending colon, and proximal 2/3 of transverse colon. IMA supplies distal 1/3 of transverse colon, descending colon, and sigmoid colon.

**The appendix:** blind-ended tube, 6-10 cm long, ~6 mm diameter, projecting from posteromedial wall of cecum below ileocecal valve. Rich in GALT; functions as immune organ and bacterial reservoir. Prone to obstruction by fecoliths or lymphoid hyperplasia causing appendicitis.

## c-cecum-colon:summary

The cecum receives ileal contents; ascending and descending colon are secondarily retroperitoneal; transverse and sigmoid are intraperitoneal. The appendix (6-10 cm) is rich in lymphoid tissue and prone to inflammation.

---

## c-rectum-anal-canal

**Rectum:** expandable terminal segment storing feces. Continuous smooth muscle (no teniae coli, no haustra). Receives blood from superior rectal artery (branch of IMA).

**Anal canal (~3-4 cm):** terminal segment with distinct histological zones:
1. **Columnar zone (upper 1/3):** simple columnar epithelium with crypts of Lieberkühn; continuous with rectal mucosa; ends at pectinate (dentate) line
2. **Transitional zone (middle 1/3):** stratified columnar/cuboidal transitioning to stratified squamous (anal pecten)
3. **Cutaneous zone (lower 1/3):** keratinized stratified squamous epithelium with hair follicles and glands

**Pectinate line:** key landmark marking the junction of endodermal (hindgut) and ectodermal (cloacal membrane) origins. Different innervation, blood supply, lymphatic drainage above and below.

**Sphincters:** internal anal sphincter (smooth muscle, involuntary) and external anal sphincter (skeletal muscle, voluntary).

## c-rectum-anal-canal:summary

The rectum stores feces with continuous muscle. The anal canal has three histological zones: columnar (upper 1/3, endoderm-derived), transitional (middle 1/3), and cutaneous (lower 1/3, ectoderm-derived). The pectinate line marks the embryological junction.

---

## c-large-intestine-histology

**Histological layers (similar to small intestine but with key differences):**

1. **Mucosa:**
   - Simple columnar epithelium with colonocytes (absorptive cells) and abundant goblet cells
   - **No villi, no plicae circulares** (unlike small intestine)
   - Straight tubular glands (crypts of Lieberkühn) with numerous goblet cells
   - Goblet cells increase in number distally, most abundant in rectum
   - **No Paneth cells** (rare or absent in large intestine)
   - Muscularis mucosae present

2. **Submucosa:** dense irregular CT with blood vessels, lymphatics, Meissner's plexus

3. **Muscularis externa:** inner circular layer (continuous); outer longitudinal layer aggregated into **3 teniae coli** (forming haustra). Auerbach's plexus between layers. In rectum, longitudinal muscle becomes continuous.

4. **Serosa or adventitia:** serosa on intraperitoneal segments (transverse/sigmoid colon); adventitia on retroperitoneal segments (ascending/descending colon). Appendices epiploicae are fat-filled pouches on adventitia.

**Cell types:**
- **Colonocytes:** absorb water and electrolytes; short apical microvilli
- **Goblet cells:** abundant, increase distally
- **Enteroendocrine cells:** secrete peptide YY, serotonin, somatostatin
- **Stem cells:** at crypt bases for renewal

## c-large-intestine-histology:summary

The large intestine lacks villi and plicae but has abundant goblet cells and straight tubular crypts. The outer longitudinal muscle forms teniae coli (forming haustra). Goblet cells increase distally. No Paneth cells. The rectum has continuous longitudinal muscle.

---

## c-appendix

The vermiform appendix is a blind-ended tube, 6-10 cm long and ~6 mm diameter, projecting from the posteromedial wall of the cecum below the ileocecal valve.

**Histology:** resembles colon with simple columnar epithelium, goblet cells, and crypts of Lieberkühn but:
- **Abundant lymphoid follicles** in lamina propria and submucosa (GALT)
- Muscularis mucosae may be incomplete or absent
- No teniae coli (unlike colon)
- Outer serosa with mesoappendix

**Functions:** immune surveillance, reservoir for beneficial gut bacteria (helps repopulate colon after infection).

**Appendicitis:** luminal obstruction (fecoliths, lymphoid hyperplasia, tumors) causes inflammation. Initially referred periumbilical pain (midgut), then localizes to right lower quadrant (parietal peritoneal irritation). Risk of perforation and peritonitis.

## c-appendix:summary

The appendix (6-10 cm) has abundant lymphoid follicles for immune surveillance and serves as a bacterial reservoir. Obstruction causes appendicitis with initial periumbilical pain localizing to the right lower quadrant.

---

## c-gallbladder

The gallbladder is a pear-shaped, hollow organ (7-10 cm long, 3-4 cm wide, capacity 30-50 mL) attached to the inferior (visceral) surface of the liver in the gallbladder fossa.

**Parts:**
- **Fundus:** rounded blind end, projects beyond inferior liver margin; contacts anterior abdominal wall at tip of 9th right costal cartilage (transpyloric plane)
- **Body:** largest portion, lies on visceral surface of liver
- **Infundibulum (Hartmann's pouch):** dilation between body and neck where gallstones frequently lodge
- **Nature:** narrow portion continuing as cystic duct; contains spiral folds (valves of Heister)

**Peritoneum:** completely surrounds fundus; binds body and neck to liver. Free surface has serosa; hepatic surface has adventitia.

**Relations:**
- Anteriorly: anterior abdominal wall, inferior surface of liver
- Posteriorly: transverse colon, first and second parts of duodenum

**Blood supply:** cystic artery (branch of right hepatic artery)
**Venous drainage:** cystic vein to portal vein
**Lymph:** cystic lymph node, hepatic nodes, celiac nodes
**Nerve:** vagus (parasympathetic) and celiac plexus (sympathetic)

**Function:** stores, concentrates (5-10x by water absorption), and expels bile in response to CCK (cholecystokinin) released after fatty meals.

## c-gallbladder:summary

The gallbladder (30-50 mL capacity) stores and concentrates bile 5-10x. It has fundus (at 9th costal cartilage), body, Hartmann's pouch, and neck. Cystic artery supplies it. CCK causes contraction after fatty meals.

---

## c-biliary-system

The biliary system transports bile from hepatocytes to the duodenum.

**Intrahepatic passages:**
1. Bile canaliculi (between hepatocytes)
2. Canals of Hering (transitional)
3. Bile ductules (cuboidal epithelium)
4. Interlobular ducts → larger intrahepatic ducts

**Extrahepatic passages:**
1. Right and left hepatic ducts (emerge from porta hepatis)
2. **Common hepatic duct** (~4 cm) - formed by union of right and left hepatic ducts
3. **Cystic duct** (~3.8 cm) - connects gallbladder neck to common hepatic duct; contains spiral valve
4. **Common bile duct (CBD)** (~8 cm) - formed by union of common hepatic duct and cystic duct
   - Course: free margin of lesser omentum → behind 1st part of duodenum → groove on posterior pancreatic head
   - Opens at **ampulla of Vater** (hepatopancreatic ampulla) on major duodenal papilla
   - Regulated by **sphincter of Oddi**

**Cystohepatic triangle (of Calot):** bounded by common hepatic duct (medially), cystic duct (laterally), cystic artery (superiorly). Contains right hepatic artery, cystic artery, lymph nodes.

## c-biliary-system:summary

Bile flows: canaliculi → canals of Hering → ductules → right/left hepatic ducts → common hepatic duct → (with cystic duct) → common bile duct → ampulla of Vater (sphincter of Oddi) → duodenum. The cystohepatic triangle (of Calot) contains key surgical structures.

---

## c-gallbladder-histology

The gallbladder wall has four specialized layers (NOT the typical GI tract pattern):

1. **Mucosa:**
   - Simple columnar epithelium with microvilli (cholangiocytes) for absorption
   - No goblet cells (unlike GI tract)
   - Highly vascular lamina propria (loose CT with capillaries, lymphocytes, plasma cells)
   - Deep mucosal folds when empty (flatten when distended)
   - No muscularis mucosae
   - No true glands except mucus-secreting glands in neck region

2. **Muscularis (Muscularis externa):**
   - Irregular bundles of smooth muscle (NOT organized circular/longitudinal layers)
   - Interspersed with collagen and elastic fibers
   - Contracts in response to CCK to expel bile

3. **Perimuscular connective tissue:**
   - Dense CT with blood vessels, lymphatics, nerves, adipose tissue

4. **Serosa or Adventitia:**
   - Free surface: serosa (mesothelium + CT)
   - Hepatic surface: adventitia (CT only, attaches to liver)

**Rokitansky-Aschoff sinuses:** epithelial outpouchings into muscular layer; normal variant, prominent in chronic cholecystitis.

**Key identification features:** simple columnar epithelium, no goblet cells, no muscularis mucosae, no submucosa, mucosa directly overlying muscularis, irregular smooth muscle bundles.

## c-gallbladder-histology:summary

The gallbladder lacks muscularis mucosae and submucosa. Layers: mucosa (simple columnar, no goblet cells, deep folds), muscularis (irregular smooth muscle), perimuscular CT, and serosa/adventitia. Rokitansky-Aschoff sinuses extend into muscular layer in chronic cholecystitis.

---

## c-pancreas

The pancreas is a soft, elongated retroperitoneal gland (15-20 cm long, 2-3 cm thick, 60-100 g) functioning as both an exocrine and endocrine gland (mixed gland).

**Location:** epigastrium and left hypochondrium; L1-L2 vertebral level (neck on transpyloric plane).

**C-shaped relationship with duodenum.**

**Not intraperitoneal:** retroperitoneal except the tail (within splenorenal ligament).

## c-pancreas:summary

The pancreas is a retroperitoneal mixed gland (exocrine + endocrine) located in the epigastrium and left hypochondrium, closely associated with the C-shaped duodenum.

---

## c-pancreas-anatomy

**Parts:**
1. **Head** - broadest part, moulded into C-shaped concavity of duodenum; lies over IVC and renal veins at L2 level; bile duct tunnels posteriorly through it
2. **Uncinate process** - hook-shaped extension from head, passes posterior to superior mesenteric vessels
3. **Neck** - constricted portion connecting head to body; lies anterior to beginning of portal vein
4. **Body** - triangular in cross-section, extends upward and left across midline
   - Anterosuperior surface: covered by peritoneum of lesser sac
   - Posterior surface: no peritoneum; in contact with aorta, splenic vein, left kidney, left suprarenal gland, SMA origin, left crus of diaphragm
   - Superior border: splenic artery runs along it; celiac artery nearby
5. **Tail** - narrow terminal portion within splenorenal ligament; reaches splenic hilum; highest concentration of Islets of Langerhans

**Ducts:**
- **Main pancreatic duct (of Wirsung):** begins in tail, runs entire length, opens at major duodenal papilla (with common bile duct)
- **Accessory pancreatic duct (of Santorini):** drains upper head, opens at minor duodenal papilla

**Blood supply:** splenic artery, superior and inferior pancreaticoduodenal arteries
**Venous drainage:** portal system
**Nerve supply:** vagus (parasympathetic) and celiac plexus (sympathetic)

## c-pancreas-anatomy:summary

The pancreas has head (in duodenal C-curve, bile duct passes through), uncinate process (hooks behind SMA), neck (over portal vein), body (three surfaces), and tail (in splenorenal ligament to splenic hilum). Main pancreatic duct opens at major duodenal papilla with CBD.

---

## c-pancreas-histology

The pancreas is a compound tubuloacinar mixed gland divided by connective tissue septa into lobules.

**Exocrine pancreas (98-99%):**
- **Pancreatic acini:** clusters of pyramidal acinar cells around small central lumen
  - Basal cytoplasm: basophilic (abundant RER for protein synthesis)
  - Apical cytoplasm: eosinophilic (zymogen granules with inactive enzymes)
- **Centroacinar cells:** pale-staining cells at acinar center, beginning of duct system; unique to pancreas; secrete bicarbonate and water
- **No myoepithelial cells** (unlike salivary glands)
- **Duct system:** centroacinar cells → intercalated ducts → intralobular ducts → interlobular ducts → main pancreatic duct
- **Secretions:** amylase (carbohydrates), lipase (fats), trypsinogen/chymotrypsinogen (protein digestion, activated by enterokinase)

**Endocrine pancreas (1-2%):**
- **Islets of Langerhans:** pale-staining rounded clusters scattered among darker exocrine tissue
- Richly vascularized with fenestrated capillaries
- Separated from acini by thin reticular tissue layer

**Cell types of Islets of Langerhans:**
| Cell Type | % | Hormone | Function |
|-----------|------|---------|----------|
| Beta (β) | 60-80% | Insulin | Decreases blood glucose |
| Alpha (α) | 15-20% | Glucagon | Increases blood glucose |
| Delta (δ) | 5-10% | Somatostatin | Inhibits insulin/glucagon secretion |
| PP (F) | 1-5% | Pancreatic polypeptide | Regulates pancreatic secretion |
| Epsilon (ε) | <1% | Ghrelin | Stimulates appetite |

**β cells** are centrally located; **α cells** are peripherally located in islets.

**Clinical:** Acute pancreatitis (premature enzyme activation), chronic pancreatitis (fibrosis, insufficiency), diabetes mellitus (β-cell dysfunction), cystic fibrosis (duct obstruction).

## c-pancreas-histology:summary

Exocrine pancreas (98-99%): serous acini with basophilic basal RER and eosinophilic zymogen granules; centroacinar cells are unique to pancreas. Endocrine pancreas (1-2%): Islets of Langerhans with β cells (insulin, 60-80%, central) and α cells (glucagon, 15-20%, peripheral).

---

## c-spleen

The spleen is the largest single mass of lymphoid tissue, located in the left hypochondrium deep to ribs 9-11, with its long axis along the 10th rib.

**Anatomical features:**
- Ovoid shape with notched anterior border
- Protected by ribs 9-11 and separated from them by diaphragm and costodiaphragmatic pleural recess
- Normal spleen cannot be palpated
- ~12 cm long, 7 cm wide, 3 cm thick, weighs 150-200 g

**Surfaces:**
- **Diaphragmatic surface:** convex, fits diaphragm concavity
- **Visceral surface:** related to stomach (gastric impression), left kidney (renal impression), left colic flexure (colic impression), and tail of pancreas (pancreatic impression)

**Borders:**
- Anterior and superior: sharp (anterior border is notched)
- Posterior and inferior: rounded

**Peritoneal ligaments:**
- **Gastrosplenic ligament:** to greater curvature of stomach (carries short gastric and left gastroepiploic vessels)
- **Lienorenal (splenorenal) ligament:** to left kidney (carries splenic vessels and tail of pancreas)

**Blood supply:** splenic artery (largest branch of celiac trunk, tortuous course)
**Venous drainage:** splenic vein (joins SMV behind pancreas neck to form portal vein)
**Lymph:** hilar nodes → celiac lymph nodes
**Nerve:** celiac plexus

**Clinical:** Most frequently injured abdominal organ in blunt trauma. Splenectomy may be required for rupture. Accessory spleens occur in ~10% of people. Splenomegaly in leukemia, infections, portal hypertension.

## c-spleen:summary

The spleen is in the left hypochondrium behind ribs 9-11. It has a notched anterior border and receives blood from the splenic artery (celiac trunk branch). The splenic vein forms the portal vein behind the pancreas neck. Protected by ribs but most commonly injured abdominal organ in trauma.

---

## c-spleen-histology

**Structure:**
- **Capsule:** dense connective tissue; sends trabeculae inward carrying blood vessels
- **Stroma:** reticular connective tissue framework
- **Parenchyma:** divided into white pulp and red pulp (NOT cortex/medulla)

**White pulp (immune component):**
- **Central artery:** branches of trabecular arteries running through white pulp
- **Periarterial lymphatic sheath (PALS):** thymus-dependent zone surrounding central artery; contains mainly **T lymphocytes** (inner layer)
- **Lymphoid follicles (nodules):** contain mainly **B lymphocytes**; may have germinal centers (secondary nodules)
- **Marginal zone:** interface between white and red pulp; contains antigen-presenting cells and macrophages

**Red pulp (blood filtering component):**
- **Splenic cords (of Billroth):** cellular aggregations supported by reticular fibers; contain macrophages, plasma cells, and blood cells
- **Splenic sinusoids (venous sinuses):** wide irregular lumen lined by longitudinally oriented endothelial cells with discontinuous basement membrane
- Functions: removes antigens, microorganisms, aged/abnormal erythrocytes and platelets

**Blood flow:** trabecular arteries → central arteries → penicilli (arterioles) → capillaries → red pulp (cords → sinusoids) → splenic veins → trabecular veins → splenic vein

**Functions:**
1. Production of antibodies (main source of circulating antibodies)
2. Destruction of aged RBCs and platelets (red pulp)
3. Blood and platelet reservoir
4. Immune surveillance (white pulp)

## c-spleen-histology:summary

White pulp = immune function (PALS with T cells around central artery, lymphoid follicles with B cells). Red pulp = blood filtering (splenic cords of Billroth with macrophages, sinusoids). The spleen has no afferent lymphatic vessels and no cortex/medulla organization like lymph nodes.

---

## c-kidney-ureter

The kidneys and ureters form part of the urinary system, responsible for blood filtration, urine formation, and transport to the urinary bladder.

## c-kidney-ureter:summary

The kidneys filter blood and form urine, which is transported to the bladder by the ureters.

---

## c-kidney-anatomy

**Kidney:**
- Paired, bean-shaped organs (~11 cm long, 6 cm wide, 3 cm thick, ~150 g each)
- Located on posterior abdominal wall between T12-L3 vertebrae
- Right kidney slightly lower than left (due to liver)
- Each kidney is retroperitoneal

**External features:**
- **Hilum:** medial indentation where renal artery enters, renal vein and ureter exit
- **Borders:** lateral (convex), medial (concave with hilum)
- **Surfaces:** anterior (relations vary between sides), posterior (against posterior abdominal wall muscles)

**Relations:**
- **Right kidney:** liver (anterior), 12th rib, diaphragm, psoas major, quadratus lumborum (posterior)
- **Left kidney:** stomach, spleen, pancreas, splenic flexure of colon (anterior); 11th-12th ribs, diaphragm, psoas major, quadratus lumborum (posterior)

**Coverings (inside to outside):**
1. Renal capsule (fibrous)
2. Perinephric fat (adipose)
3. Renal fascia (Gerota's fascia)
4. Pararenal fat

**Support:** renal fascia anchors kidney to surrounding structures; perinephric fat cushions it.

## c-kidney-anatomy:summary

The kidneys are retroperitoneal, bean-shaped organs at T12-L3. The right is lower than the left. The hilum contains (anterior to posterior): renal vein, renal artery, renal pelvis/ureter. Coverings: renal capsule, perinephric fat, renal fascia, pararenal fat.

---

## c-kidney-internal

**Internal structure:**

1. **Renal cortex** - outer region, reddish-brown
   - Contains renal corpuscles (glomeruli + Bowman's capsules)
   - Proximal and distal convoluted tubules
   - Cortical radiations (medullary rays)
   - Extends between medullary pyramids as **renal columns (of Bertin)**

2. **Renal medulla** - inner region, composed of 8-18 **renal pyramids** (medullary pyramids)
   - Contains loops of Henle and collecting ducts
   - Base of pyramid faces cortex
   - Apex (renal papilla) projects into minor calyx
   - Striated appearance due to parallel arrangement of tubules

3. **Renal pelvis** - funnel-shaped expansion of ureter within hilum
   - Divided into major calyces (2-3) → minor calyces (8-18)
   - Minor calyces cup the renal papillae
   - Urine drains from papillae into minor calyces → major calyces → renal pelvis → ureter

**Nephron** (~1 million per kidney) - functional unit:
- **Renal corpuscle:** glomerulus (capillary tuft from afferent arteriole) enclosed in Bowman's capsule
- **Proximal convoluted tubule:** in cortex; bulk reabsorption (glucose, amino acids, Na+, water)
- **Loop of Henle:** descends into medulla and returns to cortex; establishes osmotic gradient
- **Distal convoluted tubule:** in cortex; fine-tuning of reabsorption/secretion
- **Collecting duct:** runs through medulla to renal papilla; determines final urine concentration

**Blood supply pathway:**
Renal artery → segmental arteries → interlobar arteries → arcuate arteries → interlobular (cortical radiate) arteries → afferent arterioles → glomerular capillaries → efferent arterioles → peritubular capillaries (cortex) / vasa recta (medulla) → interlobular veins → arcuate veins → interlobar veins → renal vein

## c-kidney-internal:summary

The kidney has an outer cortex (glomeruli, convoluted tubules) and inner medulla (8-18 pyramids with loops of Henle and collecting ducts). Renal papillae drain into minor calyces → major calyces → renal pelvis → ureter. Each nephron has a renal corpuscle, proximal tubule, loop of Henle, distal tubule, and collecting duct.

---

## c-ureter

The ureter is a muscular tube (~25-30 cm long, 3-4 mm diameter) transporting urine from the renal pelvis to the urinary bladder.

**Course:**
1. Begins at renal pelvis at the hilum
2. Descends retroperitoneally on psoas major muscle
3. Crosses over bifurcation of common iliac artery (at pelvic brim)
4. Runs along lateral pelvic wall
5. Enters the bladder obliquely through the posterolateral wall

**Three constrictions:**
1. Pelviureteric junction (PUJ) - where renal pelvis joins ureter
2. Crossing of common iliac artery (at pelvic brim)
3. Vesicoureteric junction (VUJ) - where ureter enters bladder

**Blood supply:** variable along course (renal, gonadal, common iliac, internal iliac, vesical arteries)
**Nerve supply:** sympathetic (T10-L1) and parasympathetic (pelvic splanchnic nerves)

**Histology (three layers):**
1. **Mucosa:** transitional epithelium (urothelium) - allows stretching; lamina propria
2. **Muscularis:** inner longitudinal + outer circular smooth muscle layers (different from GI tract pattern)
3. **Adventitia:** fibroelastic connective tissue (no serosa except at pelvic portion)

**Clinical:** Ureteric stones cause severe renal colic pain. The oblique intramural passage of the ureter through the bladder wall acts as a valve preventing vesicoureteral reflux. Kidney stones may lodge at the three constrictions.

## c-ureter:summary

The ureter (25-30 cm) has transitional epithelium, inner longitudinal and outer circular muscle. It has three constrictions (PUJ, pelvic brim, VUJ) where stones may lodge. The oblique intramural course through the bladder wall prevents vesicoureteral reflux.
