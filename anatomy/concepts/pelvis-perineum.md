## c-anp-001

The pelvis (Latin: basin) is the part of the trunk inferoposterior to the abdomen, forming the transition zone between the trunk and lower limbs. Anatomically, it is surrounded by the pelvic girdle (bony pelvis) and is part of the appendicular skeleton. Topographically, it comprises a bony and ligamentous framework lined internally and externally by soft tissue, closed inferiorly by the pelvic floor. The perineum lies below the pelvic floor.

### Functions of the Pelvis

1. Protects pelvic viscera
2. Supports body weight when sitting and standing — transfers upper body weight from the axial to the appendicular skeleton
3. Provides attachment for muscles
4. In females, provides bony support for the birth canal

## c-anp-001:summary

- Pelvis = transition between trunk and lower limbs; bounded by pelvic girdle, closed inferiorly by pelvic floor
- Functions: protect viscera, support weight, muscle attachment, birth canal (female)

---

## c-anp-002

The bony pelvis is composed of 4 bones: 2 hip (pelvic) bones forming anterior and lateral walls, and the sacrum and coccyx forming the posterior wall. These are connected by 4 joints and lined by 4 muscles, forming a strong basin-shaped structure.

## c-anp-002:summary

- 4 bones: 2 hip bones + sacrum + coccyx
- Connected by 4 joints, lined by 4 muscles
- Basin-shaped; protects lower alimentary, urinary, and reproductive organs

---

## c-anp-003

The hip bone (pelvic bone) is composed of three fused bones: ilium, ischium, and pubis, which unite at the acetabulum. The two hip bones are joined at the pubic symphysis anteriorly and articulate with the sacrum at the sacroiliac joints posteriorly, and with the femur at the hip joint laterally.

## c-anp-003:summary

- Composed of ilium, ischium, pubis — fused at acetabulum
- Articulates with sacrum (sacroiliac joint), femur (hip joint), and opposite hip bone (pubic symphysis)

---

## c-anp-004

The pubis is an angulated bone forming the anterior part of the pelvic bone. Its body forms 1/5th of the acetabulum. Key features: pubic crest, pubic tubercle, pecten pubis (pectineal line), superior and inferior pubic rami. The inferior ramus fuses with the ischial ramus to form the ischiopubic ramus. The subpubic angle is formed by the two inferior pubic rami.

## c-anp-004:summary

- Forms anterior pelvic bone; body = 1/5th of acetabulum
- Features: pubic crest, tubercle, pectineal line, superior/inferior rami
- Inferior ramus + ischial ramus = ischiopubic ramus

---

## c-anp-005

The ischium is V-shaped, forming the posteroinferior part of the pelvic bone. Key features: body, superior and inferior ischial rami, ischial spine (separates greater from lesser sciatic notch), and ischial tuberosity (supports body weight when sitting; attachment of sacrotuberous ligament and hamstring muscles).

## c-anp-005:summary

- V-shaped; posteroinferior pelvic bone
- Ischial spine separates greater from lesser sciatic notch
- Ischial tuberosity = weight-bearing when sitting; hamstring origin

---

## c-anp-006

The ilium is the most superior component of the hip bone. It presents the iliac crest (between ASIS and PSIS), iliac fossa (inner concave surface), auricular surface (articulates with sacrum), and the spines: ASIS, AIIS, PSIS, PIIS.

## c-anp-006:summary

- Most superior hip bone component
- Iliac crest, iliac fossa, auricular surface (sacroiliac articulation)
- Spines: ASIS, AIIS, PSIS, PIIS

---

## c-anp-007

The sacrum is a wedge-shaped bone composed of 5 fused sacral vertebrae. It is located between the hip bones, forming the roof and posterosuperior wall of the posterior pelvic cavity. The base is formed by S1's superior surface; its anterior projecting edge is the sacral promontory. The apex is an inferior oval facet articulating with the coccyx. Features: anterior and posterior sacral foramina, median/intermediate/lateral sacral crests, sacral hiatus (absent laminae of S5 — site for caudal anaesthesia), and sacral canal (contains cauda equina). The lumbosacral angle is 130°–160°.

## c-anp-007:summary

- 5 fused sacral vertebrae; wedge-shaped
- Base (S1) with sacral promontory; apex articulates with coccyx
- Sacral hiatus = absent S5 laminae; site for caudal anaesthesia
- Sacral canal contains cauda equina

---

## c-anp-008

The coccyx is a vestigial tail bone consisting of 4 fused vertebrae forming a small triangular bone. Its base articulates with the lower end of the sacrum at the sacrococcygeal joint.

## c-anp-008:summary

- 4 fused vertebrae; vestigial tail bone
- Base articulates with sacrum

---

## c-anp-009

There are 4 pelvic joints: 2 sacroiliac joints, 1 sacrococcygeal joint, and 1 pubic symphysis. In the non-pregnant state there is very little movement, but during pregnancy hormones soften ligaments, allowing more movement to facilitate passage of the fetal head.

## c-anp-009:summary

- 4 joints: 2 sacroiliac, 1 sacrococcygeal, 1 pubic symphysis
- Little movement normally; increased during pregnancy

---

## c-anp-010

The pubic symphysis is a secondary cartilaginous (symphysis) joint. Articular surfaces are covered with hyaline cartilage, with a fibrocartilage disc between them. A cavity may develop in the disc but is not lined with synovial membrane. There is normally very little movement.

## c-anp-010:summary

- Secondary cartilaginous joint
- Hyaline cartilage + fibrocartilage disc; minimal movement

---

## c-anp-011

The sacroiliac joint is a modified synovial plane joint with rough articular surfaces. It is bound by anterior and posterior sacroiliac ligaments (among the strongest in the body). Accessory ligaments: sacrotuberous, sacrospinous, and iliolumbar ligaments. The sacrotuberous and sacrospinous ligaments convert the greater and lesser sciatic notches into foramina. The joint is almost immobile except during pregnancy.

## c-anp-011:summary

- Modified synovial plane joint; rough surfaces
- Strong anterior and posterior sacroiliac ligaments
- Accessory: sacrotuberous, sacrospinous, iliolumbar
- Almost immobile except during pregnancy

---

## c-anp-012

The sacrococcygeal joint is formed where the base of the coccyx articulates with the tip of the sacrum. During delivery, the coccyx moves backwards (nodding) at this joint to provide more space. Fracture or arthritis here causes coccydynia.

## c-anp-012:summary

- Base of coccyx articulates with sacral tip
- Coccyx nods backward during delivery for more space
- Coccydynia = painful condition from fracture/arthritis

---

## c-anp-013

The pelvis is divided by an imaginary plane (linea terminalis / pelvic brim) into the greater (false) pelvis above and the lesser (true) pelvis below.

## c-anp-013:summary

- Linea terminalis / pelvic brim divides pelvis into greater (false) and lesser (true)

---

## c-anp-014

The greater (false/major) pelvis lies above the linea terminalis. Posteriorly: L5 vertebra. Laterally: iliac fossae. Anteriorly: lower anterior abdominal wall (where ilium is deficient). It is considered part of the abdominal cavity and supports ileum, caecum, appendix, and sigmoid colon.

## c-anp-014:summary

- Above linea terminalis; part of abdominal cavity
- Supports ileum, caecum, appendix, sigmoid colon

---

## c-anp-015

The lesser (true/minor) pelvis lies inferior and behind the linea terminalis. It contains the rectum, bladder, and internal genitalia. In females, the uterus and vagina occupy the interval between rectum and bladder. It has a superior aperture (pelvic inlet), pelvic cavity, and inferior aperture (pelvic outlet). The true pelvis is modified in females to serve as the birth canal.

## c-anp-015:summary

- Below linea terminalis; contains rectum, bladder, genitalia
- Has inlet, cavity, and outlet
- Modified in females as birth canal

---

## c-anp-016

The pelvic inlet (superior aperture) is formed posteriorly by the anterior surface of S1 body, laterally by the arcuate and pectineal lines, and anteriorly by the pubic crest and pectineal line.

## c-anp-016:summary

- Posterior: S1 body; lateral: arcuate + pectineal lines
- Anterior: pubic crest + pectineal line

---

## c-anp-017

The pelvic outlet (inferior aperture) is diamond-shaped. Bounded posteriorly by the tip of the coccyx, posterolaterally by sacrotuberous ligaments, laterally by ischial tuberosities, and anteriorly by the pubic arch. The terminal parts of the urinary and gastrointestinal tracts and the vagina pass through it.

## c-anp-017:summary

- Diamond-shaped
- Posterior: coccyx tip; lateral: ischial tuberosities
- Anterior: pubic arch; posterolateral: sacrotuberous ligaments

---

## c-anp-018

The pelvic cavity extends from the pelvic brim above to the outlet below. It is almost rounded in shape, deeper posteriorly than anteriorly. Anterior border: symphysis pubis. Posterior border: sacral hollow. It has two openings: the superior (inlet) and inferior (outlet) apertures. The abdominal cavity continues inferiorly into the pelvic cavity, together forming the abdominopelvic cavity.

## c-anp-018:summary

- From brim to outlet; almost circular; deeper posteriorly
- Anterior: symphysis pubis; posterior: sacral hollow
- Continuous with abdominal cavity → abdominopelvic cavity

---

## c-anp-019

The general shape of the pelvis is classified into four types based on the Caldwell-Moloy classification: Gynaecoid (50%), Android (20%), Anthropoid (25%), and Platypelloid (5%). Shape is determined by hormonal, environmental, hereditary, and mechanical factors.

## c-anp-019:summary

- 4 types: Gynaecoid (50%), Anthropoid (25%), Android (20%), Platypelloid (5%)
- Determined by hormonal, environmental, hereditary, mechanical factors

---

## c-anp-020

The gynaecoid pelvis (50%) is the classic female pelvis, most suitable for childbirth. Features: wider pelvic brim, blunt ischial spines, subpubic angle up to 90°, subpubic arch wide enough to accommodate four knuckles.

## c-anp-020:summary

- Classic female pelvis; most common (50%)
- Wide brim, blunt ischial spines, subpubic angle up to 90°

---

## c-anp-021

The android pelvis (20%) is the classic male pelvis. Features: heart-shaped brim, shorter AP diameter, wider transverse diameter, making childbirth difficult.

## c-anp-021:summary

- Classic male pelvis (20%)
- Heart-shaped brim; short AP diameter; difficult childbirth

---

## c-anp-022

The anthropoid pelvis (25%) is oval in shape with a shorter transverse diameter. It favors a posterior position of the fetus. Seen in tall women with narrow shoulders.

## c-anp-022:summary

- Oval-shaped; shorter transverse diameter (25%)
- Favors posterior fetal position

---

## c-anp-023

The platypelloid (flat) pelvis (5%) is rare with a kidney-shaped brim. The AP diameter is smaller while the transverse diameter is wider. Not conducive for vaginal delivery.

## c-anp-023:summary

- Rare (5%); kidney-shaped brim
- Small AP diameter; not conducive for vaginal delivery

---

## c-anp-024

Pelvic measurements (pelvimetry) are important in obstetrics for predicting the likelihood of vaginal delivery. Accurate measurements of the pelvic inlet and outlet determine if the fetal head can pass without complications.

## c-anp-024:summary

- Pelvimetry: predicts vaginal delivery feasibility
- Measures inlet and outlet diameters

---

## c-anp-025

Conjugate diameters (AP): True conjugate (sacral promontory to superior pubic symphysis) ≈ 11–11.5 cm in females. Diagonal conjugate (sacral promontory to inferior pubic symphysis) ≈ 12 cm. Obstetric conjugate (sacral promontory to point below superior symphysis) ≈ 10.5 cm — the shortest AP diameter; estimated by subtracting 1.5–2 cm from diagonal conjugate. If < 10 cm → contracted pelvic inlet.

## c-anp-025:summary

- True conjugate ≈ 11–11.5 cm; Diagonal ≈ 12 cm
- Obstetric conjugate ≈ 10.5 cm (shortest AP diameter)
- < 10 cm = contracted pelvic inlet

---

## c-anp-026

Transverse diameters: Anatomical transverse diameter is the widest distance across the pelvic brim ≈ 13–13.5 cm in females. Obstetric transverse diameter bisects the true conjugate and is slightly shorter.

## c-anp-026:summary

- Anatomical ≈ 13–13.5 cm (widest diameter of inlet)
- Obstetric transverse diameter is slightly shorter

---

## c-anp-027

Oblique diameters: Right oblique from right sacroiliac joint to left iliopectineal eminence; left oblique from left sacroiliac joint to right iliopectineal eminence. Each ≈ 12 cm. The sacrocotyloid diameter (midpoint of sacral promontory to ipsilateral iliopubic eminence) ≈ 9.5 cm and represents the space for the biparietal diameter in flat pelvis.

## c-anp-027:summary

- Oblique diameters ≈ 12 cm each
- Sacrocotyloid ≈ 9.5 cm; space for biparietal diameter

---

## c-anp-028

The pelvic floor is a layer of muscle and fascia that closes the pelvic cavity inferiorly. The perineum lies below it. The pelvic diaphragm is the main muscular component, primarily formed by the levator ani and coccygeus muscles.

## c-anp-028:summary

- Closes pelvic cavity inferiorly
- Main muscular component = levator ani + coccygeus = pelvic diaphragm
- Perineum lies below

---

## c-anp-029

The levator ani is the largest and most important muscle of the pelvic floor. It is a broad, thin muscle that forms the main part of the pelvic diaphragm. It has three parts: pubococcygeus, iliococcygeus, and puborectalis.

## c-anp-029:summary

- Largest pelvic floor muscle; main part of pelvic diaphragm
- Three parts: pubococcygeus, iliococcygeus, puborectalis

---

## c-anp-030

The pubococcygeus is the main part of the levator ani. It arises from the pubis and inserts into the coccyx and anococcygeal raphe. It supports pelvic viscera, resists increased intra-abdominal pressure, and assists in controlling defecation and urination.

## c-anp-030:summary

- Main levator ani component
- Origin: pubis; Insertion: coccyx/anococcygeal raphe
- Supports viscera; controls defecation/urination

---

## c-anp-031

The iliococcygeus is the most posterior part of the levator ani. It arises from the ischial spine and inserts into the coccyx and anococcygeal raphe. It forms the posterior part of the pelvic diaphragm.

## c-anp-031:summary

- Most posterior levator ani part
- Origin: ischial spine; Insertion: coccyx/anococcygeal raphe

---

## c-anp-032

The puborectalis forms a U-shaped sling around the anorectal junction. It arises from the pubis and loops around the anorectal junction to insert into itself. It maintains the anorectal angle, which is important for fecal continence.

## c-anp-032:summary

- U-shaped sling around anorectal junction
- Maintains anorectal angle → fecal continence

---

## c-anp-033

The coccygeus (ischiococcygeus) is a small triangular muscle posterior to the levator ani. It arises from the ischial spine and inserts into the lateral border of the sacrum and coccyx. It helps support the pelvic viscera and pulls the coccyx forward.

## c-anp-033:summary

- Small triangular muscle posterior to levator ani
- Origin: ischial spine; Insertion: sacrum/coccyx
- Supports viscera; pulls coccyx forward

---

## c-anp-034

Muscles of the pelvic wall include the obturator internus and piriformis. These line the lateral walls of the pelvis.

## c-anp-034:summary

- Obturator internus and piriformis line lateral pelvic walls

---

## c-anp-035

The obturator internus arises from the inner surface of the ilium, ischium, and the deep surface of the obturator membrane. It inserts into the greater trochanter of the femur. Nerve supply: nerve to obturator internus (L5, S1, S2). Action: lateral rotation and abduction of the flexed hip.

## c-anp-035:summary

- Origin: inner ilium, ischium, obturator membrane
- Insertion: greater trochanter
- Action: lateral rotation and abduction of flexed hip

---

## c-anp-036

The piriformis is a triangular muscle and an important landmark in the gluteal region. Origin: anterior surface of S2–S4, superior margin of greater sciatic notch, sacrotuberous ligament. Insertion: greater trochanter (above obturator internus). Nerve supply: L5, S1–S3. Action: lateral rotation and abduction of femur.

## c-anp-036:summary

- Triangular; important gluteal landmark
- Origin: S2–S4, greater sciatic notch, sacrotuberous ligament
- Insertion: greater trochanter; Action: lateral rotation, abduction

---

## c-anp-037

The perineum is the region below the pelvic floor, bounded by the pubic symphysis anteriorly, coccyx posteriorly, and ischial tuberosities laterally. A horizontal line connecting the ischial tuberosities divides it into the urogenital triangle (anterior) and anal triangle (posterior).

## c-anp-037:summary

- Below pelvic floor; bounded by pubic symphysis, coccyx, ischial tuberosities
- Divided into urogenital triangle (anterior) and anal triangle (posterior)

---

## c-anp-038

The urogenital triangle is the anterior part of the perineum. It contains the external genitalia in both sexes and is related to the urogenital diaphragm and its associated muscles.

## c-anp-038:summary

- Anterior perineum; contains external genitalia
- Related to urogenital diaphragm and associated muscles

---

## c-anp-039

The anal triangle is the posterior part of the perineum. It contains the anal canal, external anal sphincter, and the ischioanal fossae on either side.

## c-anp-039:summary

- Posterior perineum; contains anal canal
- Contains external anal sphincter and ischioanal fossae

---

## c-anp-040

The superficial perineal pouch (superficial perineal space) lies between the perineal membrane inferiorly and Colles' fascia superiorly. In males, it contains the root of the penis (crura and bulb), ischiocavernosus, bulbospongiosus, and superficial transverse perineal muscles. In females, it contains the clitoris, bulbs of the vestibule, and the same muscles (modified).

## c-anp-040:summary

- Between perineal membrane and Colles' fascia
- Male: root of penis, ischiocavernosus, bulbospongiosus, superficial transverse perinei
- Female: clitoris, vestibular bulbs, same muscles

---

## c-anp-041

The deep perineal pouch (deep perineal space) lies between the perineal membrane superiorly and the inferior fascia of the urogenital diaphragm. It contains the external urethral sphincter and, in males, the bulbourethral glands.

## c-anp-041:summary

- Between perineal membrane and inferior fascia of urogenital diaphragm
- Contains external urethral sphincter; in males, bulbourethral glands

---

## c-anp-042

The perineal membrane is a fibrous sheet that spans the urogenital triangle. It provides attachment for the external genitalia and contributes to the support of the pelvic floor. In females, it separates the superficial and deep perineal pouches.

## c-anp-042:summary

- Fibrous sheet spanning urogenital triangle
- Attachment for external genitalia; separates superficial/deep pouches

---

## c-anp-043

The perineal body is a fibromuscular structure at the center of the perineum, where the superficial transverse perineal muscles, bulbospongiosus, and external anal sphincter meet. It is a key landmark for pelvic floor integrity. Its disruption during childbirth can lead to pelvic organ prolapse.

## c-anp-043:summary

- Central fibromuscular structure of perineum
- Convergence of superficial transverse perinei, bulbospongiosus, external anal sphincter
- Key for pelvic floor integrity; disruption → prolapse

---

## c-anp-044

The ischioanal fossa is a fat-filled space on each side of the anal canal and lower rectum, between the pelvic diaphragm and the obturator internus. It allows the anal canal to distend during defecation.

## c-anp-044:summary

- Fat-filled space lateral to anal canal
- Between pelvic diaphragm and obturator internus
- Allows anal canal distension during defecation

---

## c-anp-045

The superficial perineal muscles are located in the superficial perineal pouch. They include the ischiocavernosus, bulbospongiosus, and superficial transverse perineal muscles.

## c-anp-045:summary

- Located in superficial perineal pouch
- Ischiocavernosus, bulbospongiosus, superficial transverse perinei

---

## c-anp-046

The ischiocavernosus surrounds each crus (root) of the penis or clitoris. It contracts to force blood from the cavernous spaces in the crura into the corpora cavernosa, helping maintain erection.

## c-anp-046:summary

- Surrounds crura of penis/clitoris
- Forces blood into corpora cavernosa → maintains erection

---

## c-anp-047

The bulbospongiosus surrounds the bulb of the penis or the vestibular bulbs in females. In males, it contracts to empty the spongy urethra of residual semen and urine, and aids in maintaining erection. In females, it constricts the vaginal orifice.

## c-anp-047:summary

- Surrounds bulb of penis/vestibular bulbs
- Male: empties spongy urethra; aids erection
- Female: constricts vaginal orifice

---

## c-anp-048

The superficial transverse perineal muscle runs from the ischial tuberosity to the perineal body. It helps stabilize the perineal body during contraction of other perineal muscles.

## c-anp-048:summary

- From ischial tuberosity to perineal body
- Stabilizes perineal body

---

## c-anp-049

The external anal sphincter surrounds the anal canal and is under voluntary control. It has three parts: subcutaneous, superficial (attached to coccyx and perineal body), and deep (continuous with puborectalis). It maintains fecal continence.

## c-anp-049:summary

- Voluntary sphincter around anal canal
- Three parts: subcutaneous, superficial, deep
- Maintains fecal continence

---

## c-anp-050

The male reproductive system consists of the testes, epididymis, vas deferens, seminal vesicles, prostate gland, penis, and scrotum. It produces sperm and male sex hormones, and provides the organs for copulation.

## c-anp-050:summary

- Testes, epididymis, vas deferens, seminal vesicles, prostate, penis, scrotum
- Produces sperm and hormones; provides copulatory organs

---

## c-anp-051

The testes are paired ovoid organs lying in the scrotum. Each measures about 4 cm long. They produce spermatozoa (spermatogenesis) and testosterone (Leydig cells). The tunica albuginea is a thick fibrous capsule covering each testis, sending septa inward to divide it into lobules containing seminiferous tubules.

## c-anp-051:summary

- Paired ovoid organs in scrotum (~4 cm long)
- Produce spermatozoa and testosterone
- Tunica albuginea → septa → lobules → seminiferous tubules

---

## c-anp-052

The seminiferous tubules are coiled tubules within the testicular lobules where spermatogenesis occurs. They are lined with germ cells at various stages of development and Sertoli cells (sustentacular cells) that support and nourish developing spermatozoa.

## c-anp-052:summary

- Coiled tubules in lobules; site of spermatogenesis
- Lined with germ cells and Sertoli cells

---

## c-anp-053

Leydig cells (interstitial cells) are found in the connective tissue between seminiferous tubules. They produce testosterone under the influence of luteinizing hormone (LH), which is essential for spermatogenesis and male secondary sexual characteristics.

## c-anp-053:summary

- Interstitial cells between seminiferous tubules
- Produce testosterone under LH stimulation
- Essential for spermatogenesis and male secondary sex characteristics

---

## c-anp-054

Sertoli cells (sustentacular/nurse cells) line the seminiferous tubules. They support, nourish, and protect developing spermatozoa, form the blood-testis barrier, secrete inhibin (feedback to FSH), and phagocytose excess cytoplasm during spermiogenesis.

## c-anp-054:summary

- Line seminiferous tubules; support/nourish spermatozoa
- Form blood-testis barrier; secrete inhibin
- Phagocytose excess cytoplasm

---

## c-anp-055

Spermatogenesis is the process of sperm production occurring in the seminiferous tubules. It begins at puberty and continues throughout life. Spermatogonia (2n) → primary spermatocytes (2n) → meiosis I → secondary spermatocytes (n) → meiosis II → spermatids (n) → spermiogenesis → spermatozoa (n). The entire process takes about 64–72 days.

## c-anp-055:summary

- Spermatogonia → spermatocytes → spermatids → spermatozoa
- Occurs in seminiferous tubules; begins at puberty
- Takes ~64–72 days

---

## c-anp-056

The epididymis is a coiled duct situated at the posterior-superior aspect of each testis. It has three parts: head (caput), body (corpus), and tail (cauda). Functions: storage and maturation of spermatozoa. The tail continues as the vas deferens.

## c-anp-056:summary

- Coiled duct at posterior-superior testis
- Three parts: head, body, tail
- Stores and matures sperm; tail → vas deferens

---

## c-anp-057

The vas deferens (ductus deferens) is a muscular duct about 45 cm long. It ascends from the epididymis tail within the spermatic cord, passes through the inguinal canal, enters the pelvic cavity, and passes over the ureter to the posterior bladder where it joins the duct of the seminal vesicle to form the ejaculatory duct. It conveys spermatozoa during ejaculation.

## c-anp-057:summary

- Muscular duct (~45 cm); from epididymis to ejaculatory duct
- Passes through inguinal canal into pelvic cavity
- Conveys spermatozoa during ejaculation

---

## c-anp-058

The seminal vesicles are paired coiled tubular glands located between the base of the bladder and the rectum. Each is about 3–5 cm long (10–15 cm if uncoiled). They secrete alkaline fluid containing fructose, prostaglandins, and other substances that make up roughly 60–80% of semen. Each excretory duct joins the corresponding vas deferens to form the ejaculatory duct, which passes through the prostate to open into the prostatic urethra.

## c-anp-058:summary

- Paired coiled glands between bladder base and rectum
- Secrete alkaline, fructose-rich fluid (60–80% of semen)
- Duct + vas deferens → ejaculatory duct → prostatic urethra

---

## c-anp-059

The prostate gland is the largest accessory gland in the male reproductive system. It is a fibromuscular, walnut-sized gland located below the bladder and anterior to the rectum. It wraps around the prostatic urethra and contributes nutrient-rich fluid to semen. It is described by lobes (gross anatomy) and zones (histology).

## c-anp-059:summary

- Largest male accessory gland; walnut-sized
- Below bladder, anterior to rectum
- Wraps prostatic urethra; contributes fluid to semen

---

## c-anp-060

The prostate has 5 lobes: anterior (fibromuscular, little glandular tissue), posterior (most common site for carcinoma), median (common site for BPH, produces uvula vesicae), and right and left lateral lobes (contain many glands).

## c-anp-060:summary

- 5 lobes: anterior, posterior, median, right lateral, left lateral
- Posterior = most common carcinoma site
- Median = common BPH site; produces uvula vesicae

---

## c-anp-061

Histological zones: Peripheral zone (PZ, ~65%) — posterior/lateral; most common site for prostate cancer (70–80%); palpable on DRE. Central zone (CZ, ~25%) — surrounds ejaculatory ducts; relatively immune to hyperplasia. Transitional zone (TZ, 5–10%) — surrounds prostatic urethra; site of BPH. Anterior fibromuscular stroma — non-glandular, smooth muscle and fibrous tissue.

## c-anp-061:summary

- PZ (65%): posterior/lateral; cancer site (70–80%)
- CZ (25%): surrounds ejaculatory ducts
- TZ (5–10%): surrounds urethra; BPH site
- Anterior fibromuscular stroma: non-glandular

---

## c-anp-062

The penis (phallus) is the male organ of copulation, traversed by the urethra. It is composed of erectile tissue capable of engorgement with blood. Parts: root (radix) — in superficial perineal pouch, comprising two crura and the bulb; body (corpus) — free pendulous portion; glans penis — expanded distal end of corpus spongiosum.

## c-anp-062:summary

- Male copulatory organ; traversed by urethra
- Root (crura + bulb), body (free portion), glans
- Composed of erectile tissue

---

## c-anp-063

Erectile tissue: Two corpora cavernosa form the greater part of the body, surrounded by tunica albuginea. Corpus spongiosum is the forward continuation of the bulb, tapering distally to form the glans penis. The corpora cavernosa are the forward continuation of the crura.

## c-anp-063:summary

- Two corpora cavernosa (main bulk) + one corpus spongiosum
- All surrounded by tunica albuginea
- Corpus spongiosum → glans penis

---

## c-anp-064

Penile coverings from superficial to deep: skin (thin, hairless, loosely attached, with prepuce), superficial fascia (superficial fatty layer and deep membranous layer = Buck's fascia), and tunica albuginea (surrounds corpora cavernosa only). The frenulum is a sensitive fold on the ventral glans. Supports: fundiform ligament (from linea alba) and suspensory ligament (from pubic symphysis to Buck's fascia).

## c-anp-064:summary

- Skin → superficial fascia → Buck's fascia → tunica albuginea
- Prepuce covers glans; frenulum on ventral glans
- Fundiform and suspensory ligaments support the body

---

## c-anp-065

Erection is a purely vascular phenomenon mediated by parasympathetic stimulation (nervi erigentes, S2–S4). Sexual arousal → rapid inflow from helicine arteries → engorgement of cavernous spaces → compression of draining veins (with venous smooth muscle sphincter spasm) → venous outflow occluded → inflation and rigidity. Pressure in corpora cavernosa reaches 100 mmHg. Tunica albuginea contributes to stiffness.

## c-anp-065:summary

- Parasympathetic (S2–S4); vascular phenomenon
- Helicine arteries → cavernous engorgement → vein compression → rigidity
- Corpora cavernosa pressure ≈ 100 mmHg

---

## c-anp-066

Ejaculation has two processes: emission and ejaculation. Emission (sympathetic, T11–L2): seminal fluid from vas deferens, seminal vesicles, and prostate enters prostatic urethra; internal urethral sphincter constricts to prevent retrograde flow. Ejaculation (somatic, pudendal nerve): rhythmic contraction of bulbospongiosus compresses penile urethra, expelling fluid to the exterior.

## c-anp-066:summary

- Emission: sympathetic (T11–L2); semen into prostatic urethra
- Ejaculation: somatic (pudendal nerve); bulbospongiosus contracts
- Internal sphincter closes to prevent retrograde flow

---

## c-anp-067

The scrotum is a thin, pendulous external fibromuscular sac of skin containing the two testes, epididymis, and part of the spermatic cord. It is located postero-inferior to the penis and inferior to the pubic symphysis. Internally, the septum of the scrotum divides it into two compartments. The left half hangs lower than the right.

## c-anp-067:summary

- Pendulous sac; contains testes, epididymis, spermatic cord
- Divided by septum into two compartments
- Left half hangs lower

---

## c-anp-068

Scrotal layers (superficial to deep): skin, dartos muscle/fascia, external spermatic fascia (from external oblique aponeurosis), cremaster muscle and fascia (from internal oblique), internal spermatic fascia (from transversalis fascia). Blood supply: anterior and posterior scrotal arteries (from external and internal pudendal arteries). Lymph drains to superficial inguinal nodes. Innervation: anterior scrotal nerves (genital branch of genitofemoral + ilioinguinal) and posterior scrotal nerves (perineal branches of pudendal + posterior femoral cutaneous).

## c-anp-068:summary

- Layers: skin → dartos → external spermatic fascia → cremaster → internal spermatic fascia
- Blood: anterior/posterior scrotal arteries
- Lymph: superficial inguinal nodes
- Nerves: genitofemoral, ilioinguinal, pudendal, posterior femoral cutaneous

---

## c-anp-069

The female reproductive system consists of ovaries, uterine (fallopian) tubes, uterus, vagina, and external genitalia (vulva). The mammary glands are associated but not reproductive organs.

## c-anp-069:summary

- Ovaries, uterine tubes, uterus, vagina, vulva
- Mammary glands associated but not reproductive

---

## c-anp-070

The ovaries are paired ovoid or almond-shaped organs lying in the pelvic cavity. Each has a thick connective tissue capsule (tunica albuginea) covered by simple squamous to cuboidal mesothelium (germinal epithelium). The ovary is divided into an outer cortex (containing follicles in various stages of development) and an inner medulla (containing nerves, blood vessels, lymphatics, loose connective tissue, and rete ovarii).

## c-anp-070:summary

- Paired ovoid organs in pelvic cavity
- Tunica albuginea + germinal epithelium
- Cortex (follicles) + medulla (vessels, nerves, connective tissue)

---

## c-anp-071

Ovarian follicles develop through stages: primordial (primary oocyte + squamous epithelium) → primary (primary oocyte + cuboidal epithelium) → secondary (early: zona pellucida + granulosa cells; late: antrum with follicular fluid + theca) → Graafian/mature (cumulus oophorus + corona radiata + stratum granulosum + theca interna/externa). Theca interna produces androstenedione; granulosa cells convert it to estrogen under FSH influence.

## c-anp-071:summary

- Primordial → primary → secondary → Graafian
- Theca interna → androstenedione; granulosa → estrogen (FSH)
- Graafian: cumulus oophorus, corona radiata, antrum

---

## c-anp-072

After ovulation, the ruptured follicle becomes the corpus luteum. The granulosa cells enlarge to form large luteal cells; theca interna cells form small luteal cells. The corpus luteum secretes progesterone (prepares uterine lining), estrogens, relaxin (relaxes uterine muscles and pubic symphysis), and inhibin (decreases FSH and LH). If no pregnancy occurs, it degenerates into the corpus albicans (fibrous scar).

## c-anp-072:summary

- Ruptured follicle → corpus luteum
- Secretes progesterone, estrogen, relaxin, inhibin
- Degenerates → corpus albicans if no pregnancy

---

## c-anp-073

The uterine (fallopian) tubes are about 12 cm long and transport eggs from the ovaries to the uterus. Four segments: infundibulum (funnel-shaped, with fimbriae), ampulla (widest; usual site of fertilization), isthmus (narrow), and intramural/interstitial portion (within uterine wall). The wall has 3 layers: mucosa (ciliated columnar + secretory cells), muscularis (inner circular, outer longitudinal smooth muscle), and outer serosa.

## c-anp-073:summary

- ~12 cm long; 4 segments: infundibulum, ampulla, isthmus, intramural
- Infundibulum has fimbriae; ampulla = fertilization site
- Mucosa: ciliated + secretory cells

---

## c-anp-074

The uterus is a pear-shaped organ where fetal development occurs. Parts: fundus (superior portion above uterine horns), body (main portion), cervix (inferior portion projecting into vagina). The uterine wall has three layers: endometrium, myometrium, and perimetrium.

## c-anp-074:summary

- Pear-shaped; site of fetal development
- Parts: fundus, body, cervix
- Three wall layers: endometrium, myometrium, perimetrium

---

## c-anp-075

The endometrium is the innermost layer (mucosa) of the uterus. It consists of simple columnar epithelium, lamina propria (rich in cells and collagen), and tubular uterine glands. Divided into lamina functionalis (superficial, shed during menstruation, regenerates from basalis) and lamina basalis (permanent layer with straight arteries).

## c-anp-075:summary

- Innermost layer; simple columnar epithelium + glands
- Functionalis: shed during menstruation; regenerates from basalis
- Basalis: permanent; straight arteries

---

## c-anp-076

The myometrium is the thickest layer of the uterine wall, consisting of smooth muscle with inner circular and outer longitudinal layers. Between them is the stratum vasculare. It increases in thickness during pregnancy (hyperplasia + hypertrophy) and decreases after delivery.

## c-anp-076:summary

- Thickest layer; smooth muscle (inner circular + outer longitudinal)
- Stratum vasculare between layers
- Increases during pregnancy; decreases postpartum

---

## c-anp-077

The perimetrium is the outer serosa of the uterus, consisting of connective tissue covered by mesothelium of the peritoneal cavity. It contains blood vessels, lymphatics, nerves, and sympathetic ganglia.

## c-anp-077:summary

- Outer serosa; connective tissue + mesothelium
- Contains vessels, nerves, ganglia

---

## c-anp-078

The cervix is the tapering inferior portion of the uterus that projects into the vaginal canal. Its wall is mainly dense collagenous and elastic fibers (~15% smooth muscle). The mucosa contains mucous glands and deep branching folds (plicae palmatae). The cervical canal (os) undergoes tremendous dilation during birth.

## c-anp-078:summary

- Tapering inferior uterus; projects into vagina
- Mainly collagenous/elastic (15% smooth muscle)
- Plicae palmatae; cervical canal dilates during birth

---

## c-anp-079

The vagina is a fibromuscular canal (birth canal) extending from the cervix to the external genitalia. Its wall has three layers: mucosa (nonkeratinized stratified squamous epithelium + lamina propria with elastic fibers and rich vascularity), muscularis (mainly longitudinal smooth muscle), and adventitia (dense connective tissue rich in elastic fibers, vessels, and nerves). The vagina has no glands; lubrication comes from plasma seeping through the epithelium from the engorged venous plexus.

## c-anp-079:summary

- Fibromuscular canal from cervix to vulva
- Mucosa: stratified squamous; no glands
- Lubrication from transuded plasma via venous plexus

---

## c-anp-080

The external genitalia (vulva) includes the mons pubis, labia majora, labia minora, vestibule, clitoris, and vestibular glands (Bartholin's glands). The clitoris is the homologue of the penis and is highly sensitive erectile tissue.

## c-anp-080:summary

- Mons pubis, labia majora/minora, vestibule, clitoris, Bartholin's glands
- Clitoris = homologue of penis; highly sensitive

---

## c-anp-081

The urinary bladder is a muscular reservoir of urine situated in the lesser pelvis immediately posterior to the pubic symphysis. When empty, it is pyramidal and lies entirely within the lesser pelvis; when distended, it becomes ovoid and expands into the abdominal cavity. Capacity: 250–500 ml. External features: apex, base (fundus), neck, three surfaces, four borders.

## c-anp-081:summary

- Muscular urine reservoir in lesser pelvis
- Pyramidal when empty; ovoid when distended
- Capacity 250–500 ml

---

## c-anp-082

The trigone is a triangular smooth area at the base of the bladder between the two ureteric orifices (superolateral) and the internal urethral orifice (inferomedial). The mucosa here is firmly attached to the underlying muscle. The two ureteric orifices are connected by the interureteric bar. When empty, the three openings are 2.5 cm apart; when distended, they may exceed 5 cm.

## c-anp-082:summary

- Smooth triangular area at bladder base
- Bounded by 2 ureteric orifices + internal urethral orifice
- Interureteric bar connects ureteric orifices

---

## c-anp-083

The detrusor muscle is the specialized smooth muscle forming the bladder wall. It contracts during micturition and relaxes during filling. Parasympathetic nerves (pelvic splanchnic nerves, S2–S4) stimulate contraction → micturition. Sympathetic nerves (hypogastric nerve, T12–L2) inhibit contraction → relaxation and urine retention.

## c-anp-083:summary

- Smooth muscle of bladder wall
- Contracts during micturition; relaxes during filling
- Parasympathetic (S2–S4) → contraction; Sympathetic (T12–L2) → relaxation

---

## c-anp-084

The internal urethral sphincter is formed by circular smooth muscle fibers at the bladder neck. It is involuntary. In males, it contracts during ejaculation to prevent seminal regurgitation into the bladder. In females, the fibers are continuous with the urethral wall muscle.

## c-anp-084:summary

- Circular smooth muscle at bladder neck; involuntary
- Male: prevents retrograde ejaculation
- Female: continuous with urethral wall

---

## c-anp-085

The external urethral sphincter is made of skeletal muscle under voluntary control. It is the same in both males and females. It is part of the deep perineal pouch and provides voluntary control over micturition.

## c-anp-085:summary

- Skeletal muscle; voluntary control
- Same in both sexes; part of deep perineal pouch
- Provides voluntary micturition control
