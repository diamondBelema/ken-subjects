  - id: ant-001
    concept_id: c-ant-006
    front: What is the mediastinum?
    back: The central compartment of the thoracic cavity — the space between the two pleural cavities, extending from the superior to inferior thoracic aperture. It contains all thoracic viscera except the lungs.
  - id: ant-002
    concept_id: c-ant-006
    front: What structure divides the mediastinum into superior and inferior parts?
    back: The transverse thoracic plane, which passes backwards from the 2nd costal cartilage to the lower border of the T4 vertebra.
  - id: ant-003
    concept_id: c-ant-006
    front: Into how many compartments is the inferior mediastinum divided, and what divides them?
    back: Three compartments — anterior, middle, and posterior — divided by the fibrous pericardium which surrounds the heart.
  - id: ant-004
    concept_id: c-ant-029
    front: Name the contents of the superior mediastinum.
    back: Arch of aorta and its branches, SVC, brachiocephalic veins, azygos system, vagus nerves, left recurrent laryngeal nerve, phrenic nerves, sympathetic trunk, trachea, esophagus, thoracic duct, thymus, sternohyoid and sternothyroid muscles.
  - id: ant-005
    concept_id: c-ant-029
    front: What are the boundaries of the superior mediastinum?
    back: "Superior: thoracic inlet. Anterior: manubrium. Posterior: T1-T4 vertebral bodies. Inferior: transverse thoracic plane (T4/5). Lateral: mediastinal pleura."
  - id: ant-006
    concept_id: c-ant-029
    front: What is the key spatial relationship between the brachiocephalic veins and arteries in the superior mediastinum?
    back: The brachiocephalic veins cross anterior to the arteries. The left brachiocephalic vein crosses obliquely to join the right behind the 1st right costal cartilage.
  - id: ant-007
    concept_id: c-ant-030
    front: What structures are found in the anterior mediastinum?
    back: Internal thoracic artery and veins, pre-pericardial lymph nodes, parasternal and anterior mediastinal lymph nodes, sternopericardial ligaments, fat, and thymus (in infants/children).
  - id: ant-008
    concept_id: c-ant-031
    front: What are the contents of the middle mediastinum?
    back: Heart and pericardium, ascending aorta, pulmonary trunk and arteries, SVC (lower part), azygos vein, pulmonary veins, main bronchi, phrenic nerves, pericardiacophrenic vessels, and tracheobronchial/hilar lymph nodes.
  - id: ant-009
    concept_id: c-ant-032
    front: What are the contents of the posterior mediastinum?
    back: Descending thoracic aorta, esophagus, azygos vein, hemiazygos and accessory hemiazygos veins, thoracic duct, sympathetic trunk, splanchnic nerves, esophageal nerve plexus, posterior mediastinal lymph nodes.
  - id: ant-010
    concept_id: c-ant-032
    front: How does the esophagus relate to the descending thoracic aorta?
    back: The esophagus lies anterior to the thoracic aorta and slightly to the right in the upper thorax. As it descends, it shifts towards the left. It pierces the diaphragm at T10.
  - id: ant-011
    concept_id: c-ant-032
    front: What is the course of the thoracic duct through the mediastinum?
    back: The thoracic duct ascends between the thoracic aorta (left) and azygos vein (right), posterior to the esophagus. It crosses to the left at T5 behind the esophagus, passes upward through the superior mediastinum, and arches over the left lung apex to enter the left venous angle.
  - id: ant-012
    concept_id: c-ant-033
    front: What is the origin and course of the azygos vein?
    back: The azygos vein begins as the union of the right lumbar and right subcostal veins, passes through the aortic opening of the diaphragm, ascends on the posterior chest wall to T4, then arches over the right lung root to enter the SVC.
  - id: ant-013
    concept_id: c-ant-027
    front: At what vertebral levels do the three major diaphragmatic openings occur?
    back: Vena caval opening at T8, esophageal hiatus at T10, and aortic hiatus at T12.
  - id: ant-014
    concept_id: c-ant-027
    front: What structures pass through the esophageal hiatus of the diaphragm?
    back: The esophagus, anterior (left) and posterior (right) vagal trunks, esophageal branches of the left gastric artery and vein.
  - id: ant-015
    concept_id: c-ant-027
    front: What structures pass through the aortic hiatus of the diaphragm?
    back: The descending thoracic aorta, azygos vein, and thoracic duct.
  - id: ant-016

    back: "Motor: phrenic nerves (C3, C4, C5). Sensory (central part): phrenic nerve. Sensory (peripheral part): lower intercostal nerves (T5-T11) and subcostal nerve (T12)."
  - id: ant-017
    concept_id: c-ant-028
    front: What is the mnemonic for the phrenic nerve root value?
    back: "C3, 4, 5 keep the diaphragm alive— the phrenic nerve arises from spinal segments C3, C4, and C5."
  - id: ant-018
    concept_id: c-ant-026
    front: What are the three origins of the diaphragm?
    back: Sternal part (from xiphoid process), costal part (inner surfaces of ribs 7-12 and their costal cartilages), and lumbar/vertebral part (right crus from L1-L3, left crus from L1-L2).
  - id: ant-019
    concept_id: c-ant-026
    front: Which dome of the diaphragm is higher, and why?
    back: The right dome is higher (at the 5th rib) due to the underlying liver. The left dome is lower due to the heart and stomach.
  - id: ant-020
    concept_id: c-ant-009
    front: What is the parietal pleura?
    back: The thicker outer layer of serous membrane that lines the internal surface of the thoracic cavity, adhering to the ribs, mediastinum, and diaphragm. It is sensitive to pressure, pain, temperature, and touch (somatic innervation).
  - id: ant-021
    concept_id: c-ant-009
    front: What are the four parts of the parietal pleura?
    back: Costal pleura (internal thoracic wall), mediastinal pleura (lateral mediastinum), diaphragmatic pleura (superior diaphragmatic surface), and cervical pleura/cupula (lung apex).
  - id: ant-022
    concept_id: c-ant-010
    front: What is the difference between parietal and visceral pleura in terms of sensation?
    back: Parietal pleura is sensitive to pressure, pain, temperature, and touch (somatic innervation via intercostal and phrenic nerves). Visceral pleura is only sensitive to stretch (autonomic innervation from pulmonary plexus).
  - id: ant-023
    concept_id: c-ant-012
    front: What are the two pleural recesses and why are they clinically important?
    back: Costodiaphragmatic recess (between costal and diaphragmatic pleura) and costomediastinal recess (between costal and mediastinal pleura). They are sites where fluid collects in pleural effusion. The left costomediastinal recess is larger due to the pronounced cardiac notch.
  - id: ant-024
    concept_id: c-ant-011
    front: What is the normal volume of pleural fluid per lung?
    back: Approximately 25 mL per lung.
  - id: ant-025
    concept_id: c-ant-011
    front: What are the two functions of the pleural cavity fluid?
    back: (1) Lubrication — allows pleural surfaces to slide over each other during respiration. (2) Surface tension — couples parietal and visceral pleurae together so that when the thorax expands, the lungs also expand with air.
  - id: ant-026
    concept_id: c-ant-019
    front: Describe the external features of the right versus left lung.
    back: "Right lung: 3 lobes (superior, middle, inferior), 2 fissures (oblique and horizontal), ~625 g. Left lung: 2 lobes (superior, inferior), 1 fissure (oblique only), ~565 g, with cardiac notch and lingula."
  - id: ant-027
    concept_id: c-ant-019
    front: What is the lingula and what does it correspond to in the right lung?
    back: The lingula is a tongue-like projection of the left superior lobe below the cardiac notch. It corresponds to the middle lobe of the right lung.
  - id: ant-028
    concept_id: c-ant-020
    front: What is a bronchopulmonary segment?
    back: A pyramidal-shaped functional and surgical unit of the lung, supplied by a segmental bronchus and segmental pulmonary artery branch, drained by intersegmental pulmonary veins. It is the smallest area that can be surgically removed without affecting adjacent regions.
  - id: ant-029
    concept_id: c-ant-020
    front: How many bronchopulmonary segments are in each lung?
    back: 10 segments in the right lung and 8-10 segments in the left lung.
  - id: ant-030
    concept_id: c-ant-022
    front: What structures make up the root of the lung?
    back: Principal bronchus (left) or eparterial/hyparterial bronchi (right), pulmonary arteries, superior and inferior pulmonary veins, bronchial arteries and veins, pulmonary lymph nodes and vessels, and autonomic nerve plexuses. All wrapped in a sleeve of mediastinal pleura extending distally as the pulmonary ligament.
  - id: ant-031
    concept_id: c-ant-021
    front: What is the arrangement of structures in the lung hilum from anterior to posterior?
    back: Vein — Artery — Bronchus (VAB mnemonic). Superior pulmonary vein is most anterior, then pulmonary artery, then bronchus most posterior.
  - id: ant-032
    concept_id: c-ant-024
    front: Why are foreign bodies more likely to lodge in the right main bronchus?
    back: The right main bronchus is wider, shorter (~2.5 cm), and more vertical (a more direct continuation of the trachea) compared to the left main bronchus which is narrower, longer (~5 cm), and more horizontal.
  - id: ant-033
    concept_id: c-ant-023
    front: At what vertebral level does the trachea bifurcate?
    back: The trachea bifurcates at the carina, at the level of the lower border of T4/T5 (sternal angle of Louis).
  - id: ant-034
    concept_id: c-ant-023
    front: How many C-shaped cartilage rings does the trachea have?
    back: 16-20 C-shaped rings of hyaline cartilage. They are deficient posteriorly where the trachealis muscle bridges the gap.
  - id: ant-035
    concept_id: c-ant-050
    front: What is the function of the trachealis muscle?
    back: The trachealis muscle bridges the posterior gap of the C-shaped tracheal cartilage rings, providing flexibility during breathing and allowing constriction of the tracheal lumen during coughing to increase expiratory velocity.
  - id: ant-036
    concept_id: c-ant-035
    front: What type of epithelium lines the trachea?
    back: Pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium (respiratory epithelium) with goblet cells and basal cells. The cilia are more abundant in the lower trachea.
  - id: ant-037
    concept_id: c-ant-025
    front: What changes occur in the epithelium as bronchi become smaller?
    back: The epithelium transitions from pseudostratified ciliated columnar (main bronchi) to simple ciliated columnar (smaller bronchi) to cuboidal (respiratory bronchioles). Goblet cells decrease and disappear in bronchioles. Cartilage decreases then disappears. Smooth muscle increases.
  - id: ant-038
    concept_id: c-ant-046
    front: What are the three cell types found in the alveoli?
    back: Type I pneumocytes (squamous, 90% of alveolar surface, form blood-air barrier), Type II pneumocytes (cuboidal, 5% of surface, produce surfactant, can differentiate into Type I), and alveolar macrophages/dust cells (phagocytic defense).
  - id: ant-039
    concept_id: c-ant-040
    front: What is the blood-air barrier composed of?
    back: "From alveolar to capillary side: surfactant layer, Type I pneumocyte cytoplasm, pneumocyte basement membrane, intervening connective tissue, capillary endothelial basement membrane, capillary endothelial cell cytoplasm."
  - id: ant-040
    concept_id: c-ant-041
    front: What is pulmonary surfactant and what does it do?
    back: A phospholipid-protein mixture produced by Type II pneumocytes that reduces surface tension in alveoli, preventing alveolar collapse (atelectasis) during expiration, stabilizing alveolar diameter, and facilitating lung expansion. It also has bactericidal properties.
  - id: ant-041
    concept_id: c-ant-037
    front: What are Clara cells and where are they found?
    back: Clara (Club) cells are non-ciliated secretory cells found predominantly in terminal bronchioles, replacing goblet cells. They produce surfactant components, reduce surface tension, protect against emphysema, act as stem cells, and protect against inhaled toxins.
  - id: ant-042
    concept_id: c-ant-046
    front: How many alveoli are in a normal lung and what is their total surface area?
    back: Approximately 200 million alveoli with a total surface area of 75 square meters. The total capillary surface area for gas exchange is 125 square meters.
  - id: ant-043
    concept_id: c-ant-046
    front: What are the pores of Kohn?
    back: Pores in the interalveolar septum that allow passage of air between adjacent alveoli, equalizing pressure between them.
  - id: ant-044
    concept_id: c-ant-047
    front: What is the dual blood supply of the lungs?
    back: Pulmonary circulation (pulmonary arteries carry deoxygenated blood for gas exchange, pulmonary veins return oxygenated blood to left atrium) and bronchial circulation (bronchial arteries from descending aorta supply bronchi, lung roots, visceral pleura with nutritive blood).
  - id: ant-045
    concept_id: c-ant-049
    front: What is the nerve supply of the lungs?
    back: "From pulmonary plexuses: parasympathetic (vagus) causes bronchoconstriction, secretion, and vasodilation. Sympathetic causes bronchodilation and vasoconstriction. Visceral afferents conduct stretch impulses via the vagus."
  - id: ant-046
    concept_id: c-ant-043
    front: At what week does the lung bud appear?
    back: The respiratory diverticulum (lung bud) appears at approximately the 4th week of development as an outgrowth from the ventral wall of the foregut, signaled by fibroblast growth factors (FGFs).
  - id: ant-047
    concept_id: c-ant-044
    front: What is the tracheoesophageal septum and what does it form?
    back: The tracheoesophageal septum is formed by the fusion of the tracheoesophageal ridges, dividing the foregut into the esophagus (dorsally) and the trachea with lung buds (ventrally).
  - id: ant-048
    concept_id: c-ant-045
    front: At what gestational age can a premature infant survive due to sufficient surfactant production?
    back: At the 7th month of gestation, sufficient numbers of capillaries are present for gas exchange and Type II pneumocytes produce adequate surfactant, making survival of premature infants possible.
  - id: ant-049
    concept_id: c-ant-045
    front: How many alveoli are present at birth versus adulthood?
    back: About 1/6 of the adult number of alveoli are present at birth. The remaining alveoli are formed during the first 10 years of postnatal life.
