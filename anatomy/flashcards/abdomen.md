  - id: ana-040

  - id: ana-041
    concept_id: c-portal-system
    front: Where and how is the portal vein formed?
    back: Posterior to the neck of the pancreas at L2 by the union of the splenic vein and the superior mesenteric vein.

  - id: ana-042

    back: 1) Esophageal (left gastric vein ↔ azygous system), 2) Rectal (superior rectal ↔ inferior rectal veins), 3) Paraumbilical (portal veins ↔ anterior abdominal wall veins), 4) Retroperitoneal (mesenteric ↔ retroperitoneal veins).

  - id: ana-043
    concept_id: c-portal-system
    front: What is caput medusae and what causes it?
    back: Dilated veins radiating from the umbilicus on the anterior abdominal wall, caused by portal hypertension reopening paraumbilical veins.

  - id: ana-044

  - id: ana-047

  - id: ana-048

  - id: ana-049
    concept_id: c-jejunum-ileum

    back: Large aggregations of 30-50 lymphoid nodules found in the lamina propria of the ileum, serving as immune surveillance points.

  - id: ana-050
    concept_id: c-jejunum-ileum
    front: How do goblet cells change in distribution from duodenum to ileum?
    back: Goblet cells increase in number progressively from duodenum to ileum, with the highest density in the ileum.

  - id: ana-051
    concept_id: c-small-intestine-histology
    front: What are the two plexuses found in the GI tract wall and what do they control?
    back: Meissner's (submucosal) plexus — controls glandular secretions. Auerbach's (myenteric) plexus — controls motility/peristalsis.

  - id: ana-052
    concept_id: c-large-intestine-histology
    front: How does the large intestine differ histologically from the small intestine?
    back: No villi, no plicae circulares, abundant goblet cells (increasing distally), straight tubular crypts, no Paneth cells, and the outer longitudinal muscle forms 3 teniae coli (not a complete layer).

  - id: ana-053
    concept_id: c-large-intestine
    front: What are teniae coli and what do they form?
    back: Three bands of longitudinal smooth muscle that shorten the colon, creating sacculations called haustra.

  - id: ana-054
    concept_id: c-appendix
    front: How long is the vermiform appendix and what is its main histological feature?
    back: 6-10 cm long. The main histological feature is abundant lymphoid follicles (GALT) in the lamina propria and submucosa.

  - id: ana-055
    concept_id: c-appendix
    front: What causes the initial periumbilical pain in appendicitis that later localizes to the right lower quadrant?
    back: Initial pain is referred visceral pain (midgut origin, T10). As inflammation progresses and irritates the parietal peritoneum, pain localizes to the right lower quadrant (somatic pain, well-localized).
    notes: "ana-055: This is a classic example of visceral → somatic pain transition."

  - id: ana-056
    concept_id: c-gallbladder
    front: Where does the fundus of the gallbladder correspond to on the anterior abdominal wall?
    back: The tip of the 9th right costal cartilage, where the lateral border of the rectus abdominis crosses the costal margin (transpyloric plane of Addison).

  - id: ana-057
    concept_id: c-biliary-system
    front: What is the cystohepatic triangle (of Calot) and what does it contain?
    back: Bounded by the common hepatic duct (medially), cystic duct (laterally), and cystic artery (superiorly). Contains the right hepatic artery, cystic artery, Lund's lymph node, and lymphatics.

  - id: ana-058

    front: Describe the pathway of bile from hepatocytes to the duodenum.
    back: Hepatocytes → bile canaliculi → canals of Hering → bile ductules → interlobular ducts → right and left hepatic ducts → common hepatic duct → (joins cystic duct) → common bile duct → ampulla of Vater (sphincter of Oddi) → major duodenal papilla → duodenum.

  - id: ana-059
    concept_id: c-biliary-system
    front: What is the sphincter of Oddi and what does it regulate?
    back: Circular smooth muscle surrounding the terminal parts of both the bile duct and pancreatic duct and the hepatopancreatic ampulla. It regulates the flow of bile and pancreatic juice into the duodenum.

  - id: ana-060
    concept_id: c-gallbladder-histology
    front: What two layers are notably ABSENT from the gallbladder wall (unlike most GI organs)?
    back: Muscularis mucosae and submucosa. The mucosa lies directly on the muscular layer.

  - id: ana-061
    concept_id: c-gallbladder-histology
    front: What are Rokitansky-Aschoff sinuses?
    back: Epithelial outpouchings that extend into the muscular layer of the gallbladder wall. Normal variant, but become more prominent in chronic cholecystitis.

  - id: ana-062
    concept_id: c-gallbladder-histology
    front: Why does the gallbladder mucosa appear highly folded when empty but flatten when distended?
    back: The folds increase absorptive surface area for bile concentration. When the gallbladder fills with bile, the folds flatten to accommodate the increased volume.

  - id: ana-067

  - id: ana-068
    concept_id: c-pancreas-histology
    front: What are centroacinar cells and what is unique about them?
    back: Pale-staining cells located at the center of pancreatic acini, representing the beginning of the ductal system. They are unique to the pancreas (not found in salivary glands) and secrete bicarbonate and water.

  - id: ana-069
    concept_id: c-pancreas-histology
    front: Which cells of the Islets of Langerhans are most numerous and where are they located?
    back: Beta (β) cells are most numerous (60-80%) and are predominantly located in the central region of the islet.

  - id: ana-070
    concept_id: c-pancreas-histology
    front: What percentage of islet cells are alpha cells and what hormone do they secrete?
    back: 15-20% of islet cells. They secrete glucagon, which raises blood glucose by stimulating glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis.

  - id: ana-071
    concept_id: c-pancreas-histology
    front: How do pancreatic acini differ from salivary gland acini histologically?
    back: Pancreas has centroacinar cells, no striated ducts, only serous acini (no mucous or mixed), no myoepithelial cells, and Islets of Langerhans present.

  - id: ana-072
    concept_id: c-pancreas-histology
    front: What does the exocrine pancreas secrete and how are the enzymes activated?
    back: Amylase (carbohydrates), lipase (fats), trypsinogen and chymotrypsinogen (inactive proteases). Trypsinogen is activated to trypsin by enterokinase in the duodenum, which then activates other zymogens.

  - id: ana-073
    concept_id: c-spleen
    front: Where is the spleen located and how is it related to the ribs?
    back: Left hypochondrium, deep to ribs 9, 10, and 11. Its long axis lies along the shaft of the 10th rib.

  - id: ana-074

  - id: ana-075
    concept_id: c-spleen-histology

  - id: ana-076
    concept_id: c-spleen-histology
    front: What is the PALS and what cells does it contain?
    back: Periarterial lymphatic sheath — a thymus-dependent zone surrounding the central artery of the white pulp, containing mainly T lymphocytes.

  - id: ana-077

    front: What are the cords of Billroth?
    back: Cellular aggregations in the red pulp supported by reticular connective tissue, containing macrophages, plasma cells, and blood cells. They filter the blood by removing aged RBCs and microorganisms.

  - id: ana-078

  - id: ana-079
    concept_id: c-spleen
    front: What happens to splenic functions after total splenectomy in an adult?
    back: Most functions are assumed by other reticuloendothelial organs (liver and bone marrow), but there is greater susceptibility to certain bacterial infections (especially encapsulated organisms).

  - id: ana-086

  - id: ana-088

    notes: "ana-088: Failure of this mechanism causes vesicoureteral reflux, predisposing to urinary tract infections and renal damage."

  - id: ana-097
