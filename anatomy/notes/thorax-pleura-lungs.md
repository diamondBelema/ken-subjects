# Pleura and Lungs

---

## PLEURA

### Definition
- **Serous membranes** composed of a layer of simple squamous cells (mesothelium) supported by connective tissue
- Lines the lungs and thoracic cavity
- Permit effective and easy respiration
- Two pleurae associated with the lungs

### Layers

| Layer | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Visceral pleura** | Covers all surfaces of the lungs, including horizontal and oblique fissures. Cannot be dissected from lung surface. Provides smooth slippery surface for free movement |
| **Parietal pleura** | Lines the thoracic cavity, adhering to thoracic wall (ribs), mediastinum, and diaphragm. Thicker than visceral pleura |
| **Pleural cavity** | Potential space between visceral and parietal pleura. Contains ~25 mL serous fluid per lung |

### Parietal Pleura Regions

The parietal pleura is divided based on what it contacts:

- **Costal pleura** - covers internal surfaces of thoracic wall (sternum, ribs, costal cartilages, intercostal muscles, sides of thoracic vertebrae). Separated from thoracic wall by endothoracic fascia (natural cleavage plane for surgery)
- **Mediastinal pleura** - covers lateral aspect of the mediastinum. Continues superiorly into cervical pleura
- **Diaphragmatic pleura** - covers thoracic (superior) surface of diaphragm on each side of mediastinum. Connected to diaphragm by phrenicopleural fascia
- **Cervical pleura** - covers apex of lung extending through superior thoracic aperture into root of neck. Forms cup-like dome (pleural cupula). Reinforced by suprapleural membrane (Sibson fascia)

### Pleural Recesses

Anteriorly and posteroinferiorly, the lungs do not completely fill the pleural cavity, creating recesses where opposing parietal pleura surfaces touch:

| Recess | Location |
|--------|----------|
| **Costodiaphragmatic** | Between costal pleurae and diaphragmatic pleura |
| **Costomediastinal** | Between costal pleurae and mediastinal pleurae, behind sternum |

- Clinical significance: sites for **pleural effusion** fluid collection
- The **left costomediastinal recess** is larger due to more pronounced cardiac notch in left lung

### Neurovascular Supply

#### Parietal Pleura
- **Sensory**: Sensitive to pressure, pain, temperature, and touch (somatic innervation)
  - Costovertebral pleura: intercostal nerves
  - Diaphragmatic pleura: phrenic nerve (domes) + intercostal nerves (periphery)
  - Mediastinal pleura: phrenic nerve
- **Blood supply**: intercostal, internal thoracic, musculophrenic arteries
- **Venous drainage**: azygos system of veins
- **Lymphatics**: intercostal, parasternal, diaphragmatic, and posterior mediastinal nodes

#### Visceral Pleura
- **Sensory**: Only sensitive to stretch (autonomic)
- **Nerve supply**: pulmonary plexus (sympathetic trunk + vagus nerve)
- **Arterial supply**: bronchial arteries (branches of descending aorta)

## APPLIED ANATOMY OF THE PLEURA

| Condition | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Pneumothorax** | Air/gas in pleural cavity. Removes surface tension of serous fluid, reducing lung expansion. Caused by penetrating wounds or fractured ribs tearing visceral pleura |
| **Hydrothorax** | Significant fluid accumulation in pleural cavity from pleural effusion |
| **Hemothorax** | Blood accumulation in pleural cavity, usually from injury to intercostal or internal thoracic vessels |
| **Hemopneumothorax** | Combination of blood and air in pleural cavity |
| **Pleural effusion** | Excess fluid between visceral and parietal pleurae |
| **Empyema** | Pus collection in pleural space |
| **Chylothorax** | Rupture of thoracic duct |
| **Pleuritis (Pleurisy)** | Inflammation of pleura causing rough surfaces. May cause pleural adhesion. Acute form: sharp stabbing pain with respiration |
| **Fibrothorax** | Accumulation of fibrous tissue in pleural cavity from undrained pleural fluid |

**Clinical procedures**:
- **Thoracentesis**: Hypodermic needle through intercostal space to sample/remove fluid or pus
- **Chest tube insertion**
- **Thoracoscopy**: For biopsy

## LUNGS

### Definition
- Spongy, expandable organ occupying the thoracic cavity
- Conical shape, occurs in pairs
- Spongy texture enables floating in water and crepitation when handled (air in alveoli)
- Suspended freely within pleural membrane
- Separated by heart and mediastinal contents

### Weight
- Right lung: ~625g (empty of blood: ~240g)
- Left lung: ~565g (smaller due to heart)
- Male lungs heavier than female (height-dependent)

### External Features
- At birth: pinkish color
- Young adults: brown or grey
- Smokers/industrial areas: mottled black (carbon particle deposition)

### External Features

### Apex
- Rounded superior end extending into root of neck anteriorly
- Lies within thoracic cavity (posterior view) and above superior thoracic aperture (anterior view)
- Lined by cervical pleura and suprapleural membrane (Sibson fascia)
- Separated from subclavian artery and vein (which groove its anterior surface)
- Extends 3-4 cm above anterior end of 1st rib
- About 2.5 cm above medial 1/3 of clavicle

### Base (Diaphragmatic Surface)
- Concave, related to diaphragm inferiorly
- Overlies superior surface of diaphragm
- Right side: separated from right lobe of liver
- Left side: separated from left lobe of liver, fundus of stomach, and spleen

### Surfaces

| Surface | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Costal** | Smooth, convex; faces internal surface of chest wall. Bears rib marks. Lined by costal pleura and endothoracic fascia |
| **Diaphragmatic** | Concave base over dome of diaphragm. Right dome higher (liver) so right lung has deeper concavity |
| **Medial (Mediastinal)** | Two parts: vertebral (posterior 1/3, rounded, in paravertebral gutter) and mediastinal (anterior 2/3, overlies heart/pericardium). Contains hilum and pulmonary ligament attachment |

- Cardiac impression: shallow concave depression anteroinferior to hilum. Deeper in left lung
- 1st rib and subclavian vessels groove anterior surfaces of both lungs

### Borders

| Border | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Anterior** | Thin, sharp, shorter than posterior. Right is more vertical. Left has cardiac notch below 4th costal cartilage, forming lingula. Area of superficial cardiac dullness |
| **Inferior** | Sharp, thin anteriorly. Separates base from costal/medial surfaces. Descends into costodiaphragmatic recess |
| **Posterior** | Thick, ill-defined, rounded. Fits into paravertebral gutter. Contacts thoracic vertebrae and IVDs. Extends from C7 to T10 |

### Fissures and Lobes

#### Oblique Fissure (both lungs)
- Cuts across costal, diaphragmatic, and medial surfaces
- Begins posteriorly at spine of T3
- Runs downward and forward
- Ends anteriorly at 6th costochondral junction

#### Horizontal Fissure (right lung only)
- Runs from 4th costal cartilage
- Meets oblique fissure near 5th rib
- Posteriorly corresponds to T4 level

| Lung | Lobes |
|------|-------|
| **Right** | 3 lobes: superior (above horizontal fissure), middle (smallest, between horizontal and oblique fissures), inferior (behind oblique fissure) |
| **Left** | 2 lobes: superior and inferior. Lingula (tongue-like process of superior lobe below cardiac notch) corresponds to right middle lobe |

### Hilum and Root of the Lung

**Hilum**: Large depressed wedge-shaped area on medial surface near center. Lies behind 3rd-4th costal cartilage at sternal margin or T5-T7 level. Structures enter/leave lungs here.

**Root of the lung**: Short tubular collection of structures attaching lungs to mediastinum. Wrapped by sleeve of mediastinal pleura that reflexes onto lung as visceral pleura. Extends distally as pulmonary ligament.

#### Components of the Root
- Principal bronchus (left) / eparterial and hyparterial bronchi (right)
- Pulmonary arteries
- Superior and inferior pulmonary veins (2 in each lung)
- Bronchial arteries (2 left, 1 right) and veins
- Pulmonary lymph nodes and vessels
- Anterior and posterior pulmonary autonomic nerve plexuses

#### Arrangement (Anterior to Posterior): **VAB** mnemonic
- **V**ein (superior pulmonary vein)
- **A**rtery (pulmonary artery)
- **B**ronchus

### Bronchopulmonary Segments

- Pyramidal-shaped functional and surgical units of lung
- Each supplied by a segmental (tertiary) bronchus and segmental branch of pulmonary artery
- Drained by intersegmental pulmonary veins
- Smallest area that can be isolated and removed without affecting adjacent regions
- **10 segments in right lung**, **8-10 in left lung**

| Right Lung Segments | Left Lung Segments |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Upper lobe**: Apical (S1), Posterior (S2), Anterior (S3) | **Upper lobe**: Apicoposterior (S1+S2), Anterior (S3), Superior lingular (S4), Inferior lingular (S5) |
| **Middle lobe**: Lateral (S4), Medial (S5) | |
| **Lower lobe**: Superior (S6), Medial basal (S7), Anterior basal (S8), Lateral basal (S9), Posterior basal (S10) | **Lower lobe**: Superior (S6), Anteromedial basal (S7+S8), Lateral basal (S9), Posterior basal (S10) |

### Tracheobronchial Tree

Conducting air passages from trachea into lungs. Resembles inverted tree.

**Hierarchy**: Trachea -> Main (primary) bronchi -> Lobar (secondary) bronchi -> Segmental (tertiary) bronchi -> Terminal bronchioles -> Respiratory bronchioles -> Alveolar ducts -> Alveolar sacs -> Alveoli

#### Trachea
- 10-12 cm long, 2 cm diameter
- Begins at lower border of cricoid cartilage (C6)
- Bifurcates at carina (T4-T5)
- 16-20 C-shaped hyaline cartilage rings
- Trachealis muscle posteriorly allows flexibility

#### Bronchial Bifurcation at Carina
- **Right main bronchus**: wider, shorter (~2.5 cm), more vertical. Foreign bodies more likely to lodge here
- **Left main bronchus**: narrower, longer (~5 cm), more horizontal (heart position)

#### Bronchial Branching
**Right lung**:
- Superior lobar bronchus -> 3 segmental: apical, posterior, anterior (APA)
- Middle lobar bronchus -> 2 segmental: medial, lateral (ML)
- Inferior lobar bronchus -> 5 segmental: apical, medial basal, anterior basal, posterior basal, lateral basal

**Left lung**:
- Superior lobar bronchus -> superior division (apicoposterior, anterior) + lingula division (superior lingula, inferior lingula)
- Inferior lobar bronchus -> 4 segmental: apical, posterior, lateral, anteromedial

### Vascular Supply and Innervation

#### Arterial Supply
- **Pulmonary arteries** (right and left from pulmonary trunk): carry deoxygenated blood to lungs
- **Bronchial arteries** (from descending aorta): nutritive supply to bronchi, lung roots, visceral pleura, supporting tissues

#### Venous Drainage
- **Pulmonary veins** (superior and inferior on each side): carry oxygenated blood to left atrium
- **Bronchial veins**: right drains to azygos vein; left drains to accessory hemiazygos or left superior intercostal vein

#### Lymphatic Drainage
Superficial and deep lymphatic plexus -> bronchopulmonary nodes -> superior/inferior tracheobronchial nodes -> right/left bronchiomediastinal lymph trunk

#### Nerve Supply (Pulmonary Plexuses)

| Nerve | Effect |
|-------|--------|
| **Parasympathetic** (vagus nerve) | Stimulates bronchial gland secretion, bronchial smooth muscle contraction, pulmonary vessel vasodilation |
| **Sympathetic** (sympathetic trunks) | Stimulates bronchial smooth muscle relaxation, pulmonary vessel vasoconstriction |
| **Visceral afferent** | Conducts stretch impulses via vagus nerve sensory ganglion |

### Applied Anatomy of Lungs

| Condition | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Tuberculosis** | Apex most prone (least ventilated part) |
| **Pneumonia** | Inflammatory condition of lung parenchyma (alveoli). Symptoms: cough, chest pain, fever, dyspnea |
| **Bronchitis/Bronchiolitis** | Inflammation of respiratory tract |
| **COPD** | Damage causing difficulty blowing air out, shortness of breath. Most commonly from smoking |
| **Asthma** | Bronchial inflammation causing spasm, wheezing. Triggered by allergies, viral infections, air pollution |
| **Pulmonary fibrosis** | Interstitial scarring making lungs stiff. Causes shortness of breath |
| **Lung cancer** | Most caused by smoking |
| **Pertussis (Whooping cough)** | Contagious airway infection causing persistent cough |
| **Pulmonary embolism** | Blood clot from leg vein travels to heart -> pumped to lungs. Sudden dyspnea |
