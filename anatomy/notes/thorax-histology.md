# Histology of Trachea and Lungs

---

## Respiratory System Overview

### Two Functional Portions

| Portion | Structures | Function |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| **Conducting portion** | Nasal cavities, pharynx, trachea, bronchi, bronchioles (large -> smaller -> terminal) | Warm, filter, moisten air |
| **Respiratory portion** | Respiratory bronchioles, alveolar ducts, alveoli | Gas exchange |

### Key Features of Conducting Portion
- Walls have skeletal basis: bone, cartilage, and connective tissue — keeps passages always patent
- Smooth muscle in walls of trachea and bronchi enables lumen size alteration
- Lined mostly by **pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium** (respiratory epithelium)
- Epithelium kept moist by serous, goblet, and mucous gland secretions
- Mucoid secretions trap dust particles from inhaled air
- Deep to mucosa: rich capillary network warms inspired air

## Histology of the Trachea

### Structure
- Wide flexible tube
- 16-20 **C-shaped rings of hyaline cartilage**
- Gaps between rings filled by **trachealis muscle** and fibroelastic tissue
- Intervals between cartilages filled by fibrous tissue
- Keeps lumen open while allowing flexibility during inspiration/expiration

### Layers (from lumen outward)

#### 1. Mucosa

**Epithelium**: Tall pseudostratified ciliated columnar with:
- **Goblet cells** (more in upper trachea) — secrete mucus
- **Basal cells** — regenerate other cell types
- **Brush cells** — microvilli on surface
- **Kulchitsky (granule) cells** — neuroendocrine
- **Cilia** — more abundant in lower trachea, move mucus upward toward pharynx

**Lamina propria**: Contains:
- Elastic fibers
- Lymphocytes
- Mast cells
- Blood vessels that warm the air

#### 2. Submucosa
- **Mixed seromucous glands** — watery secretions humidify air; mucus traps particles
- Elastic fibers
- Aggregates of lymphoid tissue

#### 3. Cartilage and Smooth Muscle Layer
- C-shaped hyaline cartilage with perichondrium and chondrocytes
- Ends of cartilage connected by smooth muscle (trachealis)

#### 4. Adventitia
- Fibroelastic tissue

## Bronchial Histology Changes

### Primary (Main) Bronchus
Similar to trachea but differs:
- Respiratory epithelium is **less tall** with **fewer goblet cells**
- Lamina propria contains **more elastin** in upper layer
- Lamina propria is **separated from submucosa by a layer of smooth muscle** (becomes more prominent distally)
- Submucosa contains **fewer seromucous glands**
- Cartilage becomes **flattened**

### Tertiary Bronchus
- Epithelium is tall with **little pseudostratification**
- Goblet cells are **diminished**
- Lamina propria is thin, elastic, and completely encircled by smooth muscle
- Glands are sparse, rarely found in smaller airways
- Cartilage reduced to **irregular plates**
- Small lymphocytes present

### Summary of Changes as Bronchi Become Smaller

| Feature | Change |
|---------|--------|
| **Cartilage** | Irregular and smaller. Absent in bronchioles |
| **Smooth muscle** | Increases as bronchi become smaller. Spasm causes breathing difficulty in allergies |
| **Subepithelial lymphoid tissue** | Increases with decreasing bronchial diameter |
| **Glands** | Fewer. Absent in bronchioles |
| **Epithelium** | Pseudostratified ciliated columnar -> simple ciliated columnar -> non-ciliated columnar -> cuboidal (respiratory bronchioles) |

## Bronchioles

### General Features
- Airways of **<1 mm diameter**
- **No cartilage or glands**
- Ciliated columnar epithelium
- Fewer goblet cells
- Wall is smooth muscle
- Neuroendocrine cells secrete **serotonin and bombesin** (regulate muscle tone)

### Terminal Bronchiole
- Columnar epithelium
- No cartilage
- Smooth muscle present
- **Clara cells** present (replace goblet cells)

### Respiratory Bronchiole
- Cuboidal epithelium
- No mucous glands
- First airways to function in **gas exchange**
- Lined by cuboidal cells, connect to alveoli and alveolar ducts

## Cells of the Respiratory Passages

| Cell Type | Location | Function |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| **Goblet cells** | Throughout conducting airways | Secrete mucus; trap dust particles; moved by ciliary action toward pharynx |
| **Non-ciliated serous cells** | Throughout conducting airways | Secrete watery fluid keeping epithelium moist |
| **Basal cells** | Basal layer of epithelium | Multiply and transform into other cell types; replace lost cells |
| **Clara cells** | Predominantly terminal bronchioles | Produce surfactant component; secrete fluid reducing surface tension; protect against emphysema; act as stem cells; protect against inhaled harmful substances |
| **Brush cells** | Throughout | Microvilli on surface |
| **Argyrophil (neuroendocrine) cells** | Throughout | Contain granules; secrete hormones and active peptides including serotonin and bombesin |
| **Lymphocytes** | Epithelium | Immune function |

## Alveoli

### General Structure
- Sac-like evaginations at terminal end of bronchial tree
- Spongy, form the parenchyma of the lung
- ~200 million in a normal lung
- Total area: **75 square meters**
- Total capillary surface area available for exchange: **125 square meters**
- In section, resemble a honeycomb

### Interalveolar Septum
- Separates thin epithelial lining of two neighboring alveoli
- Contains:
  - Network of capillaries supported by reticular and elastic fibers
  - Occasional fibroblasts, macrophages, mast cells
  - **Alveolar pores of Kohn** — allow passage of air between alveoli, equalizing pressure
  - **Elastic fibers** — enable expansion during inspiration, passive contraction during expiration
  - **Reticular fibers** — support and prevent overdistension

### Alveolar Cells

#### Type I Pneumocytes (Squamous Epithelial Cells)
- Form lining of **90% of alveolar surface**
- Most numerous
- Squamous, extremely thin (0.05-0.2 micrometers)
- Edges of adjacent cells overlap, united by tight junctions
- Form the **blood-air barrier**

#### Type II Pneumocytes (Septal Cells)
- Rounded or cuboidal secretory cells with microvilli
- Cover only **5% of alveolar surface**
- Contain secretory granules made of several layers — **multilamellar bodies**
- **Secrete surfactant**
- Can differentiate into Type I cells (regeneration capacity)

#### Type III Cells (Alveolar Macrophages / Dust Cells)
- Enter connective tissue from blood
- Located within alveolar space
- Phagocytose dust particles (visible in cytoplasm — hence "dust cells")

---

## Pulmonary Surfactant

- Fluid secreted over alveolar surface
- Contains **phospholipids, proteins, and glycosaminoglycans**
- **Reduces surface tension** and prevents alveolar collapse during expiration
- Constantly renewed
- Removed from surface by Type I pneumocytes and macrophages
- Stabilizes alveolar diameters, facilitates expansion
- Has **bactericidal properties**

---

## Blood-Air Barrier

Consists of the following layers (from alveolar lumen to capillary blood):

1. Thin layer of **surfactant**
2. **Cytoplasm of Type I pneumocytes**
3. **Basement membrane of pneumocytes**
4. **Intervening connective tissue**
5. **Basement membrane of endothelial cell**
6. **Cytoplasm of capillary endothelial cells**

### Key Points
- Endothelial cells of alveolar capillaries are thin
- Have numerous projections increasing surface area for gaseous exchange
- At some places the **two basement membranes fuse**, reducing barrier thickness

## Defense Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Cilia** | Filter large particles; move mucus upward |
| **Mucus** | Traps smaller particles |
| **Cough reflex** | Expectoration of trapped particles |
| **Alveolar macrophages** | Phagocytose particles reaching alveoli |
| **Lymphoid tissue** | Specific immune response |
