# Urinary Bladder

## Introduction

The urinary bladder is a **muscular reservoir of urine** forming part of the urinary system. It has two functions: **urine retention** and **expulsion of urine**. It lies in the lesser pelvis and is commonly involved in clinical conditions.

---

## Location

- Situated in the **lesser pelvis**, immediately posterior to the pubic symphysis
- **Anterior to**: rectum (male); uterus (female)
- When **empty**: lies entirely within the lesser pelvis
- When **distended**: expands upward and forward into the abdominal cavity

---

## Shape and Size

- **Pyramidal** when empty; **ovoid** when distended
- Capacity: approximately **250–500 ml**

---

## External Features

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Apex** | Points toward upper pubic symphysis; gives attachment to **median umbilical ligament** (remnant of embryonic urachus) |
| **Base (fundus)** | Triangular; in males, separated from rectum by seminal vesicles and vas deferens; in females, separated from cervix by **vesicouterine pouch** |
| **Neck** | Lowest and most fixed part; where inferolateral and posterior surfaces meet; pierced by **urethra**; in males, rests on prostate; in females, in contact with urogenital diaphragm |
| **Superior surface** | Triangular; completely covered by peritoneum; in contact with coils of ileum, jejunum, sigmoid colon; in females, peritoneum reflects onto cervix forming vesicouterine pouch |
| **Inferolateral surfaces** (x2) | Applied to **levator ani** muscles; slope downward and medially; devoid of peritoneum |

## Internal Structure

Four layers (superficial to deep):

| Layer | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Mucosa** | Thick when empty, thrown into folds (**rugae**); smooth when distended |
| **Submucosa** | Loose connective tissue; blood vessels and nerves |
| **Muscularis** | The **detrusor muscle** (see below) |
| **Adventitia/Serosa** | Adventitia on non-peritoneal surfaces; serosa on peritoneal-covered surfaces |

---

## Trigone

- **Triangular area** at the base of the bladder
- **Boundaries**: two **ureteric orifices** (superolateral) and the **internal urethral orifice** (inferomedial)
- When empty, the three openings are **~2.5 cm apart**; may exceed 5 cm when distended
- **Wall is smooth** and the mucous membrane is firmly attached to underlying muscle (does not form rugae)
- The two ureteric orifices are connected by a transverse ridge called the **interureteric bar**
- **Clinical**: site of importance in bladder surgery and cystoscopy

---

## Detrusor Muscle

- **Specialised smooth muscle** forming the bulk of the bladder wall
- Three poorly defined layers: inner longitudinal, middle circular, outer longitudinal
- **Contraction during micturition** expels urine
- **Relaxation** when bladder is filling
- Richly innervated by parasympathetic fibres

---

## Sphincters

### Internal Urethral Sphincter
- **Smooth muscle** (involuntary)
- Located at the **neck of the bladder**
- Formed by circular smooth muscle fibres
- In males: continuous with fibromuscular tissue of the prostate
- In females: continuous with muscle fibres in the urethral wall
- **Contracts during ejaculation** to prevent retrograde flow of semen into the bladder

### External Urethral Sphincter
- **Skeletal muscle** (voluntary)
- Surrounds the urethra at the level of the **urogenital diaphragm**
- Same structure in both males and females
- Under conscious control; allows voluntary suppression of micturition

---

## Blood Supply

### Arterial Supply
| Artery | Territory |
|--------|-----------|
| **Superior vesical artery** | Anterosuperior parts of bladder |
| **Inferior vesical artery** (male) | Fundus and neck |
| **Vaginal artery** (female) | Fundus and neck |
| Small branches from **obturator** and **inferior gluteal** arteries | Additional supply |

All branches from the **internal iliac artery**.

### Venous Drainage
- **Vesical venous plexus** → **internal iliac veins**
- In males: continuous with **prostatic venous plexus** (receives deep dorsal vein of penis)
- In females: envelops pelvic urethra and bladder neck; receives dorsal vein of clitoris; communicates with vaginal/uterovaginal venous plexus

### Lymphatic Drainage
- **External iliac lymph nodes** (main drainage)
- Some drainage to **internal iliac lymph nodes**

---

## Nerve Supply

| System | Origin | Function |
|--------|--------|----------|
| **Parasympathetic** | Pelvic splanchnic nerves (**S2–S4**) | Contracts detrusor muscle → stimulates **micturition** |
| **Sympathetic** | Hypogastric nerve (**T12–L2**) | Inhibits detrusor contraction → promotes **urine retention** (bladder filling) |
| **Somatic** | Pudendal nerve (**S2–S4**) | Voluntary control of **external urethral sphincter** |

---

## Micturition Reflex

1. Bladder fills → stretch receptors in bladder wall are activated
2. Afferent signals travel via pelvic nerves to **sacral micturition centre** (S2-S4)
3. Parasympathetic efferents (pelvic splanchnic nerves) cause:
   - **Detrusor muscle contraction**
   - **Internal urethral sphincter relaxation**
4. Voluntary control via **pudendal nerve** (somatic) allows conscious relaxation or contraction of the **external urethral sphincter**
5. If socially appropriate: external sphincter relaxes → **micturition occurs**
6. Sympathetic input (T12-L2) promotes filling by inhibiting detrusor and contracting internal sphincter

## Clinical Considerations

### Cystitis
- **Inflammation or infection** of the bladder
- Symptoms: acute/chronic pain, discomfort, urinary frequency, hesitancy
- Common cause: bacterial infection (ascending from urethra)

### Urinary Stones (Calculi)
- May form in the kidney and travel to the bladder
- If blocking urine flow → severe pain
- Can cause haematuria and obstruction

### Bladder Cancer
- Usually discovered after **blood in urine** (haematuria)
- Major risk factors: **cigarette smoking** and workplace chemical exposure
- Most common type: transitional cell carcinoma (urothelial carcinoma)

### Overactive Bladder
- Detrusor muscle squeezes **uncontrollably**
- Causes involuntary urine leakage
- **Detrusor overactivity** is a common cause of urinary incontinence

### Urinary Incontinence
- Uncontrolled urination; may be chronic
- Many possible causes:
  - Stress incontinence (weakness of pelvic floor/sphincter)
  - urge incontinence (detrusor overactivity)
  - overflow incontinence (bladder outlet obstruction or detrusor underactivity)
  - functional incontinence

### Urinary Retention
- Urine does not exit bladder normally
- Due to **blockage** (e.g., BPH in males) or **suppressed bladder muscle activity**
- Bladder may swell to hold more than a quart of urine

### Catheterisation
- **Urethral catheter**: inserted through the urethra into the bladder for drainage
- **Suprapubic catheter**: inserted through the abdominal wall directly into the bladder (used when urethral access is not possible)
