# Development of the Respiratory System

---

## Lung Bud Formation (Week 4)

### Initial Formation
- The **respiratory diverticulum (lung bud)** appears as an outgrowth from the **ventral wall of the foregut** at approximately **week 4** of intrauterine life
- Location of the bud is signaled by **fibroblast growth factors (FGFs)** which instruct the endoderm
- Therefore, the **epithelia** of the larynx, trachea, bronchi, and lungs are of **endodermal origin**
- The **cartilaginous, muscular, and connective tissue** of the trachea and lungs are from **splanchnic mesoderm**

---

## Tracheoesophageal Separation

### Process
- The lung bud initially has **open communication with the foregut**
- As the bud expands caudally, **2 longitudinal ridges** (tracheoesophageal ridges) appear and separate it from the foregut
- When the ridges fuse to form the **tracheoesophageal septum**, the foregut is divided into:
  - **Esophagus** (dorsally)
  - **Trachea and lung buds** (ventrally)

### Clinical: Tracheoesophageal Fistula (TEF)
- Abnormalities in partitioning result in esophageal atresia with or without TEF
- **85-90%** of cases: upper esophagus ends in blind pouch, lower segment forms fistula with trachea
- Associated with **polyhydramnios** prenatally (fetus cannot swallow amniotic fluid)
- Postnatally: child regurgitates immediately upon feeding; if TEF present, lungs become congested

---

## Bronchial Development

### Primary Bronchial Buds (Week 5)
- As tracheoesophageal ridges separate from foregut, the lung bud forms the **trachea and 2 bronchial buds**
- By week 5, the 2 buds enlarge to form **right and left main bronchi**
- **Right** forms **3 secondary bronchi** (representing 3 lobes)
- **Left** forms **2 secondary bronchi** (representing 2 lobes)

### Secondary Branching
- Further growth in caudal and lateral directions — lung buds expand into the body cavity
- Within the **pericardioperitoneal canals**, they lie on each side of the foregut

### Tertiary Branching
- Secondary bronchi divide repeatedly and form:
  - **10 tertiary (segmental) bronchi** in the right lung
  - **8 tertiary (segmental) bronchi** in the left lung
- These represent the **bronchopulmonary segments**
- About **17 generations of subdivisions** formed by end of week 6
- Before final shape, 6 more divisions form during **postnatal life**

### Branching Regulation
- Branching is regulated by **epithelial-mesenchymal interactions** between:
  - Endoderm of lung buds
  - Splanchnic mesoderm surrounding them
- Signals for branching come from **fibroblast growth factor (FGF)**
- As subdivisions occur, lungs assume more caudal position
- At birth, tracheal bifurcation is opposite **T4**

## Pleural Development

### Formation
- Mesoderm covering the lung forms the **visceral pleura**
- Somatic mesoderm layer covering the inside body wall forms the **parietal pleura**
- The space between the two is the **pleural cavity**

### Pleuroperitoneal Membrans
- Pleural cavities initially have open communication with the abdominal (peritoneal) cavity
- The opening is closed by the **pleuroperitoneal folds**
- Folds extend medially and ventrally
- By **week 7**, they fuse with the mesentery of the esophagus and the septum transversum
- Further expansion of pleural cavities adds a peripheral rim to the pleuroperitoneal membranes

### Diaphragm Development
The diaphragm is derived from:
1. **Septum transversum** — forms central tendon
2. **Two pleuroperitoneal membranes**
3. **Muscular components** from lateral and dorsal body walls
4. **Mesentery of the esophagus** — forms the crura

## Lung Maturation Phases

### Canalicular Phase (until ~week 7 months)
- Bronchioles continue to divide to form smaller canals
- Vascular supply increases steadily
- Respiration becomes possible when cuboidal respiratory bronchiole cells change into thin, flat cells
- These cells contact blood and lymph capillaries
- Surrounding spaces called **terminal sacs or primitive alveoli**
- By **7th month**, sufficient capillaries present for gas exchange — premature infant can survive

### Terminal Sac Phase (last 2 months of prenatal life)
- Number of terminal sacs increases steadily
- Cells lining sacs (**Type I alveolar epithelial cells**) become thinner
- Surrounding capillaries protrude into alveolar sacs
- Epithelial and endothelial cell contact forms the **blood-air barrier**
- At end of **6th month**, **Type II alveolar epithelial cells** develop (produce surfactant)

### Fetal Lung Fluid
- Before birth, lungs are full of fluid containing:
  - High chloride concentration
  - Little protein
  - Some mucus from bronchial glands
  - Surfactant from Type II alveolar epithelial cells
- Surfactant amount increases particularly during **last 2 weeks before birth**
- **Fetal breathing movements** begin before birth — cause aspiration of amniotic fluid
- These movements stimulate lung development and condition respiratory muscles

### At Birth
- As respiration begins, most lung fluid is **rapidly reabsorbed** by blood and lymph capillaries
- Small amount expelled via trachea and bronchi during delivery
- When fluid is reabsorbed, surfactant remains as thin **phospholipid coat** on alveolar cell membranes
- With air entering alveoli during first breath, surfactant coat prevents development of high surface tension air-water interface
- Without surfactant, alveoli would collapse during expiration (**atelectasis**)

### Postnatal Lung Growth
- Respiratory movements bring air into lungs, which expand and fill pleural cavity
- Growth after birth is mainly due to increase in:
  - Number of respiratory bronchioles
  - Number of alveoli
- About **1/6 of adult number of alveoli** present at birth
- Remaining alveoli formed during **first 10 years of postnatal life**

## Clinical Correlates

### Tracheoesophageal Fistula (TEF) / Esophageal Atresia
- Abnormalities in partitioning by tracheoesophageal septum
- 85-90%: upper esophagus ends in blind pouch, lower segment forms fistula with trachea
- **Prenatal**: polyhydramnios (fetus cannot swallow amniotic fluid)
- **Postnatal**: immediate regurgitation upon feeding; lung congestion if TEF present

### Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) / Hyaline Membrane Disease
- Occurs in premature infants
- Due to **insufficient surfactant** production
- Alveoli collapse during expiration (atelectasis)
- Type II pneumocytes not sufficiently mature to produce adequate surfactant
- More common in infants born before 35 weeks gestation

### Ectopic Lung Lobes
- Lung tissue arising from trachea or esophagus
- Aberrant tissue development during embryogenesis

### Congenital Cysts of the Lung
- Abnormal cystic formations within lung tissue
- Result from abnormal branching or budding during development

### Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia
- Caused by failure of one or both pleuroperitoneal membranes to close the pericardioperitoneal canals
- Abdominal organs herniate into thoracic cavity
- Disrupts lung development, causing problems in lung function after birth
- Can be acquired (blunt force trauma)

### Bronchogenic Carcinoma
- Can compress structures in the mediastinum
- May cause mediastinal widening on imaging

### Atelectasis
- Alveolar collapse due to absence or deficiency of surfactant
- Prevents adequate gas exchange
- The fatty surfactant layer normally prevents this during expiration
