## 1. DNA/RNA Structural Biochemistry

Both A- and B-forms are right-handed helices, but the B-form is the predominant form *in vivo* under normal physiological conditions.

The A-form of the DNA double helix is favored by double-stranded RNA and DNA–RNA hybrids because the 2'-OH group of ribose prevents the B-form geometry.

The Z-form is a left-handed helix, known as Z-DNA. Here the backbone phosphates appear to zigzag, giving rise to the name "Z-DNA." It has been identified *in vivo* in short regions of DNA, proving that DNA is quite flexible and can adopt a variety of conformations.

### Comparison of B-DNA, A-DNA, and Z-DNA

| Property | B-DNA | A-DNA | Z-DNA |
|---|---|---|---|
| Diameter | ~20 Å | ~26 Å | ~18 Å |
| Rotation per bp | +36° | +33° | −30° |
| Vertical rise per bp | 3.4 Å | 2.56 Å | 3.7 Å |

### RNA Structure

- RNA is usually single-stranded, but it can assume double-stranded structures. It can also fold back on itself to perform necessary biological functions when needed — an example is **tRNA**.
- RNA folds into the thermodynamically most stable conformation available, although proteins, ligands, and cellular conditions can stabilize alternative functional conformations.
- Transfer RNA (tRNA) constitutes 10–20% of total RNA and has a molecular weight of about 25,000 (~25 kDa).

## 2. Examples of Functional Changes Due to RNA Conformation

- **Riboswitches:** mRNA domains that change structure, which changes gene expression on or off; small molecules can bind to turn gene expression on or off.
- **Viral RNA regulation:** Viral genomes switch between translation and replication.
- **Protein recognition:** RNA often undergoes conformational changes upon binding proteins or small molecules, to form functional complexes, including its scaffold and ribozymes.

## 3. RNA Conformation, Non-Canonical Base Pairing

RNA conformation arises from the molecule's flexibility, due to the presence of the extra OH group, allowing it to adopt diverse shapes vital for its functional roles in modulating gene activity. Such conformations are expressed through:

- **Secondary structure:** Formed through internal base pairing, resulting in stem-loops, hairpins, and helices.
- **Tertiary structure:** Complex 3-D shapes such as pseudoknots and riboswitches that allow RNA to bind proteins, RNA, DNA, or small molecules, often involved in gene regulation and disease.

**Non-canonical structures:** RNA can form alternate conformations such as G-quadruplexes and triplexes, often involved in gene regulation and disease.

### DNA/RNA Conformational Changes — Noncanonical Base Pairings

Conformational changes are enabled by different H-bonding pairings that exist and are termed noncanonical nucleic acid base pairings, such as:

- **Reverse Watson–Crick bonding:** Reverse Watson–Crick base pairs arise when the glycosidic bond orientation differs from the canonical arrangement while maintaining hydrogen bonding.
- Somewhere at the end of DNA strands and RNA, the pyrimidine can rotate 180° in its plane to align the hydrogen bond donors with acceptors. The glycosidic bond between base and sugar is then in an antiparallel arrangement in the reverse base pair.
- **Wobble base pairing:** G–U wobble pairing is a naturally occurring noncanonical base pair that does not require tautomerization, although rare tautomeric forms of bases can generate other mismatched base pairs.
- **Amino–imino tautomerism** and **keto–enol tautomerism:** Hoogsteen base pairs occur when the purine base rotates from the anti to the syn conformation around its glycosidic bond, allowing alternative hydrogen bonding.
  - The normal *anti* orientation allows Watson–Crick A–T and G–C base pairing.
  - The altered *syn* conformation allows/produces Hoogsteen base pairing, distorted when DNA is bound with Hoogsteen-type hydrogen bonding.

## 4. Cruciform & Superhelical DNA Conformations, Triplex DNA

DNA supercoiling naturally arises during DNA replication and transcription and is regulated by topoisomerases. Intercalating drugs can alter DNA supercoiling but are not its primary cause.

### Cruciform

- A palindrome is read 5'→3' on both complementary strands.
- Example:

```
5'-GAATTC-3'
3'-CTTAAG-5'
```

Reading each strand 5'→3' gives: GAATTC / GAATTC

- Interstrand hydrogen bonds and intrastrand hydrogen bonds together then establish a hairpin between complementary bases in each single strand (thus forming two hairpin-like arms), with 3–4 unpaired bases at their tips.
- The cruciform structure consists of two arms and two relatively long duplex DNA arms, and a junction where four unpaired nucleobases in a fully-involved base pairing are formed.

---

## Triplex DNA Structure

The A–T and G–C base pairs in canonical B-form DNA leave several hydrogen-bond donors and acceptor groups in the major groove. Each purine base has two edges that are exposed in the major groove, which can be used to form additional hydrogen bonds — Hoogsteen-type hydrogen bonds with purines.

- This third strand can consist of blocks of triplex DNA (consisting of the B-form double helix, plus the third strand bases formed) bound in the major groove, and the third strand bases form Hoogsteen hydrogen bonds with purines in base pairs.

## 5. DNA Replication

- It occurs in the S-phase of the cell cycle.
- It occurs in a semi-conservative model.
- DNA replication direction has to occur from 5' → 3'. That means the nucleotide to be synthesized in relation to the old DNA strand — the first strand will be synthesized from the 3' but since the strands are anti-parallel.

**Renaturation:** Separated strands of DNA (or DNA–RNA) will spontaneously reassociate when appropriate temperature or physiological salt concentrations are achieved. Such a reannealing process is sometimes called hybridization.

### Nucleic Acid Hydrolysis

- The bases can be hydrolyzed (or cleaved) by acids.
- For nucleic acid hydrolysis, the N-glycosidic bond is cleaved, producing apurinic (AP) sites.
- Nucleobases selectively cleaved yield an apurinic (depurinated) polynucleotide.
- Hydrolytic production of pyrimidine nucleobases requires the use of hydrazine.

## 6. Nucleic Acid Hydrolysis (continued) & Restriction Endonucleases

For strongly acidic solutions and at higher temperatures (e.g. 12M HCl, 175°C) both RNA and DNA are hydrolyzed into individual components. In addition, cytosine is deaminated to uracil.

- For alkaline hydrolysis of nucleic acids, RNA is more susceptible to hydrolytic cleavage of the phosphodiester bond between nucleotides, than DNA.
- This is because of the presence of the 2'-hydroxyl group in ribose sugar of RNA, which is susceptible to hydrolytic attack in basic solution.
- The product is 2', 3'-cyclic monophosphate and 2'-monophosphate derivatives respectively.

### Enzymatic Hydrolysis of Nucleic Acids

Nucleic acid hydrolysis is catalyzed by nucleases.

- **Exonucleases** may be defined as 3' and 5' specific, depending on which end of the polynucleotide chain the exonucleases cleave the monophosphonucleotide from.
- **Endonucleases** cleave bonds at a certain point within the polynucleotide chain.
- Endonucleases can be phosphate-specific or pentose-specific; there are ribonucleases (RNases) and deoxyribonucleases (DNases).

### Restriction Endonucleases

- Restriction endonucleases (or "restriction enzymes") recognize specific sites in the DNA.
- Restriction enzymes cleave DNA at unique DNA base sequences on both strands, at unique/palindromic sequences of bases. An example is *Bam*HI:

```
...G↓GATCC...
...CCTAG↑G...
```

Restriction enzymes were first isolated from bacteria and function defensively in the host bacteria, since they protect host DNA, especially from foreign organisms (i.e., infective phages).

## 7. Chemical Synthesis of Nucleic Acids

Nucleic acids can be chemically synthesized in the laboratory through artificial creation of specified DNA or RNA sequences.

- The current standard practice utilizes solid-phase phosphoramidite chemistry, in an automated, step-by-step assembly of nucleotides for the desired nucleic acid sequence.
- Usually the synthesis produces oligonucleotides of between 10–200 nucleotides in length.
- The synthesis occurs in the 3' to 5' direction, which is opposite to that in biological synthesis.
- There are various methods employed to accomplish the synthesis, but we shall focus on the commonly preferred method involving 4,4′-dimethoxytrityl (DMT), as a protecting group.

### The Synthesis Cycle (4 reaction steps to complete one nucleotide)

1. **Detritylation**
2. **Coupling**
3. **Oxidation**
4. **Capping**

In theory, there are 3 reaction steps required to complete one cycle of synthesis: detritylation, coupling, and oxidation. The 4th reaction step of capping is necessary in the industry to facilitate practical synthesis.

**a. Detritylation:** This first step in the synthesis is initiated by removal of the DMT protecting group of the first nucleoside, which is attached through its 3'-O to the solid support (CPG or polystyrene). The DMT group is replaced by H+, and the free nucleoside 5'-OH is ready to react with the next nucleoside.

**b. Coupling:** Activated phosphoramidite monomers react with the free 5'-OH to form a phosphite triester linkage. The coupling yields a phosphite triester, which is unstable to the desired product of a dinucleoside with a phosphite triester linkage and a free (diisopropylamino) group.

**c. Oxidation:** It converts the unstable phosphite triester to the stable phosphodiester. Oxidation is achieved with iodine in the presence of water. The product is a normal phosphotriester DNA backbone with a β-cyanoethyl protecting group on the free oxygen.

The dinucleoside can then repeat the cycle of detritylation to elongate the sequence, as required.

**d. Capping:** In industrial practice, capping is required to prevent the accumulation of short, undesired oligomers. This consists of blocking unreacted 5'-OH groups, which would react during the next cycle and enhance a missing base in the product.

Capping uses acetic anhydride to produce an acetylated 5'-OH linked nucleoside with an acetylated 5'-OH.

### Cleavage of the Final Product

After the desired number of cycles, cleavage of the final product is achieved by ester hydrolysis of the linker and detaching it from the solid support. The product is the oligonucleotide using concentrated aqueous ammonia, with a free terminal 3'-OH.

### Chemical Synthesis of RNA

For RNA synthesis, there is the need to protect the 2'-OH of ribonucleosides, in addition to the 2'-protected ribonucleoside phosphoramidite. Protecting groups include *t*-butyldimethylsilyl (TBDMS), which is later removed by treatment with fluoride ion.

**Applications of Synthetic Nucleic Acids:**

1. **PCR primers:** These are short (from 10 to 30 nucleotides) DNA strands used to initiate the polymerase chain reaction for amplification of specific segments of DNA, to multiple copies.
2. Also for primers for DNA sequencing, to determine the base sequence of a specific gene.
3. **Gene synthesis:** For creating long DNA sequences of desired composition for molecular biology of disease.
4. **mRNA vaccines:** Synthetic nucleic acids are used in mRNA vaccines such as SARS-CoV-2 vaccines.

## 8. Chemical Modification of Nucleic Acids — DNA Methylation

DNA methylation is an enzymatic process where methyl groups are added to a DNA molecule, acting as an epigenetic mechanism which alters gene expression.

**Structure:** Cytosine → (DNMT enzyme, with SAM → SAH cofactor) → methylated cytosine (5-methylcytosine).

The methylation primarily occurs at CpG islands — regions where a cytosine nucleotide is followed by a guanine nucleotide. Enzymes called DNA methyltransferases (DNMTs) catalyze the transfer of a methyl group from S-adenosylmethionine (SAM) to the fifth carbon of a cytosine ring, forming 5-methylcytosine and S-adenosylhomocysteine (SAH).

The methylated cytosine residues are usually symmetrical at CpG islands, with guanine nucleotide-cytosine residues sitting diagonally to each other on opposing DNA strands.

### DNA Methylation in Health and Disease

DNA methylation is commonly found in promoter regions of transcription. Inactive genes: methylation recruits methyl-binding proteins and generally reduces transcription factor binding, thus silencing gene expression.

For instance, the hypermethylation of tumor suppressor genes can silence them, leading to unchecked tumor growth.

- Abnormal patterns of methylation have been linked to severe human diseases such as cancers, muscular dystrophy, and a range of birth defects that appear to be caused by defective imprinting mechanisms, such as embryonic development, X-chromosome inactivation, and preservation of chromosome stability.
- In summary, DNA methylation is one of several epigenetic mechanisms that cells use to control gene expression at certain locations within the genomes of different species, where control or regulation of gene expression is required.

## 9. Nucleic Acid Phosphorylation

**Nucleic acid phosphorylation** is the enzymatic process of adding a phosphate group (PO₄³⁻) to a nucleic acid, such as DNA or RNA.

- The most widely used enzyme for this reaction is **T4 Polynucleotide Kinase (T4 PNK)**.
- T4 PNK efficiently transfers the terminal gamma (γ) phosphate group from an ATP donor molecule to the 5' hydroxyl termini of DNA or RNA oligonucleotide.

### Functions in the Laboratory

- **Genetic engineering:** DNA fragments require a 5'-phosphate group as a donor or a phosphate group at a site for a ligation reaction, which is necessary for molecular cloning.
- **Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR):** Taq polymerase leaves 5'-OH on PCR products unless phosphorylated primers were used. Products must undergo enzymatic phosphorylation before they can be successfully ligated into a non-phosphorylated vector.
- **Synthetic DNA:** Chemically synthesized DNA oligonucleotides usually have 5'-OH and 3'-OH unless modified.

### Functions in Nature

- **DNA damage repair:** Ionization, radiation, or oxidative stress often leaves 5' or 3' ends single-stranded or double-stranded breaks. Enzymes like polynucleotide kinase rapidly phosphorylate these broken 5'-OH ends, targeted for polynucleotide DNA ligation (Ligase IV). Without this, DNA cannot be repaired to initiate the DNA repair process.
- Failure to repair DNA breaks activates the DNA damage response, which may result in apoptosis (programmed cell death).

### Histone Phosphorylation vs. Nucleic Acid Phosphorylation

- **Histone phosphorylation** regulates chromatin structure and gene expression. It is an epigenetic modification, but it is not phosphorylation of nucleic acids.
- **Nucleic acid phosphorylation** mainly functions in DNA repair and molecular biology techniques.

### Innate Immunity

- Cells recognize nucleic acids partly through their phosphorylation status. For example, viral RNAs bearing 5'-triphosphates activate RIG-I–mediated innate immune signaling.
