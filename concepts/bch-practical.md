## bch-proteinuria

# Abnormal Urine Analysis (Heat Coagulation Test)

## Aim
To detect the presence of abnormal proteins (primarily albumin) in a urine sample using the heat coagulation method.

## Principle
Proteins are stabilized by hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, and hydrophobic interactions. Heating near boiling point breaks these bonds, causing proteins to unfold and aggregate (coagulate), forming visible white turbidity.

### Why Acetic Acid?
- Lowers pH to albumin's isoelectric point (~pH 4.6), neutralizing net charge, causing maximum precipitation
- **Protein precipitate:** persists/reintensifies with acid + reheating
- **Phosphate precipitate:** dissolves in acid (false positive removed)

## Reagents
- 5% acetic acid
- Unknown urine sample

## Procedure
1. Take 5 ml urine in a clean test tube
2. Heat the top 1/3 layer until it boils
3. Observe for cloudy precipitate or froth
4. Add 1 to 2 drops of 5% acetic acid
5. Reheat to boiling and observe again

## Results
- **Negative:** No precipitate after heating + acid (phosphate dissolved)
- **Positive:** Foamy precipitate persists/intensifies after acid + reheating

## Clinical Significance
- Positive result = proteinuria, which can indicate glomerular damage, nephrotic syndrome, or UTI
- Most sensitive to albumin (coagulates rapidly)
- Further testing: Biuret test for quantification

## bch-proteinuria:summary

Heat coagulation test for proteinuria. Heating denatures protein causing turbidity. Acetic acid distinguishes protein (persists) from phosphate (dissolves). Positive = glomerular damage, nephrotic syndrome. Most sensitive to albumin.

## bch-chromatography

# Paper Chromatography of Amino Acids

## Aim
To separate a mixture of unknown amino acids by ascending paper chromatography and identify them using reference standards.

## Principle
Partition chromatography based on polarity differences.

### Phases
- **Stationary phase:** Thin layer of water adsorbed within cellulose fibres of the paper
- **Mobile phase:** Solvent mixture (n-butanol : glacial acetic acid : water, 4:1:1)

### Separation Mechanism
- Solvent migrates up the paper via capillary action
- Amino acids distribute between stationary (water) and mobile (organic solvent) phases
- **Polar amino acids** (e.g., glycine) interact strongly with stationary phase, travel slower
- **Non-polar amino acids** (e.g., methionine) dissolve in mobile phase, travel faster

### Visualization
- Ninhydrin spray reacts with primary amino groups, producing Ruhemann's Purple (blue-violet)
- **Exception:** Proline and hydroxyproline appear yellow (secondary amine)

### Rf Value
Rf = Distance moved by amino acid / Distance moved by solvent front
- No units, always between 0 and 1
- Used to identify unknowns by comparison with standards

## Reagents
- Cellulose acetate strips
- Solvent (n-butanol : glacial acetic acid : water, 4:1:1)
- Ninhydrin spray reagent
- Standard amino acid solutions (Glycine, Methionine, Hydroxyproline, etc.)

## Procedure
1. Draw pencil line 1 cm from bottom of paper
2. Mark spots 1.5 cm apart for standards and unknown
3. Spot samples using capillary tubes, dry completely
4. Place paper vertically in pre-saturated chromatography jar (solvent below origin line)
5. Seal jar, let solvent ascend near the top
6. Remove, mark solvent front, dry
7. Spray with ninhydrin, heat gently to develop colored spots

## Clinical Significance
- Used for amino acid composition analysis
- Clinical screening for metabolic disorders (e.g., Phenylketonuria, PKU)

## bch-chromatography:summary

Paper chromatography separates amino acids by polarity. Stationary phase = water in cellulose. Mobile phase = organic solvent. Polar amino acids travel slower. Ninhydrin produces purple (yellow for proline). Rf = amino acid distance / solvent distance. No units, 0 to 1.

## bch-serum-urea

# Determination of Serum Urea Concentration

## Aim
To determine the concentration of urea in serum using the urease enzymatic colorimetric method.

## Principle
Two-step reaction:

### Reaction 1: Urease Hydrolysis
Urea + H2O, with Urease enzyme, produces 2NH3 + CO2
- Urease is specific to urea, making this a highly specific test

### Reaction 2: Color Development (Berthelot)
- Ammonia reacts with phenol and hypochlorite, catalyzed by sodium nitroprusside, to form green-blue indophenol chromogen
- Intensity proportional to urea concentration
- Measured at 578 nm

### Beer-Lambert Law
Absorbance is directly proportional to concentration.

## Reagents
- Urease enzyme
- Alkaline reagent (phenol-hypochlorite or diacetyl monoxime)
- Standard urea solution (5.33 mmol/L)
- Serum sample

## Calculation
Concentration (sample) = (Absorbance sample / Absorbance standard) x Concentration standard

## Normal Range
- Adults: 2.5 to 7.1 mmol/L (7 to 20 mg/dL)

## Clinical Significance
- **High serum urea (Azotemia):** Renal impairment, dehydration, high-protein catabolic states
- **Low serum urea:** Severe liver disease (failure to synthesize urea), malnutrition
- BUN is approximately half the value of standard urea measurement

## bch-serum-urea:summary

Serum urea via urease method. Urease produces ammonia, which undergoes Berthelot reaction to produce blue color at 578 nm. Beer-Lambert: absorbance is proportional to concentration. Normal: 2.5 to 7.1 mmol/L. High = renal failure. Low = liver disease.

## bch-electrolytes

# Electrolyte Determination (Na+, K+, Cl-)

## Aim
To determine the concentration of sodium, potassium, and chloride ions in serum using colorimetric or turbidimetric methods.

## Principle

### Sodium (Na+) at 630 nm
Sodium reacts with a selective chromogen (macrocyclic compound) to form a colored complex. Absorbance at 630 nm is proportional to Na+ concentration.

### Potassium (K+) at 578 nm
Potassium reacts with tetraphenylborate under alkaline pH to form a fine turbid emulsion (colloidal suspension). Light is scattered by the turbidity, and absorbance at 578 nm is proportional to K+ concentration.

### Chloride (Cl-) at 500 nm
Mercuric thiocyanate displacement method:
- Cl- displaces SCN- from Hg(SCN)2, forming non-ionized HgCl2
- Free SCN- reacts with Fe3+ to form ferric thiocyanate (deep red)
- Absorbance at 500 nm is proportional to Cl- concentration

## Reagents
- Standard electrolyte solutions (Na+, K+, Cl-)
- Specific chromogenic reagents for each ion
- Serum sample

## Calculation
Concentration (sample) = (Absorbance sample / Absorbance standard) x Concentration standard

## Normal Ranges
- **Sodium:** 135 to 145 mmol/L
- **Potassium:** 3.5 to 5.0 mmol/L
- **Chloride:** 98 to 106 mmol/L

## Clinical Significance
- **Hyponatremia:** Dehydration, vomiting, diuretic use
- **Hypernatremia:** Excessive water loss, high sodium intake
- **Hypokalemia:** Cardiac arrhythmias
- **Hyperkalemia:** Medical emergency, renal failure
- **Hypochloremia:** Metabolic alkalosis, vomiting
- **Hyperchloremia:** Metabolic acidosis, dehydration

## bch-electrolytes:summary

Na+ (630 nm, chromogen complex), K+ (578 nm, turbidity with tetraphenylborate), Cl- (500 nm, mercuric thiocyanate displacement producing red Fe-SCN complex). All use Beer-Lambert ratio calculation. Na+ 135 to 145, K+ 3.5 to 5.0, Cl- 98 to 106 mmol/L.

## bch-creatinine

# Determination of Urinary Creatinine (Jaffe Reaction)

## Aim
To determine the concentration of creatinine in urine using the Jaffe colorimetric method.

## Principle
**Jaffe Reaction:** Creatinine reacts with alkaline picrate (picric acid + NaOH) to form an orange-red complex (creatinine-picrate complex). The intensity of the color is proportional to creatinine concentration and measured spectrophotometrically.

### Key Points
- Picric acid is yellow in alkaline solution
- After reaction with creatinine it turns orange-red
- Measured at 500 to 520 nm

## Reagents
- Picric acid
- Sodium hydroxide (NaOH)
- Standard creatinine solution
- Distilled water
- Urine sample

## Procedure
1. Prepare blank, standard, and sample tubes
2. Add picric acid and NaOH to all tubes
3. Mix and incubate to allow color to develop
4. Read absorbance against blank

## Calculation
Concentration (sample) = (Absorbance sample / Absorbance standard) x Concentration standard

## Clinical Significance
- Creatinine is a breakdown product of creatine phosphate in muscle
- Freely filtered by glomerulus, used as marker of GFR (glomerular filtration rate)
- **High urine creatinine:** Normal renal function
- **Low urine creatinine:** Impaired kidney function, muscle wasting
- Used in creatinine clearance calculations

## bch-creatinine:summary

Jaffe reaction: creatinine plus alkaline picrate produces orange-red complex. Measured at 500 to 520 nm. Creatinine is a muscle breakdown product, filtered by glomerulus. Marker of GFR. Low = impaired kidney function.

## bch-albumin

# Estimation of Albumin (Dye Binding Method)

## Aim
To estimate albumin concentration in a serum sample using the dye binding spectrophotometric method.

## Principle
Albumin binds specific dyes (e.g., bromocresol green) causing a color change. The intensity of the color is proportional to albumin concentration, measured at 630 nm.

### Why Dye Binding?
- Albumin has the strongest dye-binding capacity among serum proteins
- Relatively specific for albumin
- Simple, rapid, and suitable for clinical labs

## Reagents
- Standard albumin solution
- Normal saline
- Serum sample
- Dye reagent

## Procedure
1. Prepare blank, standard, and sample tubes
2. Add dye reagent to all tubes
3. Heat if required by protocol
4. Read absorbance at 630 nm against blank

## Calculation
Concentration (sample) = (Absorbance sample / Absorbance standard) x Concentration standard

## Normal Range
- Adults: 35 to 50 g/L (3.5 to 5.0 g/dL)

## Clinical Significance
- **Low albumin:** Liver disease (reduced synthesis), nephrotic syndrome (loss in urine), malnutrition, burns, inflammation
- **High albumin:** Dehydration (hemoconcentration)
- Albumin maintains oncotic pressure; low levels cause edema

## bch-albumin:summary

Albumin estimation via dye binding method. Albumin plus dye produces color change at 630 nm. Normal: 35 to 50 g/L. Low = liver disease, nephrotic syndrome, malnutrition. High = dehydration.

## bch-ogtt

# Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT) and Glucose Oxidase Method

## Aim
To determine glucose concentration in blood plasma using the glucose oxidase enzymatic method.

## Principle

### Glucose Oxidase Reaction
Glucose + O2 + H2O, with glucose oxidase, produces gluconic acid + H2O2

### Color Development
H2O2 + Chromogen, with peroxidase, produces quinoneimine (pink/rose-pink color)

- Intensity proportional to glucose concentration
- Measured spectrophotometrically

### Why Glucose Oxidase?
- Specific for beta-D-glucose, highly accurate
- Does not react with other sugars (fructose, galactose)

## OGTT Procedure
1. Fasting blood sample (0 min)
2. Patient drinks 75g glucose solution
3. Blood samples at 30, 60, 90, 120 min
4. Plot glucose curve

## Normal OGTT Values
- Fasting: less than 5.6 mmol/L
- 2 hours: less than 7.8 mmol/L

## Interpretation
- **Diabetes:** Fasting greater than or equal to 7.0 mmol/L OR 2-hour greater than or equal to 11.1 mmol/L
- **Impaired glucose tolerance:** 2-hour value 7.8 to 11.0 mmol/L

## Clinical Significance
- Screens for diabetes mellitus and impaired glucose tolerance
- Glucose oxidase method is the clinical gold standard for blood glucose
- OGTT curve shape helps differentiate diabetes types

## bch-ogtt:summary

Glucose oxidase method: glucose produces gluconic acid and H2O2, which with peroxidase produces pink quinoneimine. Specific for beta-D-glucose. OGTT: fasting less than 5.6, 2-hour less than 7.8 mmol/L. Fasting greater than or equal to 7.0 or 2-hour greater than or equal to 11.1 = diabetes.

## bch-uric-acid

# Estimation of Plasma Uric Acid (Phosphotungstic Acid Method)

## Aim
To estimate the concentration of plasma uric acid using the phosphotungstic acid colorimetric method.

## Principle
Uric acid reduces phosphotungstic acid in an alkaline medium to produce tungsten blue (blue color). Measured at 700 nm.

### Key Points
- Uric acid acts as a reducing agent
- Phosphotungstic acid is the oxidizing agent
- Alkaline medium required for the reaction
- Blue color intensity is proportional to uric acid concentration

## Reagents
- Plasma sample
- Standard uric acid solution
- Uric acid reagent (phosphotungstic acid)
- Cyanide (alkaline medium)
- Distilled water

## Procedure
1. Prepare blank, standard, and sample tubes
2. Add uric acid reagent to all tubes
3. Mix and incubate
4. Read absorbance at 700 nm against blank

## Calculation
Concentration (sample) = (Absorbance sample / Absorbance standard) x Concentration standard

## Normal Range
- Males: 210 to 420 micromol/L (3.5 to 7.0 mg/dL)
- Females: 150 to 350 micromol/L (2.5 to 6.0 mg/dL)

## Clinical Significance
- **Hyperuricemia:** Gout, renal failure, cell lysis (leukemia), high-purine diet
- **Hypouricemia:** Liver disease, SIADH, Fanconi syndrome
- Uric acid is the end product of purine metabolism in humans

## bch-uric-acid:summary

Phosphotungstic acid method: uric acid reduces phosphotungstic acid to produce tungsten blue at 700 nm. Normal: males 210 to 420, females 150 to 350 micromol/L. High = gout, renal failure. End product of purine metabolism.

## bch-transamination

# Transamination Reaction

## Aim
To understand the action of ALT (Aminotransferase) in reactions between amino acids (Alanine, Glutamate) and keto acids (alpha-ketoglutarate, Pyruvate).

## Principle
**Transamination:** Transfer of an amino group (-NH2) from an amino acid to a keto acid, catalyzed by aminotransferases (transaminases).

### The Reaction
Alanine + alpha-ketoglutarate, with ALT, produces Pyruvate + Glutamate

### Key Points
- Requires pyridoxal phosphate (PLP) as coenzyme (vitamin B6)
- Reversible reaction
- ALT = Alanine aminotransferase (liver-specific marker)
- AST = Aspartate aminotransferase (liver + heart + muscle)

## Detection
- Paper chromatography with Ninhydrin spray
- Amino acids appear as purple spots
- Keto acids do not produce color with ninhydrin

## Clinical Significance
- **ALT elevation:** Liver damage (hepatitis, cirrhosis, drug toxicity)
- **AST elevation:** Myocardial infarction, liver damage, muscle injury
- ALT/AST ratio helps differentiate liver disease types
- Transamination is a key step in amino acid metabolism and gluconeogenesis

## bch-transamination:summary

Transamination: amino group transfer from amino acid to keto acid via ALT or AST. Requires PLP (B6). Alanine plus alpha-ketoglutarate produces pyruvate plus glutamate. ALT = liver marker. AST = liver, heart, muscle. Reversible reaction.

## bch-alkaline-phosphatase

# Enzyme Kinetics: Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP)

## Aim
To study the kinetics of alkaline phosphatase under varying conditions (temperature, substrate concentration, pH, inhibitors).

## Principle
ALP catalyzes the hydrolysis of PNPP (p-nitrophenyl phosphate):

**PNPP + H2O, with ALP, produces PNP (yellow) + Pi**

- PNP (p-nitrophenol) is yellow at alkaline pH
- Measured at 405 nm
- Rate of color development equals enzyme activity

## Sub-Experiments

### 1. Effect of Temperature
- Activity increases with temperature up to optimum (approximately 37 degrees C)
- Above optimum: enzyme denatures, activity drops

### 2. Effect of Substrate Concentration
- Activity increases with substrate concentration until Vmax
- At Vmax: all enzyme active sites are saturated
- Follows Michaelis-Menten kinetics

### 3. Effect of pH
- ALP has optimal pH of 9.8 to 10.0 (alkaline range)
- Activity drops at acidic or very alkaline pH

### 4. Lineweaver-Burk Plot
- Double reciprocal plot: 1/V vs 1/[S]
- x-intercept = -1/Km
- y-intercept = 1/Vmax
- Km = substrate concentration at half Vmax (measure of affinity)

### 5. Effect of Inhibitors
- **Inorganic phosphate (Pi):** Competitive inhibitor, increases Km, Vmax unchanged
- **Mg2+:** Divalent cation required for ALP activity, acts as cofactor

## Reagents
- PNPP solution (substrate)
- ALP enzyme extract
- Carbonate buffer (pH 9.8 to 10.0)
- NaOH (stop solution)

## Clinical Significance
- **High ALP:** Bone disease (rickets, Paget's), liver disease (cholestasis), pregnancy
- **Low ALP:** Hypophosphatasia (genetic deficiency)
- ALP is a marker for both bone and liver pathology
- Used to differentiate obstructive jaundice from hepatocellular disease

## bch-alkaline-phosphatase:summary

ALP hydrolyzes PNPP to produce yellow PNP at 405 nm. Optimum: 37 degrees C, pH 9.8 to 10.0. Michaelis-Menten kinetics. Lineweaver-Burk: 1/V vs 1/[S]. Pi = competitive inhibitor. Mg2+ = cofactor. High = bone disease, cholestasis. Low = hypophosphatasia.
