# Ventilation-Perfusion Ratio (V/Q Ratio)

## Pulmonary Circulation

### Blood Supply

| Vessel | Origin | Supply |
|---|---|---|
| **Bronchial arteries** | Thoracic aorta | Lung parenchyma, respiratory bronchioles, pleura, nerves, connective tissue. Venous drainage → systemic circulation |
| **Pulmonary arteries** | Right ventricle | Capillary network around alveoli. Venous drainage → pulmonary veins → left atrium |

### Characteristics of Pulmonary Circulation

| Feature | Value/Detail |
|---|---|
| Cardiac output | Same as systemic — **5 L/min** |
| Pressure | Low resistance — **25/10 mmHg** |
| Capillary pressure | **10 mmHg** (below plasma osmotic pressure → prevents fluid exudation into alveoli) |
| Vessel walls | Distensible, virtually no muscle coat → low vascular resistance |
| Blood reservoir | Contains ~**1 L** of blood |

### Regulation of Pulmonary Blood Flow

**Vasoconstriction:**
- Hypoxia (hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction)
- Hypercapnea (↑PCO₂) or ↓pH
- Adrenaline, noradrenaline, angiotensin II
- Thromboxanes, PGF2α

**Vasodilation:**
- ACh, isoprenaline, PGI₂
- Baroreceptor stimulation → slight vasodilation
- Chemoreceptor stimulation → vasoconstriction

## Definition of V/Q Ratio

A measure of the relationship between airflow reaching the alveoli (**ventilation**) and blood flow in surrounding capillaries (**perfusion**).

- **V** = ventilation = airflow to alveoli (~**4 L/min** at rest)
- **Q** = perfusion = blood flow to pulmonary capillaries (~**5 L/min** = cardiac output)

$$V/Q = \frac{\text{Alveolar Ventilation}}{\text{Pulmonary Blood Flow}}$$

### Optimal vs Normal

- **Optimal V/Q ≈ 1.0**: ventilation and perfusion are perfectly matched
- **Average V/Q in lungs ≈ 0.8** (4/5): reflects normal physiological conditions
  - More ventilation in upper lung zones
  - More perfusion in lower lung zones

---

## V/Q Mismatch

An imbalance between airflow and blood flow in the lungs → impaired gas exchange.

### Low V/Q Ratio (< 0.8) — "Shunt"

- Ventilation is **reduced** but perfusion continues
- Deoxygenated blood passes through lungs **without picking up oxygen**
- Blood bypasses ventilated alveoli → **shunt effect**

**Causes:**
- Pneumonia (fluid in alveoli)
- Asthma (bronchoconstriction)
- Mucus plugs
- Atelectasis (alveolar collapse)
- Pulmonary edema

### High V/Q Ratio (> 0.8) — "Dead Space"

- Ventilation is normal but **perfusion is blocked**
- Air enters alveoli but no blood is there to absorb O₂
- Creates **dead space** ventilation

**Causes:**
- Pulmonary embolism (blood clot blocks flow)
- Emphysema (destruction of capillary bed)

---

## West Zones of the Lung

The V/Q ratio varies from apex to base of the lung due to gravitational effects on blood flow and ventilation:

| Zone | Location | Alveolar Pressure vs Capillary Pressure | V/Q Ratio | Description |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Zone 1** | Apex (top) | PA > Pa > Pv | Highest (>1.0) | Alveolar pressure exceeds arterial pressure → capillaries compressed → **dead space** ventilation |
| **Zone 2** | Middle | Pa > PA > Pv | ~1.0 | Intermittent blood flow during systole (waterfall effect) |
| **Zone 3** | Base (bottom) | Pa > Pv > PA | Lowest (<1.0) | Both arterial and venous pressure exceed alveolar pressure → capillaries fully open → **maximum perfusion** |
| **Zone 4** | Very base (supine) | Pa > Pi > Pv | Very low | Interstitial pressure exceeds venous pressure → compression of dependent vessels |

### Key Points

- **More ventilation** in upper zones (apex)
- **More perfusion** in lower zones (base) due to gravity
- Normal V/Q ratio of 0.8 represents the **weighted average** across all zones

## Clinical Significance

### Common Causes of V/Q Mismatch

| Condition | Type of Mismatch | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Pulmonary embolism** | High V/Q (dead space) | Blood clot blocks perfusion; ventilation normal |
| **Pneumonia** | Low V/Q (shunt) | Fluid/inflammation blocks ventilation; perfusion normal |
| **COPD** | Mixed V/Q mismatch | Chronic airway obstruction → some areas over-perfused relative to ventilation |
| **Asthma** | Low V/Q (shunt) | Bronchoconstriction reduces airflow in affected regions |
| **Pulmonary fibrosis** | High V/Q | Thickened membrane impairs diffusion |
| **Pulmonary edema** | Low V/Q | Fluid in alveoli reduces ventilation |
| **ARDS** | Low V/Q (shunt) | Widespread alveolar collapse and fluid accumulation |
| **Atelectasis** | Low V/Q (shunt) | Alveolar collapse eliminates ventilation |
| **Pulmonary hypertension** | High V/Q | Vascular remodeling reduces perfusion |

### Clinical Manifestations

- **Hypoxemia** (low blood O₂) is the primary consequence
- May also see **hypercapnia** (↑CO₂) in severe cases
- V/Q mismatch is the **most common cause of hypoxemia** in clinical practice
- Diagnosed by measuring A-a gradient (alveolar-arterial O₂ difference)

### Compensation

- Hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction diverts blood away from poorly ventilated areas
- Bronchodilation increases airflow to under-ventilated regions
- Chronic V/Q mismatch may lead to pulmonary hypertension and cor pulmonale
