# Body Fluids

## Overview

The maintenance of a relatively constant volume and stable composition of body fluids is essential for homeostasis. The kidney (renal system) is a major player in the maintenance of water balance and composition of ECF.

- **Fluid intake** must match **water output** to prevent body fluid volumes from increasing or decreasing
- Water is added via fluid ingestion/food (~2100 ml/day) and synthesis from oxidation of carbohydrates (~200 ml/day)
- Daily water loss:
  - **Evaporation** (respiratory tract and skin — insensible water loss): ~700 ml/day
  - **Sweat**: 100 ml/day to 1–2 L during exercise
  - **Feces**: ~100 ml/day (can increase to several liters/day in severe diarrhea)
  - **Urine (Kidneys)**: the most important route for fluid and electrolyte balance (0.5 L/day in dehydrated to 20 L/day in hyperhydrated person)

## Body Fluid Compartments

An average 70 kg man has about **42 L** of water (60% of body weight). Depends on age, gender, and obesity.

- **Females**: ~50% body weight (more body fat)
- **Premature/newborn**: 70–75%

### Two Major Compartments

| Compartment | Approximate Volume | % Body Weight | Location |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ICF** (Intracellular Fluid) | ~28 L | ~40% | Inside 100 trillion cells |
| **ECF** (Extracellular Fluid) | ~14 L | ~20% | Outside cells |

### ECF Sub-compartments

| Sub-compartment | Volume | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Interstitial fluid** | ~11 L | 3/4 of ECF |
| **Blood plasma** | ~3 L | 1/4 of ECF |
| **Transcellular fluid** | Variable | Synovial, peritoneal, pericardial, intraocular, CSF |

### ICF vs ECF Composition

- **ICF** is low in Na+ and Cl−; high in K+, phosphate, Mg2+, sulfate; large protein content (~4× plasma)
- **ECF** is high in Na+ and Cl−; low in K+
- Plasma and interstitial fluid have the **same composition** except for **proteins** (higher in plasma)

### Fluid Exchange Mechanisms

- ECF distribution between plasma and interstitium: determined by **hydrostatic and colloid osmotic forces** across capillary membranes
- ICF vs ECF distribution: determined by **osmotic effect of electrolytes** (especially Na+, Cl−) across the cell membrane
- Cell membranes are highly permeable to water but relatively impermeable to Na+ and Cl−
- Water moves rapidly across cell membranes → ICF remains **isotonic** with ECF

## Measurement of Body Fluid Compartments

All compartments are measured using the **indicator dilution method** (dye dilution):

$$V = \frac{\text{Amount of substance injected}}{\text{Concentration after mixing}}$$

### Characteristics of Marker Substances

- Non-toxic
- Mixes thoroughly in the shortest time
- Not excreted rapidly
- Excreted completely within reasonable time
- Does not change color or volume of body fluid

### Specific Measurements

| Compartment | Marker Substances |
|---|---|
| **Total Body Water** | Tritium (³H₂O), Deuterium (²H₂O) — mix with all compartments within hours |
| **ECF Volume** | Radioactive Na+, Cl−, iothalamate, thiosulfate, inulin |
| **Plasma Volume** | ¹²⁵I-albumin, Evans blue dye (T-1824) |
| **ICF Volume** | Total body water − ECF volume (calculated, not measured directly) |
| **Interstitial Fluid** | ECF volume − Plasma volume (calculated) |
| **Blood Volume** | Plasma volume / (1 − Hematocrit) |

## Dehydration

**Excessive loss of water from the body.** Minimum intake ~1 L/day; active individuals need 2–3 L/day.

### By Severity

| Type | Fluid Lost | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | ~5% of body fluid | Rehydration |
| **Moderate** | ~10% of body fluid | Rehydration |
| **Severe** | ~15% of body fluid | Hospitalization & emergency treatment |

### By Solute-to-Solvent Ratio

| Type | Mechanism | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Isotonic** | Balanced loss of Na+ and water | Diarrhea, vomiting |
| **Hypotonic** | Loss of more Na+ than water | Excess diuretic use |
| **Hypertonic** | Loss of more water than Na+ | Fever |

### Signs and Symptoms

**Mild/Moderate:**
- Dry mouth, excess thirst
- Decreased sweating and urine formation
- Headache, dizziness, weakness
- Leg and arm cramps

**Severe:**
- Decreased blood volume and cardiac output
- Low blood pressure
- Hypovolemic cardiac shock
- Fainting

**Very Severe:**
- Organ damage (brain, liver, kidneys)
- Mental depression and confusion
- Renal failure, convulsions, coma

## Water Intoxication (Overhydration)

Condition characterized by a great increase in body water content. Also called **hyperhydration** or **water poisoning**.

### Causes

- **Heart failure** — heart cannot pump blood properly
- **Renal disorders** — fails to excrete enough H₂O
- **Hypersecretion of ADH** (e.g. SIADH)
- **IV administration** of excessive fluids/medications
- Infants at higher risk in first month of life

### Signs and Symptoms

- Behavioral changes (brain is most vulnerable)
- Drowsiness, inattention, nausea, vomiting
- Sudden weight loss, weakness, blurred vision
- Anemia, cyanosis, hemorrhage, shock
- Muscular weakness, cramps, twitching, paralysis
- **Severe**: delirium, seizures, coma

### Treatment

- **Mild**: fluid restriction
- **Severe**: diuretics, ADH receptor antagonists, IV saline to restore Na+

---

## Clinical Applications

- Kidneys adjust excretion of water and electrolytes to match intake, and compensate for excessive losses in disease
- Sodium intake varies from 20 mEq/day to 300–500 mEq/day
- Body fluid balance is maintained through coordinated renal, hormonal, and behavioral mechanisms
