# Renal Physiology

## Kidney Functions Overview

The renal system (kidneys, ureters, bladder, urethra) filters ~200 L of fluid per day from renal blood flow.

### Primary Functions

- **Excretion** of metabolic waste products (urea, creatinine, uric acid, bilirubin, toxins)
- **Regulation** of water and electrolyte balance
- **Regulation** of body fluid osmolarity and electrolyte concentration
- **Blood pressure** regulation
- **Acid-base balance** regulation
- **Hormone** secretion, metabolism, and excretion (renin, erythropoietin, thrombopoietin, calcitriol)
- **Gluconeogenesis**

---

## Anatomical Structure

### Gross Anatomy

- Bean-shaped organ, located retroperitoneally in upper posterior abdomen (T12–L3)
- Left kidney slightly higher than right (liver displacement)
- Each kidney: ~150 g, size of a clenched fist
- Hilum: indentation on medial side where renal artery, vein, lymphatics, nerves, and ureter enter/exit

### Layers of the Kidney

| Layer | Description |
|---|---|
| **Cortex** | Outermost, dark granular appearance. Contains renal corpuscles and convoluted tubules. Cortical tissue penetrates medulla as renal columns (columns of Bertini) |
| **Medulla** | Inner layer, tubular/vascular structures arranged radially in 8–18 medullary (Malpighian) pyramids |
| **Renal Sinus** | Contains renal pelvis, major/minor calyces, nerves, arteries, veins, connective tissue, fat |

### Renal Circulation

- Kidneys receive **22% of total cardiac output** (~1100 ml/min)
- Unique **dual capillary bed** arranged in series:

```
Renal artery → Interlobar → Arcuate → Interlobular → Afferent arteriole
    → Glomerular capillaries (high pressure ~60 mmHg — favors filtration)
    → Efferent arteriole
    → Peritubular capillaries (low pressure ~13 mmHg — favors reabsorption)
    → Interlobular vein → Arcuate → Interlobar → Renal vein
```

- Afferent arteriole resistance > efferent arteriole resistance
- Kidneys regulate pressure in both capillary beds by adjusting afferent/efferent arteriole resistance

## Nephron Structure

The **nephron** is the functional unit of the kidney. Each kidney contains **800,000–1,000,000 nephrons**. Kidneys cannot regenerate nephrons; after age 40, nephron number decreases ~10% per decade.

### Components of a Nephron

1. **Renal corpuscle** (glomerulus + Bowman's capsule)
2. **Tubular portion** (PCT → Loop of Henle → DCT → Collecting duct)

### Nephron Types

| Type | % | Location | Loop of Henle | Blood Supply | Function |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Cortical** | 85% | Outer cortex | Short (outer medulla) | Peritubular capillaries | General filtration/reabsorption |
| **Juxtamedullary** | 15% | Corticomedullary junction | Long (tip of papilla) | Vasa recta | Urine concentration |

### Key Features of the Renal Corpuscle

- **Filtration membrane**: Capillary endothelium (fenestrated, pores ~0.1 μm) + fused basement membrane + visceral epithelium (podocytes with pedicles and slit pores)
- Vascular system in glomerulus is **purely arterial**
- Afferent arteriole diameter > efferent arteriole diameter

### Tubular Portion

| Segment | Length/Diameter | Epithelium | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PCT** | 14 mm, 55 μm | Cuboidal with brush border | 88% reabsorption site |
| **Loop of Henle** | Descending + hairpin bend + ascending | Flattened (no brush border) | Countercurrent mechanism |
| **DCT** | — | Cuboidal without brush border (intercalated cells) | Hormone-regulated reabsorption |
| **Collecting duct** | Multiple ducts unite → papillary ducts of Bellini → minor calyx → major calyx | — | ADH-dependent water reabsorption |

## Glomerular Filtration (GFR)

The first step in urine formation. Large amounts of fluid are filtered from glomerular capillaries into Bowman's capsule.

- **Glomerular filtrate**: similar to plasma but devoid of proteins, cellular elements, Ca2+, and fatty acids
- **Average GFR**: ~125 ml/min or **180 L/day**
- **Filtration fraction**: ~20% of plasma filtered

$$\text{Filtration Fraction} = \frac{\text{GFR}}{\text{Renal Plasma Flow}} \times 100$$

### Net Filtration Pressure (NFP)

Based on the **Starling hypothesis**:

| Force | Value (mmHg) | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Glomerular hydrostatic pressure (GHP) | 60 | Favors filtration |
| Bowman's capsule COP | 0 | — |
| Bowman's capsule hydrostatic pressure | 15 | Opposes filtration |
| Glomerular capillary COP | 25 | Opposes filtration |

$$\text{NFP} = 60 - 25 - 15 = 20 \text{ mmHg}$$

### Factors Affecting GFR

**Directly proportional to GFR:**
- Renal blood flow (~1100–1300 ml/min)
- Surface area of capillary membrane
- Permeability of capillary membrane

**Inversely proportional to GFR:**
- Colloid osmotic pressure (increased in dehydration/hyperproteinemia → ↓GFR)
- Bowman's capsule hydrostatic pressure (increased in urethral obstruction/edema → ↓GFR)

**Autoregulation range**: BP 80–180 mmHg (GFR unaffected outside this range)

**Autoregulation mechanisms:**
- **Myogenic response**: afferent arteriole constricts with ↑BP, dilates with ↓BP
- **Tubuloglomerular feedback (TGF)**: macula densa senses Na+/Cl− concentration
  - ↑Na+/Cl− → adenosine release → afferent vasoconstriction
  - ↓Na+/Cl− → renin, bradykinin, PGE2 release → vasodilation + efferent constriction via angiotensin II

**Sympathetic stimulation**: Strong stimulation causes vasoconstriction (more on efferent arteriole). Recovery after ~30 minutes due to reduction in neurotransmitter.

**Hormonal factors:**

| Increase GFR (vasodilation) | Decrease GFR (vasoconstriction) |
|---|---|
| ANP, BNP, cAMP | Angiotensin II, Endothelins |
| Dopamine, NO, PGE2 | Noradrenaline, PGF2α, Platelet activating factor |

### Tubular Reabsorption (Transcellular and Paracellular Routes)

**Transcellular**: Lumen → tubular cell (apical surface) → interstitial fluid → capillary
**Paracellular**: Lumen → interstitial fluid (through tight junctions) → capillary

### Substances Reabsorbed by Segment

| Segment | Substances | % Reabsorption |
|---|---|---|
| **PCT** | Glucose, amino acids, Na+, K+, Ca2+, Cl−, phosphate, bicarbonate, water, urea, uric acid | 88% |
| **Loop of Henle** | Na+, Cl− | — |
| **DCT** | Na+, Ca2+, bicarbonate, water | — |

### Transport Maximum (Tm) and Renal Threshold

- **Tm**: maximum rate at which a substance can be reabsorbed
  - Tm for glucose (TmG) = 375 mg/min (males), 300 mg/min (females)
- **Renal threshold**: plasma concentration at which a substance appears in urine
  - Glucose threshold = **180 mg/dL**

## Tubular Secretion

Substances transported from blood into renal tubules (tubular excretion):

- **K+**: actively secreted by Na+-K+ pump in PCT, DCT, and collecting ducts
- **NH3**: secreted in PCT
- **H+**: secreted in PCT and DCT (maximum in PCT)
- **Urea**: secreted in Loop of Henle

---

## Countercurrent Mechanism

Enables the kidney to concentrate urine. Involves the Loop of Henle and vasa recta working together.

### Countercurrent Multiplier (Loop of Henle)

- Develops the **medullary osmotic gradient** (300 mOsm/L at corticomedullary junction → 1200 mOsm/L at inner medulla)
- **Thick ascending limb**: actively transports NaCl out → increases medullary interstitial osmolarity
- **Descending limb**: water moves out into hyperosmotic medulla → filtrate becomes more concentrated
- NaCl is **repeatedly recirculated** between descending and ascending limbs

**Urea recycling:**
- 50% of filtered urea reabsorbed in PCT
- Equal amount secreted in Loop of Henle
- Collecting duct impermeable to urea, but ADH-mediated water reabsorption concentrates urea → urea diffuses into inner medullary interstitium (via UT-A1/UT-A3)
- Urea enters ascending limb → returns to collecting duct → recirculates

### Countercurrent Exchanger (Vasa Recta)

- Maintains the medullary gradient developed by the multiplier
- Runs parallel to Loop of Henle in hairpin configuration
- Blood flows slowly → NaCl accumulates in descending limb, diffuses out in ascending limb
- Water simultaneously enters ascending limb
- **Exchanges NaCl and urea for water** between ascending and descending limbs

### Urine Concentration and Dilution

- **Dilute urine**: body water excess → ADH inhibited → dilute urine excreted
- **Concentrated urine**: water deficit → ADH released → water reabsorbed from DCT and collecting duct
- Normal filtrate osmolarity: 300 mOsm/L; concentrated urine: up to **1200 mOsm/L**

## Acid-Base Balance

Urine is acidic (pH 4.5–6). Kidneys maintain acid-base balance by excreting H+ and retaining HCO3−.

### Three Mechanisms

#### 1. Bicarbonate Mechanism

- All filtered HCO3− is reabsorbed (80% PCT, 15% Loop of Henle, 5% DCT/collecting duct)
- H+ secreted via Na+/H+ antiport and ATP-driven proton pump (DCT/collecting duct)

#### 2. Phosphate Mechanism

- H+ secreted in exchange for Na+
- Na+ reabsorbed under influence of aldosterone → combines with HCO3− in interstitium
- H+ reacts with NaHPO4 → forms NaH2PO4 → excreted in urine
- Occurs mainly in DCT and collecting duct

#### 3. Ammonia Mechanism

- **Most important** mechanism for H+ excretion and urine acidification
- Glutamine → glutamic acid (via glutaminase) → NH3
- NH3 secreted into lumen → combines with H+ → NH4+ (ammonium)
- NH4+ cannot cross membrane → trapped in lumen → excreted in urine
- Occurs mainly in PCT

## Micturition Reflex

Micturition (urination) is the reflex process of expelling urine from the bladder.

### Anatomy

- **Detrusor muscle**: 3 layers of smooth muscle in bladder wall
- **Internal urethral sphincter**: smooth muscle at bladder neck, involuntary
- **External urethral sphincter**: skeletal muscle, voluntary (pudendal nerve S2–S4)
- Normal bladder capacity: **300–500 ml** for 2–5 hours

### Mechanism

1. **Filling phase**: Sympathetic nerve (hypogastric, L1–L2) keeps detrusor relaxed and internal sphincter constricted → "nerve of filling"
2. **At ~250 ml**: stretch receptors stimulated → parasympathetic fibers (pelvic nerve S1–S3) activated → "nerve of emptying"
3. Signal relayed to **pontine micturition center (PMC)** in pons
4. PMC receives input from higher brain centers for voluntary control
5. **If voiding**: parasympathetic → detrusor contracts + internal sphincter relaxes; somatic → pudendal nerve inhibited → external sphincter relaxes → urine expelled

### Pathophysiology

| Condition | Mechanism | Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Atonic bladder** | Loss of sensory pelvic nerve fibers | Bladder overfills, overflow dribbling (overflow incontinence). E.g. spinal injury, syphilis |
| **Automatic bladder** | Hyperactive reflex, loss of voluntary control | Small amount of urine triggers reflex emptying. Recovery stage from spinal cord transection |
| **Uninhibited neurogenic bladder** | Midbrain lesion | Frequent, uncontrollable micturition. Even small volumes trigger reflex |
| **Nocturnal enuresis** | Incomplete myelination of bladder motor nerves | Bedwetting, normal in children <3 years; resolves with myelination |

## Diuretics

Substances that enhance urine formation and output by increasing excretion of water, Na+, and Cl−.

### Clinical Uses

- Hypertension
- Congestive cardiac failure
- Edema

### Types

| Type | Mechanism | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Osmotic diuretics** | Increase osmotic pressure in tubular fluid → ↓water reabsorption | Mannitol, urea, sucrose, glucose |
| **Loop diuretics** | Inhibit active reabsorption of Na+/Cl− in ascending Loop of Henle | Furosemide, bumetanide |
| **Thiazides** | Inhibit Na+ reabsorption in DCT | Hydrochlorothiazide |
| **K+-sparing diuretics** | Inhibit Na+ reabsorption, spare K+ | Triamterene, amiloride |
| **Aldosterone antagonists** | Block aldosterone action | Spironolactone, eplerenone |
| **Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors** | Inhibit carbonic anhydrase in PCT | Acetazolamide |
| **Xanthines** | Increase GFR, decrease Na+ reabsorption | Caffeine, theophylline |
| **ADH secretion inhibitors** | Block ADH release | Large volumes of water, ethanol |
| **ADH receptor (V2) antagonists** | Block ADH action on collecting duct | Conivaptan |

## Renal Function Tests

### Urinalysis

**Physical examination:**
- **Volume**: increased in chronic renal failure, diabetes insipidus, glycosuria
- **Color**: jaundice, hematuria, hemoglobinuria, medications
- **Appearance**: normally clear; turbid in crystalluria, mucus, blood cells, bacteria
- **Specific gravity**: low in diabetes insipidus; high in diabetes mellitus, acute renal failure
- **Osmolarity**: measured with osmometer; decreases in diabetes insipidus
- **pH**: depends on diet; alkaline in vegetarians, acidic in non-vegetarians

**Microscopic examination (urine sediment):**

| Finding | Significance |
|---|---|
| RBCs | Glomerulonephritis |
| WBCs | UTI, pyelonephritis |
| Epithelial cells | Nephrotic syndrome, tubular necrosis |
| Hyaline casts | Dehydration, strenuous exercise, diuretic use |
| RBC casts | Glomerulonephritis |
| WBC casts | Pyelonephritis |
| Granular casts | Chronic/acute kidney disease |
| Waxy casts | Severe chronic kidney disease |

**Chemical analysis:**
- **Glucose** (>180 mg/dL): glycosuria → diabetes mellitus
- **Protein** (albumin >30 mg/day): proteinuria → glomerulonephritis
- **Ketone bodies**: diabetes mellitus, pregnancy, starvation
- **Bilirubin**: hepatic/posthepatic jaundice
- **Blood (hematuria)**: glomerulonephritis, stones, infection, malignancy

### Blood Examination

| Parameter | Normal Value |
|---|---|
| Total proteins | 7.3 g/dL |
| Serum albumin | 4.7 g/dL |
| Serum globulin | 2.3 g/dL |
| Fibrinogen | 0.3 g/dL |
| Urea | 25–40 mg/dL |
| Uric acid | 2.5 mg/dL |
| Creatinine | 0.5–1.5 mg/dL |

### Plasma Clearance (Renal Clearance)

$$Cl = \frac{U \times V}{P}$$

Where U = urine concentration, V = urine flow rate, P = plasma concentration

| Measurement | Marker Substance | Normal Value |
|---|---|---|
| **GFR** | Inulin (ideal) or creatinine | ~125 ml/min |
| **Renal plasma flow** | PAH (para-aminohippuric acid) | ~660 ml/min |
| **Renal blood flow** | Calculated from RPF and hematocrit | ~1200 ml/min |

$$\text{Renal Blood Flow} = \frac{\text{Renal Plasma Flow}}{\% \text{ Plasma in Blood}}$$

- **Urea clearance test**: normal value ~70 ml/min; assesses renal function
