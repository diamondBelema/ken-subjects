# Control of Respiration (RESP Vb)

## Overview

The nervous system regulates respiration to maintain adequate O₂, CO₂, and pH in blood via a **negative feedback system**.

### Respiratory Control System Components

| Component | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Sensors** | Chemoreceptors (peripheral and central), lung stretch receptors, proprioceptors |
| **Central Controller** | Nuclei in medulla oblongata, pons, and other brain regions |
| **Effectors** | Respiratory muscles (diaphragm, intercostals, abdominals) |

---

## Neural Control of Respiration

Two distinct mechanisms:

### 1. Voluntary Control
- Controlled by the **cerebral cortex**
- E.g. breath holding
- Impulses sent to respiratory motor neurons via the **corticospinal tract**

### 2. Automatic Control
- Controlled by **medulla oblongata and pons**
- Produces automatic, rhythmic respiration
- Receives, evaluates, and emits signals to respiratory muscles

---

## Medullary Respiratory Centers

### Dorsal Respiratory Group (DRG)

- Located within the **nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS)**
- Sensory termination of cranial nerves IX and X
- Contains **inspiratory neurons**
- Generates the **basic respiratory rhythm**
- Sends impulses to inspiratory muscles in a **ramp fashion** (not instantaneous)
- Inspiration time > expiration time

#### Ramp Pattern and Breathing Rate

- During inspiration, ramp increases rapidly → lungs fill
- The earlier the ramp ceases → shorter inspiration → **faster respiration**
- **Inspiratory phase**: abrupt increase in discharge frequency → ramp-like increase in firing rate
- **Off-switch**: abruptly decreases firing rate → ends inspiration
- **Expiratory Phase I**: inspiratory motor neuron activity decreases
- **Expiratory Phase II**: inspiratory activity completely abolished; active expiratory muscle stimulation during exercise

### Ventral Respiratory Group (VRG)

- **Cranial division**: nucleus ambiguus
- **Caudal division**: nucleus retroambigualis
- Functions:
  - Drives **spinal respiratory motorneurons** (intercostal and abdominal muscles)
  - Drives **auxiliary muscles of respiration** innervated by the vagus

---

## Pontine Respiratory Centers

### Pneumotaxic Center

- Located in **upper pons** (nucleus parabrachialis)
- **Prevents apneusis** (arrest of respiration at inspiration)
- **Increases rate** of respiration

### Apneustic Center

- Located in **lower pons**
- Tonically active
- Responsible for **apneusis** (prolonged inspiration)
- **Inhibited by the pneumotaxic center**

### Role of Pontine Centers

To make the rhythmic discharge of medullary neurons **smooth and regular**

---

## Descending Pathways

- Axons from cortex, DRG, VRG, and pontine centers descend in white matter
- Influence phrenic, intercostal, and abdominal motorneurons
- From cortex: lateral corticospinal or cortico-rubro-spinal tracts
- From DRG/VRG: cross midline, descend in ventro-lateral columns of cord

## Chemical Control of Respiration

### Stimuli

- **Decreased PO₂**
- **Increased PCO₂**
- **Decreased pH**

### Peripheral Chemoreceptors

#### Carotid Bodies

- Located at **bifurcation of common carotid artery**
- Afferent/efferent neurons via **carotid sinus nerve** and **CN IX (glossopharyngeal)** → medullary respiratory center
- Size: ~10 mg, ~4 mm long
- Blood flow: ~2000 ml/min/100 g tissue (extremely high)
- Due to enormous blood flow, O₂ needs met largely by **dissolved O₂ alone**

#### Aortic Bodies

- Located mainly at **arch of aorta**
- Afferent/efferent neurons via **aortic nerves** and **CN X (vagus)** → medullary respiratory center

#### Histology (Both Bodies)

- **Type I (glomus) cells**: epithelioid, 10–15 μm, contain dense core vesicles with dopamine, noradrenaline, adrenaline, 5-HT, ACh, ATP, K+, Ca2+, Na+
- **Type II (sustentacular) cells**: surround Type I cells
- Nerve fibers between Type I and Type II cells

#### Stimuli for Peripheral Chemoreceptors

| Stimulates | Does NOT Stimulate |
|---|---|
| ↓Arterial PO₂ | Anemia |
| ↓O₂ delivery per unit time (stasis) | CO poisoning |
| Cyanide (prevents O₂ utilization) | |
| Nicotine, lobeline (in sufficient doses) | |

- Peripheral chemoreceptors are **not stimulated** by anemia or CO poisoning because dissolved O₂ reaching receptors is normal

### Central Chemoreceptors (Chemosensitive Areas of Medulla)

- Specialized cell groups in the **ventro-lateral surface of the medulla**
- Sensitive to the **ECF around them**
- ECF is in contact with **cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)**

#### Mechanism

1. CO₂ diffuses rapidly across the **blood-brain barrier**
2. PCO₂ in CSF parallels arterial PCO₂
3. In CSF: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → HCO₃⁻ + H⁺
4. **H⁺** stimulates chemosensitive cells → excites respiratory center
5. Stimulatory effect is greatest in first few hours, declines over 1–2 days as renal readjustment normalizes [H⁺]

#### Key Points

- CSF is a protein-free fluid secreted by choroid plexus
- Blood-brain barrier has **low ionic permeability** — H⁺ cannot cross directly
- CO₂ crosses easily → converts to H⁺ on the brain side

---

## Response to Hypercapnea and Hypoxia

### Response to ↑CO₂ (Hypercapnea)

- ↑PCO₂ → ↑CO₂ crosses BBB → ↑H⁺ in CSF → stimulates central chemoreceptors → **↑ventilation**
- Also stimulates peripheral chemoreceptors → further ↑ventilation
- Most powerful stimulus for ventilation under normal conditions

### Response to ↓O₂ (Hypoxia)

- ↓PO₂ → stimulates **peripheral chemoreceptors** (carotid and aortic bodies) → ↑ventilation
- Central chemoreceptors are **not directly sensitive** to low PO₂
- Hypoxic drive becomes important when PCO₂ is chronically elevated (e.g. COPD)
