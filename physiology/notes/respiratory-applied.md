# Applied Respiratory Physiology (RESP VI, VIa, VIb)

## Hypoxia

**Oxygen deficiency at tissue level.**

### Types of Hypoxia

| Type | Mechanism | Arterial PO₂ | Hb | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Hypoxic hypoxia** | ↓PO₂ in inspired air or impaired O₂ transfer to blood | ↓ | Normal | High altitude, hypoventilation, diffusion block, V/Q mismatch |
| **Anemic hypoxia** | ↓Functional Hb available to carry O₂ | Normal | ↓ or dysfunctional | CO poisoning, severe anemia |
| **Stagnant hypoxia** | ↓Blood flow to tissues despite normal PO₂ and Hb | Normal | Normal | Congestive heart failure, shock |
| **Histotoxic hypoxia** | Tissues cannot utilize delivered O₂ | Normal | Normal | Cyanide poisoning (inhibits cytochrome oxidase) |

### Hypoxic Hypoxia — Causes

- **↓PO₂ in inspired air**: high altitude, breathing O₂-poor gas mixtures
- **Hypoventilation**: airway obstruction, respiratory muscle paralysis, drugs (morphine), ↑airway resistance (asthma, emphysema)
- **Diffusion block**: pulmonary fibrosis, ↓alveolar membrane area (pneumonia)
- **V/Q mismatch**: emphysema, cyanotic congenital heart disease

### Oxygen Cascade

PO₂ decreases at each step from inspired air to mitochondria:

```
Atmospheric air → Tracheal air → Alveolar air → Arterial blood → Tissue cells → Mitochondria
   159 mmHg        150 mmHg      100 mmHg        95 mmHg        40 mmHg        ~5 mmHg
```

## Hypercapnea

**Retention of carbon dioxide in the body.** Occurs when rate of CO₂ formation > removal.

### Effects

- **Respiratory acidosis** (↑PCO₂ → ↓pH)
- Excretion of large amounts of bicarbonate
- Depression of CNS
- Confusion, diminished sensory acuity
- Coma and respiratory depression

### Asphyxia and Drowning

- Both hypoxia and hypercapnea present
- ↑Respiration, BP, HR, catecholamine secretion, ↓pH
- Eventually: BP and HR fail → **cardiac arrest → death**

## High Altitude

### Effects of Reduced Barometric Pressure

| Altitude (ft) | Barometric Pressure (mmHg) | PO₂ in Air (mmHg) |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | 760 | 159 |
| 10,000 | 523 | 110 |
| 20,000 | 349 | 73 |
| 30,000 | 226 | 47 |
| 40,000 | 141 | 29 |
| 50,000 | 87 | 18 |

### Physiological Response

1. ↓Barometric pressure → ↓PO₂
2. Hypoxic stimulation of chemoreceptors
3. **↑Ventilation** (hyperventilation)
4. ↓Alveolar PCO₂ → ↓arterial PCO₂
5. **Respiratory alkalosis**

### Symptoms in Unacclimatized Individuals (at 12,000 ft)

- Drowsiness, lassitude, mental and muscle fatigue
- Headache, nausea, euphoria
- At 18,000–23,000 ft: twitchings/seizures, coma, death

### Acute Mountain Sickness

- Develops 8–24 hrs after arrival at altitude, lasts 4–8 days
- Headache, nausea/vomiting, irritability
- Insomnia, pulmonary edema, cerebral edema
- Breathlessness, decreased mental proficiency
- **Treatment**: O₂, rest, slow ascent, avoid exertion

### Acclimatization Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Effect |
|---|---|
| ↑2,3-DPG | ↑O₂ delivery to tissues |
| ↑Erythropoietin secretion | ↑RBC production |
| ↑Mitochondria | ↑O₂ utilization |
| ↑Myoglobin | ↑O₂ storage in muscle |
| ↑Cytochrome oxidase | ↑O₂ utilization in cells |

### Natural Acclimatization (Highlanders)

- Living at 13,000–17,500 ft, working at 19,000 ft (Andes, Himalayas)
- Acclimatization begins at infancy
- **Increased chest size**, slightly decreased body size → high ventilatory capacity-to-mass ratio
- **Larger hearts** → increased cardiac output → better O₂ delivery

### Work Capacity at Altitude

| Condition | Work Capacity (% normal) at 17,000 ft |
|---|---|
| Unacclimatized | 50% |
| Acclimatized 2 months | 68% |
| Native at 13,200 ft working at 17,000 ft | 87% |

### Complications

- **Acute cerebral edema**: local vasodilation from hypoxia → severe disorientation
- **Acute pulmonary edema**: cause unknown; may be fatal without O₂
- **Chronic mountain sickness** (prolonged exposure):
  - Excessively high RBC mass and hematocrit → ↑blood viscosity → ↓tissue blood flow
  - ↑Pulmonary arterial pressure
  - Right heart enlargement → congestive heart failure
  - Death if not moved to lower altitude (usually recoverable)

## Deep Sea Diving

### Pressure Effects

- Ambient pressure increases by **1 atm per 10 m depth** in seawater (10.4 m in freshwater)
- SCUBA gear reduces hazards

### Problems of Increased Barometric Pressure

- **O₂ toxicity**: lung damage, convulsions
- **N₂ narcosis**: euphoria, impaired performance, tremors, somnolence
- **Decompression sickness** (see below)

### Nitrogen Narcosis

- Caused by increased N₂ partial pressure at depth
- Symptoms: euphoria, impaired judgment, tremors, somnolence
- Similar to alcohol intoxication
- Resolves upon ascent

### Decompression Sickness ("The Bends")

**Caused by rapid ascent from depth:**

- At depth, N₂ dissolves into tissues under high pressure
- If ascent is **too rapid**, N₂ escapes from solution → **bubbles form** in tissues and blood
- Symptoms:
  - Pain in joints
  - Itching
  - Obstruction of cerebral arteries → paralysis and respiratory failure

**Prevention:**
- Slow ascent
- Substitute **He for N₂** in breathing gas (helium is ½ as soluble as N₂, 1/7 molecular weight → diffuses out of tissues faster)

### Oxygen Toxicity

- Occurs at high O₂ partial pressures (prolonged exposure to high PO₂)
- **Central nervous system toxicity**: convulsions (especially at depth)
- **Pulmonary toxicity**: inflammation, edema, atelectasis
- SCUBA divers use gas mixes with controlled O₂ partial pressures

## Exercise and Respiration

### Changes in Ventilation

- **Abrupt increase** at onset of exercise
- **More gradual increase** during sustained exercise
- **Abrupt decrease** when exercise ceases (but does not immediately reach baseline)

#### During Moderate Exercise

- ↑Depth (tidal volume, VT) of respiration
- ↑Rate (f) of respiration as exercise becomes strenuous

#### Abrupt Increase — Causes

- **Psychic stimuli** (anticipation, motivation)
- **Afferents from proprioceptors** in muscles, tendons, and joints

#### Gradual Increase — Causes (Humoral)

- ↑[H+] (questionable)
- ↑Temperature
- ↑Sensitivity of respiratory center to CO₂
- ↑[K+]

### Changes in Tissues During Exercise

- Capillary beds **dilate**; previously closed capillaries **open**
- Mean distance from blood to tissues **decreases** → facilitates O₂ diffusion
- Contracting muscle uses O₂ → **tissue PO₂ falls to nearly zero**
- More O₂ diffuses from blood → blood PO₂ drops → more O₂ dissociates from Hb

### Additional Factors

- ↑Temperature → right shift of O₂-Hb dissociation curve → ↑O₂ unloading
- ↑2,3-DPG → further right shift

### After Exercise

- Abrupt fall in ventilation (not to baseline)
- **O₂ debt** — accumulated H⁺ must be removed before ventilation returns to baseline

### Anaerobic Threshold

- Exercise intensity above which aerobic metabolism is insufficient
- Lactic acid accumulates → metabolic acidosis
- Compensated by hyperventilation (blowing off CO₂)
- Marked by increase in ventilation out of proportion to O₂ consumption

## Fatigue

Poorly understood phenomenon, but likely due to:

- **Acidosis on the brain**
- **Muscle ischemia** during sustained contractions
- **Accumulation of Substance P** (stimulates pain receptors)
- **Accumulation of interstitial fluid** in muscles during exertion
