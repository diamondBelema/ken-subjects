  - id: phy-023
    front: What percentage of cardiac output do the kidneys receive?
    back: Approximately 22% of total cardiac output, which is about 1100 mL/min.
    concept_id: c-ren-001
  - id: phy-024
    front: What makes the renal circulation unique?
    back: "It has two capillary beds arranged in series (glomerular and peritubular), separated by efferent arterioles that regulate hydrostatic pressure in both sets."
  - id: phy-025
    front: What are the hydrostatic pressures in glomerular vs peritubular capillaries?
    back: "'Glomerular capillaries: ~60 mmHg (favors rapid filtration). Peritubular capillaries: ~13 mmHg (favors rapid reabsorption).'"
    concept_id: c-ren-002
  - id: phy-026
    front: What is the functional unit of the kidney and how many are in each kidney?
    back: The nephron. Each kidney contains approximately 800,000 to 1,000,000 nephrons.
    concept_id: c-ren-001
  - id: phy-027
    front: What percentage of nephrons are cortical vs juxtamedullary?
    back: "'Cortical nephrons: 85% (short loop of Henle, peritubular capillaries). Juxtamedullary nephrons: 15% (long loop of Henle, vasa recta, concerned with urine concentration).'"
    concept_id: c-ren-001
  - id: phy-028
    front: What three layers form the filtration membrane in the renal corpuscle?
    back: "1) Glomerular capillary endothelium with fenestrae (0.1 μm pores), 2) Fused basement membrane, 3) Podocytes of Bowman's visceral layer with slit pores between pedicels."
    concept_id: c-ren-002
  - id: phy-029
    front: What is the normal GFR and how much filtrate is produced daily?
    back: GFR = 125 mL/min, which equals 180 L/day of filtrate.
    concept_id: c-ren-008
  - id: phy-030
    front: What is the net filtration pressure (NFP) at the glomerulus?
    back: NFP = GHP(60) - GCCOP(25) - BCHP(15) = 20 mmHg. Based on Starling forces.
    concept_id: c-ren-002
  - id: phy-031
    front: What is the filtration fraction?
    back: "Filtration fraction = GFR / Renal plasma flow × 100 ≈ 20%. About 20% of plasma passing through the kidney is filtered."
    concept_id: c-ren-001
  - id: phy-032
    front: Name factors that increase GFR.
    back: "Atrial natriuretic peptide, brain natriuretic peptide, cAMP, dopamine, nitric oxide, prostaglandin PGE2 (all cause vasodilation)."
    concept_id: c-ren-008
  - id: phy-033
    front: Name factors that decrease GFR.
    back: "Angiotensin II, endothelins, noradrenaline, platelet activating factor, PGF2α (vasoconstriction), sympathetic stimulation, mesangial cell contraction."
    concept_id: c-ren-008
  - id: phy-034
    front: What is the transport maximum (Tm) for glucose?
    back: "TmG = 375 mg/min in males and 300 mg/min in females. The maximum rate at which glucose can be reabsorbed from the tubular fluid."
  - id: phy-035
    front: What is the renal threshold for glucose?
    back: "180 mg/dL. Below this plasma concentration, glucose is completely reabsorbed and does not appear in urine. Above it, glucose appears in urine (glycosuria)."
  - id: phy-036
    front: How is glucose reabsorbed in the PCT?
    back: "Via SGLT2 transporters on the luminal (apical) membrane into tubular cells, then via GLUT2 on the basolateral membrane into the medullary interstitium."
  - id: phy-037
    front: What is the difference between obligatory and facultative water reabsorption?
    back: "'Obligatory: Occurs in PCT, follows Na+ reabsorption regardless of body needs. Facultative: Occurs in DCT and collecting duct, regulated by ADH based on body water status.'"
    concept_id: c-ren-007
  - id: phy-038
    front: What is the countercurrent multiplier and where does it operate?
    back: "The Loop of Henle. It creates the medullary osmotic gradient (300 to 1200 mOsm/L) by actively pumping NaCl from the thick ascending limb into the interstitium while water leaves the descending limb."
    concept_id: c-ren-005
  - id: phy-039
    front: What is the countercurrent exchanger and what is its role?
    back: "The vasa recta. It maintains (does not create) the medullary gradient by exchanging NaCl and urea for water between its ascending and descending limbs, preventing washout of the gradient."
    concept_id: c-ren-017
  - id: phy-040
    front: What are the three mechanisms of renal acid-base balance?
    back: "1) Bicarbonate mechanism (HCO3- reabsorption), 2) Phosphate mechanism (H+ excretion as NaH2PO4), 3) Ammonia mechanism (NH3 + H+ → NH4+ trapped in lumen)."
    concept_id: c-ren-009
  - id: phy-041
    front: Which acid-base mechanism is the most important for H+ excretion?
    back: "The ammonia mechanism. Glutamine is converted to NH3 in PCT cells (via glutaminase), secreted into lumen, combines with H+ to form NH4+ which is trapped and excreted in urine."
    concept_id: c-ren-021
  - id: phy-042
    front: Where is bicarbonate reabsorbed along the nephron?
    back: "80% in PCT, 15% in Loop of Henle, 5% in DCT and collecting duct. Reabsorbed as CO2 via carbonic anhydrase mechanism."
    concept_id: c-ren-001
  - id: phy-043
    front: How does the phosphate buffer mechanism acidify urine?
    back: "H+ is secreted into the tubular lumen and combines with NaHPO4 to form NaH2PO4, which is excreted in urine. Occurs mainly in DCT and collecting duct."
    concept_id: c-ren-007
  - id: phy-044
    front: What is the equation for renal plasma clearance?
    back: "Cl = (U × V) / P, where U = concentration of substance in urine, V = urine flow rate, P = concentration in plasma."
    concept_id: c-ren-011
  - id: phy-045
    front: Which substance is the ideal marker for measuring GFR and why?
    back: "Inulin — it is freely filtered but neither reabsorbed nor secreted. Creatinine is also used clinically because it is endogenous and its plasma concentration is steady."
    concept_id: c-ren-008
  - id: phy-046
    front: Which substance measures renal plasma flow and why?
    back: "Para-aminohippuric acid (PAH) — it is both filtered and secreted but not reabsorbed, so its clearance indicates total plasma passing through the kidneys."
    concept_id: c-ren-001
  - id: phy-047
    front: How is renal blood flow calculated from renal plasma flow?
    back: "'Renal blood flow = Renal plasma flow / Fraction of plasma in blood. Example: If RPF = 660 mL/min and plasma fraction = 55%, then RBF = 660/0.55 = 1200 mL/min.'"
    concept_id: c-ren-030
  - id: phy-048
    front: What is the normal urea clearance?
    back: 70 mL/min.
    concept_id: c-ren-011
  - id: phy-049
    front: What nerve is the 'Nerve of Filling' and what is the 'Nerve of Emptying'?
    back: "Sympathetic (Hypogastric nerve, L1-L2) is the Nerve of Filling — relaxes detrusor. Parasympathetic (Pelvic nerve, S1-S3) is the Nerve of Emptying — contracts detrusor."
  - id: phy-050
    front: What nerve provides voluntary control of micturition?
    back: "The pudendal nerve (S2, S3, S4), which controls the external urethral sphincter (skeletal muscle)."
    concept_id: c-ren-021
  - id: phy-051
    front: At what bladder volume is the micturition reflex triggered?
    back: When the bladder fills to approximately 250 mL of urine.
  - id: phy-052
    front: What are the two intrinsic mechanisms of renal autoregulation?
    back: "'1) Myogenic response: Afferent arteriole constricts in response to increased pressure and dilates with decreased pressure. 2) Tubuloglomerular feedback (TGF): Macula densa senses Na+/Cl- concentration and adjusts afferent arteriole tone.'"
    concept_id: c-ren-019
  - id: phy-053
    front: How does tubuloglomerular feedback work when Na+/Cl- is high in the filtrate?
    back: "High Na+/Cl- → adenosine release from macula densa → binds A1 receptors on afferent arteriole → vasoconstriction → reduced GFR."
    concept_id: c-ren-008
  - id: phy-054
    front: How does tubuloglomerular feedback work when Na+/Cl- is low in the filtrate?
    back: "Low Na+/Cl- → macula densa releases renin, bradykinin, and PGE2 → vasodilation of afferent arterioles + renin induces angiotensin II (constricts efferent arteriole) → increased GFR."
    concept_id: c-ren-008
  - id: phy-055
    front: What are the features of an atonic (flaccid) bladder?
    back: "Loss of detrusor muscle tone due to destruction of sensory pelvic nerve fibers. Bladder fills without micturition, urine overflows in drops (overflow incontinence). Caused by spinal injury or syphilis."
    concept_id: c-ren-021

  - id: phy-ren-034
    front: "What is the countercurrent multiplication system?"
    back: "A mechanism in the loop of Henle that creates an osmotic gradient in the renal medulla. The ascending limb actively pumps NaCl into the interstitium, and the descending limb is permeable to water, allowing water to leave and concentrate the tubular fluid."
    concept_id: c-ren-016
  - id: phy-ren-035
    front: "What is the countercurrent exchange mechanism?"
    back: "The vasa recta (peritubular capillaries) run parallel to the loop of Henle in a hairpin loop arrangement. This allows them to exchange solutes and water with the medullary interstitium, preserving the osmotic gradient."
    concept_id: c-ren-018
  - id: phy-ren-036
    front: "What is the role of aldosterone in the kidney?"
    back: "Aldosterone acts on the distal convoluted tubule and collecting duct to increase Na+ reabsorption and K+ secretion. It is secreted by the adrenal cortex in response to angiotensin II and elevated plasma K+."
    concept_id: c-ren-013
  - id: phy-ren-037
    front: "What is the RAAS and how does it regulate blood pressure?"
    back: "Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System: Renin from the kidney converts angiotensinogen to angiotensin I, which is converted to angiotensin II by ACE. Angiotensin II causes vasoconstriction and stimulates aldosterone release, increasing Na+ and water reabsorption."
    concept_id: c-ren-014
  - id: phy-ren-038
    front: "What is the role of ANP in the kidney?"
    back: "Atrial Natriuretic Peptide, released by atrial cardiomyocytes in response to atrial stretch. It promotes natriuresis (Na+ excretion), diuresis, and vasodilation, reducing blood volume and pressure."
    concept_id: c-ren-015
  - id: phy-ren-039
    front: "What is the countercurrent multiplier?"
    back: "The loop of Henle creates a hyperosmotic medullary interstitium through active NaCl transport in the thick ascending limb and water reabsorption in the descending limb. The osmolarity increases from ~300 mOsm/L at the cortex to ~1200 mOsm/L at the papilla."
    concept_id: c-ren-016
  - id: phy-ren-040
    front: "How does urea contribute to the medullary osmotic gradient?"
    back: "Urea is recycled between the collecting duct and the medullary interstitium. ADH increases urea permeability in the inner medullary collecting duct, allowing urea to diffuse into the interstitium, contributing ~50% of the medullary osmolarity."
    concept_id: c-ren-018
  - id: phy-ren-041
    front: "What is potassium handling by the kidney?"
    back: "Most K+ is reabsorbed in the proximal tubule and loop of Henle. K+ is secreted in the distal tubule and collecting duct under the influence of aldosterone. The kidney can both conserve and excrete K+ to maintain plasma K+ within narrow limits (3.5-5.0 mEq/L)."
    concept_id: c-ren-020
  - id: phy-ren-042
    front: "How does the kidney regulate acid-base balance?"
    back: "Through three mechanisms: bicarbonate reabsorption (proximal tubule), phosphate buffer system (titratable acid), and ammonia buffer system (collecting duct). The kidney excretes H+ and regenerates new HCO3-."
    concept_id: c-ren-022
  - id: phy-ren-043
    front: "What is the bicarbonate buffer mechanism in the kidney?"
    back: "The proximal tubule reabsorbs ~80-90% of filtered bicarbonate. H+ is secreted into the lumen, combines with HCO3- to form H2CO3, which is converted to CO2 and H2O by carbonic anhydrase. CO2 diffuses into the cell and is converted back to HCO3-."
    concept_id: c-ren-022
  - id: phy-ren-044
    front: "What is the phosphate buffer mechanism in the kidney?"
    back: "In the distal tubule, H+ is secreted and combines with HPO42- to form H2PO4- (titratable acid). This represents excretion of H+ bound to urinary buffers, allowing the kidney to eliminate acid without greatly lowering urine pH."
    concept_id: c-ren-024
  - id: phy-ren-045
    front: "What is the ammonia buffer mechanism in the kidney?"
    back: "The proximal tubule produces NH3 from glutamine. NH3 diffuses into the tubular lumen where it combines with H+ to form NH4+, which is trapped and excreted. This is the major mechanism for excreting large acid loads."
    concept_id: c-ren-023
  - id: phy-ren-046
    front: "How do diuretics work?"
    back: "Diuretics increase urine output by inhibiting Na+ and water reabsorption at different nephron segments: osmotic diuretics (proximal tubule), loop diuretics (thick ascending limb), thiazides (distal tubule), K+-sparing diuretics (collecting duct)."
    concept_id: c-ren-027
  - id: phy-ren-047
    front: "What is tubuloglomerular feedback?"
    back: "A mechanism where the macula densa cells in the distal tubule sense changes in tubular NaCl concentration. High NaCl causes release of ATP and adenosine, which constricts the afferent arteriole, reducing GFR."
    concept_id: c-ren-029
  - id: phy-ren-048
    front: "How is renal blood flow autoregulated?"
    back: "Through two mechanisms: myogenic response (vascular smooth muscle contracts in response to stretch) and tubuloglomerular feedback. These maintain relatively constant RBF and GFR over a wide range of perfusion pressures (80-180 mmHg)."
    concept_id: c-ren-029

  - id: phy-ren-049
    front: "What is the role of the proximal convoluted tubule?"
    back: "Reabsorbs ~65% of filtered Na+, Cl-, water, and almost all glucose and amino acids. Secretes H+, organic acids, and drugs. Major site of bulk reabsorption."
    concept_id: c-ren-004
  - id: phy-ren-050
    front: "What is the role of the distal convoluted tubule?"
    back: "Fine-tunes reabsorption and secretion under hormonal control (aldosterone, PTH). Reabsorbs Na+ and Ca2+, secretes K+ and H+. Contains macula densa cells."
    concept_id: c-ren-006
  - id: phy-ren-051
    front: "What is tubular secretion?"
    back: "The transfer of materials from peritubular capillaries to the tubular lumen. Important for eliminating waste products, drugs, and excess K+. Occurs mainly in PCT and DCT."
    concept_id: c-ren-010
  - id: phy-ren-052
    front: "What is the role of ADH in the kidney?"
    back: "Antidiuretic hormone (vasopressin) increases water reabsorption in the collecting ducts by inserting aquaporin-2 channels. Released from posterior pituitary in response to high plasma osmolarity."
    concept_id: c-ren-012
  - id: phy-ren-053
    front: "What is the role of calcium in renal function?"
    back: "Calcium is freely filtered at the glomerulus. ~60% reabsorbed in PCT, ~25% in thick ascending limb, ~10% in DCT (under PTH control). PTH stimulates Ca2+ reabsorption."
    concept_id: c-ren-025
  - id: phy-ren-054
    front: "What is the role of magnesium in renal function?"
    back: "Magnesium is freely filtered. ~60% reabsorbed in thick ascending limb of loop of Henle. ~5% reabsorbed in DCT. Regulated by PTH and dietary intake."
    concept_id: c-ren-026
  - id: phy-ren-055
    front: "What is urine osmolarity and how is it regulated?"
    back: "Normal urine osmolarity ranges from 50-1200 mOsm/L. Regulated by ADH (increases osmolarity by water reabsorption), aldosterone, and ANP. Depends on hydration status."
    concept_id: c-ren-028
