cards:
- id: phy-001
  front: What percentage of body weight is total body water in an average 70 kg male?
  back: 60% of body weight, which equals approximately 42 liters.
  concept_id: c-bf-001
- id: phy-002
  front: How is body fluid divided into major compartments?
  back: 'Two major compartments: Extracellular Fluid (ECF, ~20% body weight, ~14 L)
    and Intracellular Fluid (ICF, ~40% body weight, ~28 L).'
  concept_id: c-bf-001
- id: phy-003
  front: What are the three subcompartments of the ECF?
  back: Blood plasma (~3 L, 1/4 of ECF), interstitial fluid (~11 L, 3/4 of ECF), and
    transcellular fluid (synovial, CSF, peritoneal, pericardial, intraocular fluids).
  concept_id: c-bf-001
- id: phy-004
  front: What ions are predominantly found in the ICF versus ECF?
  back: 'ICF: High K+, phosphate, magnesium, sulfate. ECF: High Na+, Cl-. Cell membranes
    are highly permeable to water but relatively impermeable to Na+ and Cl+.'
  concept_id: c-bf-002
- id: phy-005
  front: How much protein does the ICF contain compared to plasma?
  back: ICF contains approximately four times as much protein as plasma.
  concept_id: c-bf-002
- id: phy-006
  front: What is the daily water intake and its sources?
  back: '~2300 mL/day total: fluid ingestion and water in food (~2100 mL/day) plus
    metabolic water from carbohydrate oxidation (~200 mL/day).'
  concept_id: c-bf-008
- id: phy-007
  front: What is the most important route for maintenance of fluid and electrolyte
    balance?
  back: The kidneys (urine output), which can range from 0.5 L/day in dehydrated persons
    to 20 L/day in hyperhydrated persons.
  concept_id: c-bf-009
- id: phy-008
  front: What is the formula for the indicator dilution method?
  back: Volume = Amount of substance injected / Concentration of substance after mixing.
    Used to measure body fluid compartment volumes.
  concept_id: c-bf-001
- id: phy-009
  front: How is total body water measured?
  back: Using radioactive water (tritium, 3H2O) or heavy water (deuterium, 2H2O),
    which mix with all compartments within hours. Plasma concentration is measured
    and the dilution formula applied.
  concept_id: c-bf-001
- id: phy-010
  front: How is plasma volume measured?
  back: 'Using substances that bind to plasma proteins and do not diffuse into the
    interstitium: 125I-albumin or Evans blue dye (T-1824).'
  concept_id: c-bf-004
- id: phy-011
  front: How is ICF volume calculated?
  back: ICF volume = Total body water - ECF volume (cannot be measured directly).
  concept_id: c-bf-001
- id: phy-012
  front: What determines fluid distribution between plasma and interstitial fluid?
  back: The balance of hydrostatic and colloid osmotic forces across the capillary
    membranes (Starling forces).
  concept_id: c-bf-004
- id: phy-013
  front: What determines fluid distribution between ICF and ECF?
  back: The osmotic effect of small solutes (especially Na+, Cl-) acting across the
    cell membrane. Water moves rapidly to maintain isotonicity.
  concept_id: c-bf-002
- id: phy-014
  front: What are the three classifications of dehydration based on severity?
  back: Mild (5% fluid loss — rehydration), Moderate (10% — rehydration), Severe (15%
    — hospitalization and emergency treatment).
  concept_id: c-bf-009
- id: phy-015
  front: What is isotonic dehydration and what causes it?
  back: Balanced loss of solute (Na+) and water. Caused by diarrhea and vomiting.
  concept_id: c-bf-012
- id: phy-016
  front: What is hypotonic dehydration?
  back: Loss of more solute (Na+) than water. Caused by excess use of diuretics.
  concept_id: c-bf-012
- id: phy-017
  front: What is hypertonic dehydration?
  back: Loss of more water than solute. Commonly caused by fever.
  concept_id: c-bf-012
- id: phy-018
  front: What are the signs of very severe dehydration?
  back: Organ damage (brain, liver, kidneys), mental depression and confusion, renal
    failure, convulsions, and coma.
  concept_id: c-bf-012
- id: phy-019
  front: What is water intoxication?
  back: Also called hyperhydration or water poisoning — a condition characterized
    by a great increase in the water content of the body, occurring when more fluid
    is taken than can be excreted.
  concept_id: c-bf-016
- id: phy-020
  front: Name five causes of water intoxication.
  back: Heart failure, renal disorders, hypersecretion of ADH (SIADH), intravenous
    administration of excessive fluids, and infants in first month of life.
  concept_id: c-bf-016
- id: phy-021
  front: What are the treatment options for severe water intoxication?
  back: Diuretics (increase water loss), ADH receptor antagonists (prevent ADH-induced
    water reabsorption), and IV saline to restore Na+.
  concept_id: c-bf-009
- id: phy-022
  front: Why is water loss through sweat potentially dangerous?
  back: Sweat loss can range from 100 mL/day to 1-2 L during exercise. If thirst centers
    are not stimulated to cause water intake, it can deplete body fluids.
  concept_id: c-bf-001

- id: phy-bf-023
  front: "What is interstitial fluid?"
  back: "The fluid that bathes and surrounds the cells of the body. It is part of the ECF and makes up about 3/4 of the ECF volume (~11 L)."
  concept_id: c-bf-005
- id: phy-bf-024
  front: "What is transcellular fluid?"
  back: "Fluid that is separated from plasma and interstitial fluid by an epithelial cell layer. Includes synovial, CSF, peritoneal, pericardial, and intraocular fluids."
  concept_id: c-bf-006
- id: phy-bf-025
  front: "What is fluid balance?"
  back: "The balance between water intake and water output. Normally, intake equals output to maintain a constant body water volume."
  concept_id: c-bf-007
- id: phy-bf-026
  front: "What are the three main routes of water loss?"
  back: "Urine (kidneys, most important route), insensible loss (skin and lungs, ~600-900 mL/day), and sweat (variable, 100 mL to 1-2 L/day)."
  concept_id: c-bf-009
- id: phy-bf-027
  front: "How is interstitial fluid volume measured?"
  back: "Using indicators that distribute throughout the ECF but do not enter cells, such as inulin or sucrose. The volume is calculated using the indicator dilution principle."
  concept_id: c-bf-010
- id: phy-bf-028
  front: "What forces govern fluid exchange across capillary walls?"
  back: "Starling forces: capillary hydrostatic pressure, interstitial hydrostatic pressure, capillary oncotic pressure, and interstitial oncotic pressure."
  concept_id: c-bf-011
- id: phy-bf-029
  front: "What is isotonic dehydration and what are its causes?"
  back: "Balanced loss of solute (Na+) and water, so ECF osmolarity is unchanged. Causes: diarrhea, vomiting, hemorrhage, burns."
  concept_id: c-bf-013
- id: phy-bf-030
  front: "What is hypotonic dehydration and what are its causes?"
  back: "Loss of more solute (Na+) than water, so ECF osmolarity decreases. Water moves into cells causing swelling. Causes: diuretics, salt-restricted diets, adrenal insufficiency."
  concept_id: c-bf-014
- id: phy-bf-031
  front: "What is hypertonic dehydration and what are its causes?"
  back: "Loss of more water than solute, so ECF osmolarity increases. Water moves out of cells causing shrinkage. Causes: fever, excessive sweating, diabetes insipidus, insufficient water intake."
  concept_id: c-bf-015
