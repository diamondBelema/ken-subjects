## c-bf-001

Total body water constitutes approximately 60% of body weight in an average 70 kg male (about 42 L). This varies with age, gender, and body composition — females average ~50% and neonates ~70-75%.

### Intracellular Fluid (ICF)
- Makes up ~40% of body weight (~28 L of the total 42 L)
- Trapped inside approximately 100 trillion cells, separated from ECF by the cell membrane
- Contains low concentrations of Na+ and Cl-, almost no Ca2+
- Rich in K+, phosphate, magnesium, and sulfate ions
- Contains approximately four times more protein than plasma

### Extracellular Fluid (ECF)
- Makes up ~20% of body weight (~14 L total)
- Divided into:
  - **Blood plasma** (~3 L, 1/4 of ECF)
  - **Interstitial fluid** (~11 L, 3/4 of ECF)
  - **Transcellular fluid** — special ECF including synovial fluid, cerebrospinal fluid, peritoneal, pericardial, and intraocular fluids
- Plasma and interstitial fluid have nearly identical composition except for proteins (higher in plasma)

### Fluid Balance

**Water Intake (~2300 mL/day):**
- Fluid ingestion and water in food (~2100 mL/day)
- Metabolic water from oxidation of carbohydrates (~200 mL/day)

**Water Loss:**
| Route | Amount |
|-------|--------|
| Insensible loss (respiratory tract + skin) | ~700 mL/day |
| Sweat | 100 mL/day to 1-2 L (exercise) |
| Feces | ~100 mL/day (can increase dramatically in diarrhea) |
| Urine (kidneys) | 0.5 L/day (dehydrated) to 20 L/day (hyperhydrated) |

The kidneys are the most important route for maintaining fluid and electrolyte balance.

### Measurement of Body Fluid Compartments

Measured by the **indicator dilution method** (dye dilution method):

**Volume = Amount of substance injected / Concentration after mixing**

- **Total body water**: Tritium (3H2O) or heavy water (2H2O) — mixes with all compartments within hours
- **ECF volume**: Radioactive Na+, Cl-, iothalamate, thiosulfate, inulin — permeate ECF but not cells
- **Plasma volume**: 125I-albumin or Evans blue dye (T-1824) — bind to plasma proteins
- **ICF volume**: Calculated as Total body water minus ECF volume
- **Interstitial fluid**: ECF volume minus plasma volume
- **Blood volume**: Plasma volume / (1 - hematocrit)

### Marker Substance Requirements
- Non-toxic
- Mixes thoroughly in shortest time
- Not rapidly excreted
- Excreted completely within reasonable time
- Should not change fluid color or volume

### Fluid Exchange Between Compartments
- ECF distribution between plasma and interstitium is determined by hydrostatic and colloid osmotic forces across capillary membranes
- Distribution between ICF and ECF is determined by osmotic effects of small solutes (especially Na+, Cl-) across cell membranes
- Cell membranes are highly permeable to water but relatively impermeable to Na+ and Cl+
- ICF remains isotonic with ECF due to rapid water movement

## c-bf-012

Excessive loss of water from the body. Minimum intake ~1 L/day; active individuals need 2-3 L/day.

### Classification by Severity
| Severity | Fluid Lost | Treatment |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Mild | ~5% of body fluid | Rehydration |
| Moderate | ~10% of body fluid | Rehydration |
| Severe | ~15% of body fluid | Hospitalization, emergency treatment |

### Classification by Solute-Solvent Ratio
- **Isotonic dehydration**: Balanced loss of Na+ and water (e.g., diarrhea, vomiting)
- **Hypotonic dehydration**: Greater loss of Na+ than water (e.g., excess diuretics)
- **Hypertonic dehydration**: Greater loss of water than Na+ (e.g., fever)

### Signs and Symptoms
- **Mild/Moderate**: Dry mouth, excess thirst, decreased sweating, decreased urine, headache, dizziness, weakness, muscle cramps
- **Severe**: Decreased blood volume and cardiac output, low blood pressure, hypovolemic shock, fainting
- **Very severe**: Organ damage (brain, liver, kidneys), mental confusion, renal failure, convulsions, coma

## c-bf-016

Condition characterized by a great increase in water content of the body. Also called hyperhydration or water poisoning.

### Causes
- Heart failure (inability to pump blood properly)
- Renal disorders (inability to excrete enough water)
- Hypersecretion of ADH (e.g., SIADH)
- Intravenous administration of excessive fluids
- Infants at higher risk in first month of life

### Signs and Symptoms
- Behavioral changes (brain most vulnerable)
- Drowsiness, inattention, nausea, vomiting
- Weight loss, weakness, blurred vision
- Anemia, cyanosis, hemorrhage, shock
- Muscular weakness, cramps, twitching, paralysis
- Severe: delirium, seizures, coma

### Treatment
- Mild: Fluid restriction
- Severe: Diuretics, ADH receptor antagonists, IV saline to restore Na+
