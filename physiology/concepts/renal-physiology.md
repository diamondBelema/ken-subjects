## c-ren-001

Bean-shaped organs located retroperitoneally in the upper posterior abdomen between T12 and L3 vertebrae. Left kidney typically higher than right due to the liver. Each weighs ~150 g, size of a clenched fist. The renal artery, vein, lymphatics, nerves, and ureter enter/exit through the hilum on the medial side.

### Layers
- **Cortex** (outer): Dark granular appearance; contains renal corpuscles and convoluted tubules. Cortical tissue extends into medulla as columns of Bertini.
- **Medulla** (inner): Tubular and vascular structures arranged in 8-18 medullary (Malpighian) pyramids.
- **Renal Sinus**: Contains renal pelvis, major and minor calyces, nerves, arteries, veins, connective tissue, and fat.

### Renal Circulation
- Kidneys receive ~22% of total cardiac output (~1100 mL/min)
- Unique two-capillary-bed system arranged in series:
  - **Glomerular capillaries** (high pressure ~60 mmHg) — favor rapid filtration
  - **Peritubular capillaries** (low pressure ~13 mmHg) — favor rapid reabsorption
- Connected by efferent arterioles that regulate pressure in both beds
- Pathway: Renal artery → interlobar → arcuate → interlobular → afferent arterioles → glomerular capillaries → efferent arterioles → peritubular capillaries → interlobular vein → arcuate vein → interlobar vein → renal vein

## c-ren-005

The functional unit of the kidney. Each kidney contains ~800,000-1,000,000 nephrons. Kidneys cannot regenerate nephrons; number decreases ~10% per decade after age 40.

### Structure
Each nephron consists of:
1. **Renal Corpuscle** — Glomerulus + Bowman's Capsule
2. **Tubular portion** — PCT, Loop of Henle, DCT, Collecting Duct

### Filtration Membrane
Three-layer barrier:
- Glomerular capillary endothelium with fenestrae (pores ~0.1 μm diameter)
- Fused basement membrane (capillary + Bowman's visceral layer)
- Podocytes (visceral epithelial cells) with pedicels (foot processes) creating slit pores

### Types of Nephrons
| Feature | Cortical (85%) | Juxtamedullary (15%) |
|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| Corpuscle location | Outer cortex | Corticomedullary junction |
| Loop of Henle | Short (outer medulla) | Long (to papilla tip) |
| Blood supply | Peritubular capillaries | Vasa recta |
| Function | General filtration | Urine concentration |

### Tubular Portion

**Proximal Convoluted Tubule (PCT):** ~14 mm length, 55 μm diameter. Brush-bordered cuboidal epithelial cells. Site of ~88% reabsorption (glucose, amino acids, Na+, K+, Ca2+, Cl-, phosphate, bicarbonate, water, urea, uric acid).

**Loop of Henle:** Descending limb (permeable to water, flattened epithelium), hairpin bend, ascending limb (impermeable to water, thick segment contains macula densa). Reabsorbs Na+ and Cl-.

**Distal Convoluted Tubule (DCT):** Cuboidal cells without brush border (intercalated cells). Reabsorbs Na+, Ca2+, bicarbonate, and water. Continues as collecting duct.

**Collecting Duct:** Multiple initial collecting ducts unite, pass through medulla, form papillary ducts (ducts of Bellini) that open at papilla into minor calyces. Minor calyces → major calyces → renal pelvis → ureter.

## c-ren-012

First step in urine formation. Ultrafiltration of fluid from glomerular capillaries into Bowman's capsule. The filtrate resembles plasma but lacks proteins, cellular elements, Ca2+, and fatty acids.

### GFR
- Average GFR: **125 mL/min** or **180 L/day**
- Filtration fraction = GFR / Renal plasma flow × 100 ≈ **20%**

### Starling Forces
| Force | Direction | Value (mmHg) |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Glomerular hydrostatic pressure (GHP) | Favors filtration | 60 |
| Bowman's capsule colloid osmotic pressure (BCCOP) | Favors filtration | 0 |
| Bowman's capsule hydrostatic pressure (BCHP) | Opposes filtration | 15 |
| Glomerular capillary colloid osmotic pressure (GCCOP) | Opposes filtration | 25 |

**Net Filtration Pressure (NFP)** = GHP - GCCOP - BCHP = 60 - 25 - 15 = **20 mmHg**

Starling hypothesis: Net filtration through capillary membrane is proportional to hydrostatic pressure difference minus oncotic pressure difference across the membrane.

### Factors Affecting GFR
**Increase GFR:** Atrial/brain natriuretic peptide, cAMP, dopamine, nitric oxide, prostaglandin PGE2 (vasodilation)

**Decrease GFR:** Angiotensin II, endothelins, noradrenaline, platelet activating factor, PGF2 (vasodilation), sympathetic stimulation, mesangial cell contraction, increased colloid osmotic pressure, increased Bowman's capsule pressure

**Autoregulation range:** Blood pressure 80-180 mmHg — GFR remains relatively constant via myogenic response and tubuloglomerular feedback (TGF).

## c-ren-016

Selective reabsorption of needed substances from renal tubular fluid back into blood.

### Routes
- **Transcellular**: Lumen → tubular cell (apical membrane) → interstitial fluid → capillary
- **Paracellular**: Lumen → lateral intercellular space (tight junctions) → interstitial fluid → capillary

### Reabsorption by Segment
| Segment | Substances Reabsorbed |
|---------|----------------------|
| PCT (88%) | Glucose, amino acids, Na+, K+, Ca2+, Cl-, phosphate, bicarbonate, water, urea, uric acid |
| Loop of Henle | Na+, Cl- |
| DCT | Na+, Ca2+, bicarbonate, water |

### Transport Maximum (Tm) and Renal Threshold
- **Tm**: Maximum rate of reabsorption of a substance. Tm for glucose (TmG) = 375 mg/min (males), 300 mg/min (females).
- **Renal threshold**: Plasma concentration at which a substance appears in urine. For glucose: **180 mg/dL**. Below this, glucose is completely reabsorbed.

### Key Reabsorption Details
- **Na+**: ~99% reabsorbed. PCT: antiport (Na+/H+ exchange) and symport (with glucose/amino acids). Na+-K+ pump on basolateral side. DCT: stimulated by aldosterone.
- **Water**: Obligatory (PCT, follows Na+ reabsorption); Facultative (DCT/collecting duct, ADH-dependent)
- **Glucose**: Completely reabsorbed in PCT via SGLT2 (luminal) → GLUT2 (basolateral)
- **Bicarbonate**: Reabsorbed as CO2 in PCT via carbonic anhydrase mechanism

## c-ren-018

Transport of substances from peritubular capillaries into the tubular lumen. Also called tubular excretion.

- **K+**: Actively secreted by Na+-K+ pump in PCT, DCT, and collecting ducts
- **Ammonia**: Secreted in PCT
- **H+**: Secreted in PCT and DCT (maximum in PCT)
- **Urea**: Secreted in Loop of Henle
- Important for elimination of toxins, organic acids/bases, drugs, and foreign substances

## c-ren-019

**Urinary excretion rate = Filtration rate - Reabsorption rate + Secretion rate**

| Substance Type | Example | Handling |
|---------------|---------|----------|
| Freely filtered, not reabsorbed or secreted | Creatinine, urea | Excretion = Filtration rate |
| Freely filtered, partly reabsorbed | Na+, Cl- | Excretion = Filtration - Reabsorption |
| Freely filtered, completely reabsorbed | Glucose, amino acids | Not excreted (normal) |
| Freely filtered + secreted | Toxins, drugs | Excretion = Filtration + Secretion |

### Urine Concentration
- Filtrate osmolarity: ~300 mOsm/L
- Maximum urine osmolarity: ~1200 mOsm/L (4× plasma)
- Dilute urine: When ADH is inhibited (body water excess)
- Concentrated urine: When ADH is active + countercurrent mechanism

## c-ren-020

Enables kidney to concentrate urine. Two components:

### Countercurrent Multiplication (Loop of Henle)
- Creates medullary osmotic gradient: 300 mOsm/L (corticomedullary junction) → 1200 mOsm/L (inner medulla)
- **Thick ascending limb**: Actively pumps NaCl into interstitium (impermeable to water)
- **Descending limb**: Water moves out into hyperosmotic medulla (permeable to water, impermeable to solutes)
- NaCl recirculates between limbs, progressively concentrating the medulla
- **Urea recycling**: ~50% urea reabsorbed in PCT, secreted in Loop of Henle, concentrated in collecting duct, diffuses into inner medullary interstitium via UT-A1/UT-A3 (ADH-activated), contributing to inner medullary hyperosmolarity

### Countercurrent Exchange (Vasa Recta)
- Maintains (not creates) the medullary gradient
- Vasa recta run parallel to Loop of Henle in hairpin configuration
- Descending limb: Accumulates NaCl, loses water
- Ascending limb: Loses NaCl, gains water
- NaCl and urea exchanged for water between limbs — preserves the gradient

### Pathophysiology
- **Bartter syndrome**: Defect in thick ascending segment → decreased Na+/water reabsorption
- **Diabetes insipidus**: ADH deficiency → failure to reabsorb water → polyuria
- **Osmotic diuresis**: Solutes like glucose cause diuresis (diabetes mellitus)
- **SIADH**: Excess ADH → water retention → decreased ECF osmolarity

## c-ren-023

Urine is acidic (pH 4.5-6). Kidneys maintain acid-base balance by excreting H+ and retaining HCO3-.

### Three Mechanisms

**Bicarbonate Mechanism:**
- All filtered HCO3- reabsorbed (80% PCT, 15% Loop of Henle, 5% DCT/collecting duct)
- H+ secreted via Na+/H+ antiport (PCT) and ATP-driven proton pump (DCT/collecting duct)
- HCO3- reabsorbed as CO2 via carbonic anhydrase

**Phosphate Mechanism:**
- H+ secreted into lumen in exchange for Na+ (aldosterone-dependent)
- H+ combines with NaHPO4 → NaH2PO4 → excreted in urine
- Makes urine acidic; occurs mainly in DCT and collecting duct

**Ammonia Mechanism** (most important for H+ excretion):
- Glutamine → glutamic acid → NH3 (via glutaminase in PCT cells)
- NH3 secreted into lumen, combines with H+ → NH4+
- NH4+ trapped in lumen (membrane impermeable to ammonium) → excreted in urine
- Also: Deamination of glycine and alanine produces NH3

**Clinical:** Metabolic acidosis (failure to excrete H+); Metabolic alkalosis (excessive H+ excretion)

## c-ren-027

Substances that enhance urine formation and output by increasing water, Na+, and Cl- excretion.

### Types
1. **Osmotic diuretics**: Increase tubular fluid osmotic pressure → reduce water reabsorption (urea, mannitol, sucrose, glucose)
2. **Electrolyte reabsorption inhibitors**: Block Na+/K+ reabsorption → osmotic diuresis (furosemide, bumetanide, triamterene, amiloride, thiazides)
3. **Aldosterone antagonists**: Block aldosterone action (spironolactone, eplerenone)
4. **Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors**: (acetazolamide)
5. **GFR-increasing agents**: Xanthines increase GFR and decrease Na+ reabsorption (caffeine, theophylline)
6. **ADH secretion inhibitors**: Large water volumes, ethanol
7. **ADH receptor (V2) antagonists**

**Clinical uses:** Hypertension, congestive cardiac failure, edema

## c-ren-028

Urination — the process of expelling urine from the body. A reflex process with voluntary control in adults.

### Anatomy
- **Detrusor muscle**: Three smooth muscle layers of bladder wall
- **Internal urethral sphincter**: Smooth muscle at bladder neck, involuntary control
- **External urethral sphincter**: Skeletal muscle (pelvic floor), voluntary control
- Normal bladder capacity: 300-500 mL for 2-5 hours

### Neural Control
- **Sympathetic (Hypogastric nerve, L1-L2)**: "Nerve of filling" — relaxes detrusor, constricts internal sphincter
- **Parasympathetic (Pelvic nerve, S1-S3)**: "Nerve of emptying/micturition" — contracts detrusor, relaxes internal sphincter
- **Somatic (Pudendal nerve, S2-S4)**: Voluntary control of external sphincter

### Reflex Arc
1. Bladder fills to ~250 mL → stretch receptors activated
2. Parasympathetic fibers relay to sacral spinal cord (S1-S3)
3. Information conveyed to Pontine Micturition Center (PMC) in pons
4. PMC integrates signals from higher brain centers
5. If voiding: Parasympathetic → detrusor contraction → internal sphincter relaxation
6. Simultaneously: Somatic inhibition → external sphincter relaxation → urine expulsion

### Pathophysiology
- **Atonic bladder (flaccid)**: Loss of detrusor tone from sensory nerve destruction → overflow incontinence (spinal injury, syphilis)
- **Automatic bladder**: Hyperactive reflex with loss of voluntary control (spinal cord transection recovery)
- **Uninhibited neurogenic bladder**: Frequent uncontrollable micturition from midbrain lesion
- **Nocturnal enuresis**: Bedwetting from incomplete myelination (< 3 years old)

## c-ren-029

### Urinalysis
**Physical:** Volume, color, appearance, specific gravity, osmolarity, pH
**Microscopic:** RBC casts (glomerulonephritis), WBC casts (pyelonephritis), granular casts (CKD), waxy casts (severe kidney disease), crystals, bacteria
**Chemical:** Glucose (>180 mg/dL = glycosuria), protein (>30 mg/day = proteinuria), ketone bodies, bilirubin, urobilinogen, blood, nitrite

### Blood Examination
- Plasma proteins: Total 7.3 g/dL, albumin 4.7 g/dL, globulin 2.3 g/dL
- Urea: 25-40 mg/dL, Uric acid: 2.5 mg/dL, Creatinine: 0.5-1.5 mg/dL
- Elevated in renal failure

### Plasma Clearance
**Cl = (U × V) / P** where U = urine concentration, V = urine flow rate, P = plasma concentration

- **Inulin clearance** = GFR (ideal: freely filtered, not reabsorbed/secreted)
- **Creatinine clearance** = GFR (clinically easier, endogenous)
- **PAH clearance** = Renal plasma flow (filtered and secreted, not reabsorbed)
- **Renal blood flow** = RPF / (1 - hematocrit)
- **Urea clearance**: Normal ~70 mL/min
