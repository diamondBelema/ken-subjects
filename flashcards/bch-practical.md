  # ── PROTEINURIA ──
  - id: bch-fc-001
    front: "State the principle of the heat coagulation test for proteinuria."
    back: "Heating urine near boiling point denatures proteins by breaking hydrogen bonds, causing them to unfold and coagulate into visible turbidity. Acetic acid distinguishes protein precipitate (persists) from phosphate precipitate (dissolves)."
    concept_id: bch-proteinuria
    tags: [proteinuria, principle]
  - id: bch-fc-002
    front: "Why is acetic acid added after heating urine?"
    back: "Acetic acid lowers pH to albumin's isoelectric point (~4.6), neutralizing its charge for maximum precipitation. It also dissolves phosphate precipitates (false positives), so if turbidity persists after acid + reheating, it confirms protein."
    concept_id: bch-proteinuria
    tags: [proteinuria, reagents]
  - id: bch-fc-003
    front: "Why is albumin the major protein detected by the heat coagulation test?"
    back: "Albumin coagulates rapidly upon heating because it is the most abundant protein in pathological urine and has a low isoelectric point (~4.6), making it highly sensitive to the heat + acid test."
    concept_id: bch-proteinuria
    tags: [proteinuria, clinical]
  - id: bch-fc-004
    front: "A patient has a positive heat coagulation test. What are the possible clinical causes?"
    back: "Glomerular damage (glomerulonephritis), nephrotic syndrome, urinary tract infections, or renal tubular disease. Further quantitative testing (e.g., Biuret test) is needed."
    concept_id: bch-proteinuria
    tags: [proteinuria, clinical]
  - id: bch-fc-005
    front: "What does a negative heat coagulation test mean? Can it be misleading?"
    back: "It means no protein was detected. However, it can be misleading because the test is most sensitive to albumin — other proteins (e.g., Bence Jones protein in myeloma) may not coagulate with heat and could be missed."
    concept_id: bch-proteinuria
    tags: [proteinuria, limitations]

  - id: bch-fc-006
    front: "What is the stationary phase in paper chromatography?"

    concept_id: bch-chromatography

    back: "The solvent mixture of n-butanol : glacial acetic acid : water in a 4:1:1 ratio."
    concept_id: bch-chromatography
    tags: [chromatography, phases]
  - id: bch-fc-008
    front: "What force causes the solvent to move up the paper?"
    back: "Capillary action — the solvent climbs between the cellulose fibres of the paper."
    concept_id: bch-chromatography
    tags: [chromatography, mechanism]
  - id: bch-fc-009
    front: "Why is pencil used instead of pen to mark the origin line?"
    back: "Pencil contains graphite, which is insoluble in the solvent. Pen ink dissolves in the solvent, producing extra spots that interfere with separation."
    concept_id: bch-chromatography
    tags: [chromatography, procedure]
  - id: bch-fc-010
    front: "Why must the solvent level be below the origin line?"
    back: "If the solvent touches the origin spots, the amino acids will dissolve directly into the solvent reservoir instead of travelling up with the solvent front."
    concept_id: bch-chromatography
    tags: [chromatography, procedure]
  - id: bch-fc-011
    front: "Why is ninhydrin sprayed after chromatography?"
    back: "Ninhydrin reacts with free amino groups (-NH2) of amino acids to produce a coloured compound (Ruhemann's Purple), allowing the separated amino acids to be visualized."
    concept_id: bch-chromatography
    tags: [chromatography, visualization]
  - id: bch-fc-012
    front: "What colour do proline and hydroxyproline produce with ninhydrin, and why?"
    back: "Yellow instead of purple. They contain secondary amino groups (secondary amines) rather than primary amino groups, so they form a different complex with ninhydrin."
    concept_id: bch-chromatography
    tags: [chromatography, exception]
  - id: bch-fc-013
    front: "How is the Rf value calculated?"
    back: "Rf = Distance moved by amino acid / Distance moved by solvent front. It has no units and is always between 0 and 1."
    concept_id: bch-chromatography
    tags: [chromatography, calculation]
  - id: bch-fc-014
    front: "How do you use Rf values to identify an unknown amino acid?"
    back: "Compare the Rf value of the unknown spot to the Rf values of known standard amino acids run on the same chromatogram. Matching Rf values indicate the same amino acid."
    concept_id: bch-chromatography
    tags: [chromatography, identification]
  - id: bch-fc-015
    front: "Why must the solvent front be marked immediately after removing the paper?"
    back: "The solvent evaporates quickly. If not marked immediately, you cannot measure the distance the solvent travelled, making Rf calculation impossible."
    concept_id: bch-chromatography
    tags: [chromatography, procedure]

  # ── SERUM UREA ──
  - id: bch-fc-016
    front: "Why is urease used instead of another enzyme to measure serum urea?"
    back: "Urease is highly specific — it acts only on urea. This ensures no other substance in serum interferes with the reaction, making the test accurate and reliable."
    concept_id: bch-serum-urea
    tags: [urea, reagents]
  - id: bch-fc-017
    front: "Write the two-step reaction for serum urea determination."
    back: "Step 1: Urea + H2O --(Urease)--> 2NH3 + CO2. Step 2: NH3 + phenol + hypochlorite --(sodium nitroprusside)--> indophenol blue (measured at 578 nm)."
    concept_id: bch-serum-urea
    tags: [urea, reaction]
  - id: bch-fc-018
    front: "Why do we need a blank in the serum urea assay?"
    back: "The blank contains everything except urea. It sets the spectrophotometer to zero, removing absorbance contributed by reagents so that only the urea-derived color is measured."
    concept_id: bch-serum-urea
    tags: [urea, procedure]
  - id: bch-fc-019
    front: "What is the purpose of the standard in the serum urea assay?"
    back: "The standard contains a known concentration of urea. By comparing the sample's absorbance to the standard's absorbance, we can calculate the unknown concentration using the Beer-Lambert ratio."
    concept_id: bch-serum-urea
    tags: [urea, procedure]
  - id: bch-fc-020
    front: "State Beer-Lambert Law and its significance in this assay."
    back: "Beer-Lambert Law states that absorbance is directly proportional to concentration. More urea means more ammonia means more colored complex means higher absorbance. This allows us to calculate concentration from absorbance."
    concept_id: bch-serum-urea
    tags: [urea, principle]
  - id: bch-fc-021
    front: "A patient has very high serum urea. Give three possible causes."
    back: "1) Kidney failure (reduced filtration, urea accumulates). 2) Dehydration (less water, urea becomes concentrated). 3) High-protein diet or severe tissue breakdown (increased urea production)."
    concept_id: bch-serum-urea
    tags: [urea, clinical]
  - id: bch-fc-022
    front: "Why is severe liver disease associated with low serum urea?"
    back: "The liver synthesizes urea via the urea cycle. If the liver is damaged, it cannot produce urea effectively, so serum urea drops."
    concept_id: bch-serum-urea
    tags: [urea, clinical]

  # ── ELECTROLYTES ──

    concept_id: bch-electrolytes

  - id: bch-fc-026

    concept_id: bch-electrolytes
    tags: [electrolytes, normal-values, standards]
  - id: bch-fc-027a

  - id: bch-fc-027b

    concept_id: bch-electrolytes

  - id: bch-fc-027c
    front: "Why can SIADH cause hyponatremia?"
    back: "SIADH = Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH secretion. Too much ADH causes the kidney to retain too much water. This water dilutes the sodium in blood, so serum sodium concentration falls. The sodium isn't lost — it is diluted."

  - id: bch-fc-027d

  - id: bch-fc-028a

    concept_id: bch-electrolytes
    tags: [electrolytes, potassium, physiology]
  - id: bch-fc-028b
    front: "Is potassium estimation colorimetric or turbidimetric? Why?"
    back: "Turbidimetric. Potassium reacts with tetraphenylborate to form a cloudy suspension (turbidity), not a true color. The spectrophotometer measures light scattered by the particles, not light absorbed by a colored compound."

    tags: [electrolytes, potassium, method]
  - id: bch-fc-028c
    front: "Why does kidney failure cause high potassium?"

    concept_id: bch-electrolytes
    tags: [electrolytes, potassium, clinical]
  - id: bch-fc-028d
    front: "Give three causes of hypokalemia."

    concept_id: bch-electrolytes
    tags: [electrolytes, potassium, clinical]
  - id: bch-fc-028

    concept_id: bch-electrolytes
    tags: [electrolytes, potassium, clinical]
  - id: bch-fc-029
    front: "How do you calculate electrolyte concentration from absorbance values?"

    tags: [electrolytes, calculation]

  # ── CREATININE ──
  - id: bch-fc-030
    front: "State the principle of the Jaffe reaction."
    back: "Creatinine reacts with alkaline picrate (picric acid + NaOH) to form an orange-red creatinine-picrate complex. The color intensity is proportional to creatinine concentration, measured at 500-520 nm."
    concept_id: bch-creatinine
    tags: [creatinine, principle]
  - id: bch-fc-031
    front: "What colour change occurs in the Jaffe reaction?"
    back: "Picric acid is yellow in alkaline solution. After reacting with creatinine, it turns orange-red (creatinine-picrate complex)."
    concept_id: bch-creatinine
    tags: [creatinine, reaction]
  - id: bch-fc-032
    front: "Why is creatinine used as a marker of kidney function?"
    back: "Creatinine is a breakdown product of creatine phosphate in muscle, freely filtered by the glomerulus, and not reabsorbed. Its blood level reflects glomerular filtration rate (GFR)."
    concept_id: bch-creatinine
    tags: [creatinine, clinical]
  - id: bch-fc-033
    front: "What is creatinine clearance and why is it important?"
    back: "Creatinine clearance measures the volume of blood plasma cleared of creatinine per minute. It estimates GFR and is the most common clinical measure of kidney function."
    concept_id: bch-creatinine
    tags: [creatinine, clinical]
  - id: bch-fc-033a
    front: "Why is creatinine considered a better indicator of kidney function than urea?"
    back: "Creatinine production is relatively constant and less influenced by diet. It is mainly removed by glomerular filtration, so increased blood creatinine reflects reduced kidney filtration more accurately than urea, which is affected by protein intake, hydration, and liver function."
    concept_id: bch-creatinine
    tags: [creatinine, clinical, viva]
  - id: bch-fc-033b
    front: "What is the difference between creatine and creatinine?"
    back: "Creatine phosphate is an energy storage molecule in muscle. Creatinine is the waste product of creatine phosphate metabolism. The pathway: Creatine phosphate -> Creatinine -> Blood -> Kidney filtration -> Urine. We measure creatinine, not creatine."
    concept_id: bch-creatinine
    tags: [creatinine, viva, trap]
  - id: bch-fc-033c
    front: "Compare urea and creatinine as renal function markers."
    back: "Urea: from protein metabolism, affected by diet/hydration, made by liver, less specific for renal function. Creatinine: from muscle creatine metabolism, more constant production, made by muscle, better marker of GFR."
    concept_id: bch-creatinine
    tags: [creatinine, urea, comparison, viva]

  - id: bch-fc-046
