# Glycolysis

## Overview

- **Definition**: Derived from *glycos* (sweet/sugar) + *lysis* (breakdown). An enzyme-catalyzed pathway used by both aerobic and anaerobic cells to break glucose down into 2 molecules of pyruvate.
- **Also called**: Embden-Meyerhof pathway (after Gustav Embden, Otto Meyerhof, and Jacob Parnas who elucidated it by 1940).
- **Location**: Cytosol (the soluble fraction of the cytoplasm).
- **Significance**:
  - Major oxidative pathway for glucose
  - Employed by all tissues
  - Tightly regulated pathway
  - Mainly catabolic (ATP production) but has some anabolic features (amphibolic)
- **History**:
  - Louis Pasteur (1854-1864): discovered fermentation was caused by microorganisms
  - Edward Buchner (1897): demonstrated fermentation could occur in cell-free systems

---

## General Overview

- Glucose is converted to pyruvate while generating **2 ATP** and **2 NADH**.
- The pathway has **10 enzyme-catalyzed reactions** divided into **two phases**.

---

## Phase 1 — Energy Investment Phase (Steps 1-5)

**Purpose**: Glucose is phosphorylated and cleaved to yield 2 molecules of **glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (GAP)**. Uses **2 ATP**.

**Summary**: A 6-carbon glucose molecule is converted to 2 molecules of 3-carbon glyceraldehyde. No NADH is harvested in this phase.

### Step 1 — Phosphorylation of Glucose

| Enzyme | Hexokinase (non-specific) or **Glucokinase** (glucose-specific, liver) |
|--------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Reaction | Glucose → **Glucose-6-phosphate** (G6P) |
| Cofactors | ATP donates the phosphate group |
| Significance | G6P cannot diffuse out of the cell — traps glucose inside |

**Hexokinase vs Glucokinase**:

| Feature | Hexokinase | Glucokinase |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Km for glucose | Low (high affinity) | High (low affinity) |
| Location | All cells | Liver cells |
| Inhibited by | G6P (feedback inhibition) | Fructose-6-phosphate (indirect), translocated to nucleus bound to GKRP |
| Function | Peripheral cells use glucose for energy | Acts when blood glucose is high (postprandial) — converts excess to glycogen |

**Glucokinase Regulatory Protein (GKRP)**:
- High fructose-6-phosphate → GK translocates to nucleus, binds GKRP → inactive
- High glucose → GK released from GKRP → enters cytosol → active

### Step 2 — Isomerization of Glucose-6-Phosphate

| Enzyme | **Glucose phosphate isomerase** |
|--------|--------------------------------|
| Reaction | Glucose-6-phosphate → **Fructose-6-phosphate** |
| Mechanism | Conversion of C-2 OH to a ketone (aldose → ketose) |

### Step 3 — Phosphorylation of Fructose-6-Phosphate

| Enzyme | **Phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1)** — **KEY REGULATORY ENZYME** |
|--------|----------------------------------------------------------------|
| Reaction | Fructose-6-phosphate → **Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate** |
| Cofactors | Requires **Mg²⁺** and **ATP** |
| Significance | 2nd ATP utilization step; **committed step** (rate-limiting) of glycolysis. Once F-1,6-BP is formed, glucose must go through glycolysis. |

**PFK-1 Structure**: Tetrameric protein (4 subunits) with tissue-specific isoenzymes:
- **M4** (muscle), **L4** (liver), **M3L**, **M2L2** (red cells), **ML3**

### Step 4 — Aldol Cleavage of Fructose-1,6-Bisphosphate

| Enzyme | **Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate aldolase** |
|--------|----------------------------------------|
| Reaction | F-1,6-BP (6C) → **Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate** (3C) + **Dihydroxyacetone phosphate** (DHAP, 3C) |

### Step 5 — Interconversion of Triose Phosphates

| Enzyme | **Triose phosphate isomerase (TPI)** |
|--------|--------------------------------------|
| Reaction | DHAP → **Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate** |
| Significance | Only GAP continues in the oxidative phase. Both trioses now enter Phase 2. |

> **Phase 1 Summary**: 6C glucose → 2 × 3C glyceraldehyde. **2 ATP consumed. 0 NADH produced.**

---

## Phase 2 — Energy Payoff Phase (Steps 6-10)

**Purpose**: Two molecules of GAP are oxidized and converted to pyruvate, generating **4 ATP** and **2 NADH**.

> **Important**: From this point, stoichiometry changes — each reaction occurs **×2** (once per GAP molecule).

### Step 6 — Oxidation of Glyceraldehyde-3-Phosphate

| Enzyme | **Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH)** |
|--------|------------------------------------------------------|
| Reaction | GAP + NAD⁺ + Pi → **1,3-Bisphosphoglycerate** (1,3-BPG) + NADH + H⁺ |
| Key Points | 1,3-BPG is a **high-energy phosphate** (ΔG°' = -11.8 kcal/mol, higher than ATP at -7.7 kcal/mol). Electrons transferred from GAP to NAD⁺. |
| Enzyme Info | Tetramer, MW 145 kDa. NAD⁺ is the coenzyme. |

**Substrate-Level Phosphorylation**: An enzyme transfers a phosphate group from an organic substrate to ADP, forming ATP. (Distinct from oxidative phosphorylation in the ETC.)

### Step 7 — Conversion of 1,3-BPG to 3-Phosphoglycerate

| Enzyme | **Phosphoglycerokinase** |
|--------|--------------------------|
| Reaction | 1,3-BPG + ADP → **3-Phosphoglycerate** + **ATP** |
| Significance | **First ATP-yielding reaction** in glycolysis. High-energy phosphate from 1,3-BPG transferred to ADP. |

### Step 8 — Isomerization of 3-Phosphoglycerate to 2-Phosphoglycerate

| Enzyme | **Phosphoglyceromutase** |
|--------|-------------------------|
| Reaction | 3-Phosphoglycerate → **2-Phosphoglycerate** |
| Mechanism | Transfers the phosphate group from C-3 to C-2 |

### Step 9 — Conversion of 2-Phosphoglycerate to Phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP)

| Enzyme | **Enolase** (requires **Mg²⁺**) |
|--------|--------------------------------|
| Reaction | 2-Phosphoglycerate → **Phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP)** + H₂O |
| Key Points | PEP is a **high-energy molecule** (ΔG°' = -14.8 kcal/mol — higher than ATP) |

### Step 10 — Synthesis of ATP and Conversion of PEP to Pyruvate

| Enzyme | **Pyruvate kinase** (tetrameric, allosteric enzyme) — **REGULATORY ENZYME** |
|--------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Reaction | PEP + ADP → **Pyruvate** + **ATP** |
| Significance | **Second ATP-yielding reaction**; another regulatory point in glycolysis. Pyruvate is the end product of aerobic glycolysis. |

## Net ATP Yield Calculation

### Aerobic Glycolysis

| Phase | ATP Consumed | ATP Produced | NADH Produced |
|-------|-------------|--------------|---------------|
| Phase 1 (Investment) | 2 ATP | 0 | 0 |
| Phase 2 (Payoff) | 0 | 4 ATP (2 × 2) | 2 NADH |
| **Net** | **-2** | **+4** | **+2 NADH** |

- **Net ATP from substrate-level phosphorylation**: **2 ATP**
- **Each NADH → 3 ATP** via ETC in mitochondria (older estimate) → **6 ATP from NADH**
- **Total aerobic glycolysis**: **~8 ATP** (2 from substrate-level + 6 from NADH oxidation)

### Anaerobic Glycolysis

- NADH **cannot** enter ETC (no O₂ and/or no mitochondria)
- NADH is used to reduce pyruvate to lactate → regenerates NAD⁺
- **Net ATP**: **2 ATP** only (from substrate-level phosphorylation)

### Glycolysis in RBCs

| Condition | Net ATP |
|-----------|---------|
| Without 2,3-BPG synthesis | 2 ATP |
| With 2,3-BPG shunt | 0 ATP |

> **Note**: Mature RBCs have no mitochondria, no nucleus, no ribosomes — depend entirely on glycolysis for energy.

## Regulation of Glycolysis

The **3 kinase-catalyzed irreversible reactions** are the regulatory points.

### 1. Hexokinase / Glucokinase

| Enzyme | Regulation |
|--------|-----------|
| **Hexokinase** | Inhibited by **G6P** (negative feedback) |
| **Glucokinase** | Indirectly inhibited by **fructose-6-phosphate**; translocates to nucleus bound to GKRP when F-6-P is high. Released by high glucose. |

### 2. Phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1) — **KEY REGULATOR**

| Effect | Molecules |
|--------|----------|
| **Inhibited by** | **ATP**, **Citrate** |
| **Activated by** | **AMP** |

### 3. Pyruvate Kinase

| Effect | Molecules |
|--------|----------|
| **Activated by** | **Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate** (feedforward activation) |
| **Covalent modification** | Phosphorylation by glucagon-stimulated kinase inactivates liver pyruvate kinase |

### Regulatory Mechanisms Summary

- **Rapid, short-term**: Allosteric regulation, covalent modifications
- **Slow, long-term**: Induction/repression of enzyme synthesis

### Hormonal Regulation

- **Insulin**: Stimulates glycolysis (induces glucokinase, PFK-1, pyruvate kinase)
- **Glucagon**: Inhibits glycolysis in liver (phosphorylates/inactivates pyruvate kinase)

## Fate of Pyruvate

### Aerobic Conditions

- Pyruvate enters mitochondria → **Pyruvate dehydrogenase complex** → **Acetyl-CoA** → enters **TCA cycle**

### Anaerobic Conditions (Muscle Cells & Mature RBCs)

- Pyruvate **cannot** be converted to Acetyl-CoA (limited mitochondria or no O₂)
- **NAD⁺ must be regenerated** for glycolysis to continue
- Pyruvate is reduced to **lactate** by **lactate dehydrogenase (LDH)**
  - Pyruvate + NADH + H⁺ → **Lactate + NAD⁺**
- This is **obligatory**: if lactate is not formed, all NAD⁺ converts to NADH → glycolysis stops → cell death

### Anaerobic Conditions (Yeast)

- Pyruvate → **Acetaldehyde** (by pyruvate decarboxylase) + CO₂
- Acetaldehyde → **Ethanol** (by alcohol dehydrogenase) + NAD⁺

---

## Anaerobic Glycolysis and Lactate

- NADH produced cannot be used by ETC for ATP production
- Less ATP production compared to aerobic glycolysis
- **Lactate is an obligatory end product** to regenerate NAD⁺

### Lactate Dehydrogenase (LDH)

- Catalyzes: Pyruvate + NADH + H⁺ ⇌ Lactate + NAD⁺
- Essential for regenerating NAD⁺ under anaerobic conditions

### RBC Metabolism

- Mature RBCs: no mitochondria, no nucleus, no ribosomes
- Depend entirely on glucose via glycolysis
- End product: **lactate**
- No net production or consumption of NADH
- Also generates **2,3-bisphosphoglycerate (2,3-BPG)**:
  - Major pathway for glucose consumption in erythrocytes
  - 2,3-BPG binds deoxyhemoglobin → decreases O₂ affinity → facilitates O₂ release in tissues

## Entry of Other Carbohydrates into Glycolysis

### Galactose

- Epimer of glucose (differs at C-4 hydroxyl position)
- **Galactokinase** phosphorylates galactose → Galactose-1-phosphate
- UDP-glucose + Gal-1-P → (by UDP-glucose Gal-1-P transferase) → Glucose-1-P + UDP-galactose
- Glucose-1-P → (isomerase) → Glucose-6-P → enters glycolysis

### Fructose

- Metabolism initiated by **fructokinase** (liver) and sperm cells
- Fructose → Fructose-1-phosphate (F1P)
- F1P is cleaved by **Aldolase B** → two 3C intermediates (one not phosphorylated)
- Liver effectively removes fructose from portal blood

### Polysaccharides and Disaccharides

- Glycogen/starch → glucose units → enter glycolysis
- Sucrose/lactose → hydrolyzed to monosaccharides → enter glycolysis

## Clinical Significance

### Pyruvate Kinase Deficiency in RBCs

- Results in **hemolytic anemia**
- Severity depends on:
  - Degree of PK deficiency
  - Compensation by 2,3-BPG

### RBC Glycolysis Summary

| Feature | Value |
|---------|-------|
| End product | Lactate |
| Net NADH change | None (consumed to make lactate) |
| ATP without 2,3-BPG | 2 ATP |
| ATP with 2,3-BPG shunt | 0 ATP |

### General Take-Home Messages

| Condition | Net ATP |
|-----------|---------|
| Aerobic glycolysis | **8 ATP** |
| Anaerobic glycolysis | **2 ATP** |
| RBC glycolysis (no 2,3-BPG) | **2 ATP** |
| RBC glycolysis (with 2,3-BPG) | **0 ATP** |

## Substrate-Level vs Oxidative Phosphorylation

| Feature | Substrate-Level Phosphorylation | Oxidative Phosphorylation |
|---------|--------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Mechanism | Phosphate transferred from substrate to ADP/GDP | ADP phosphorylated coupled to electron transfer to O₂ via ETC |
| Location | Cytosol or mitochondria | Inner mitochondrial membrane |
| Requires ETC | No | Yes |
| Example | Steps 7 & 10 of glycolysis; Step 5 of TCA cycle | NADH/FADH₂ → ETC → ATP |

---

## Warburg Effect

- Cancer cells preferentially use **aerobic glycolysis** (glycolysis even in the presence of O₂)
- Glucose is converted to lactate rather than being fully oxidized
- Allows rapid ATP production and provides biosynthetic precursors for rapidly dividing cells
- Basis for PET scan imaging (¹⁸F-fluorodeoxyglucose uptake)
