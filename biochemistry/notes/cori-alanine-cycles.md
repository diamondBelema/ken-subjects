# Cori Cycle, Alanine Cycle & Gluconeogenesis

---

## Part 1: Cori Cycle

### Discovery

- Discovered by **Carl Ferdinand Cori** and **Gerty Theresa Cori**
- Demonstrates metabolic cooperation between **skeletal muscle** (under low oxygen) and the **liver**

### Where Does It Occur?

- First discovered as cooperation between **skeletal muscle and liver**
- Also occurs between **liver and any tissue** that does not completely oxidize glucose to CO₂ and H₂O
- Tissues that continually produce lactic acid:
  - **Red blood cells**
  - **Immune cells** in lymph nodules
  - **Proliferating cells** in bone marrow
  - **Epithelial cells** in skin

### The Cycle

```
MUSCLE                              LIVER
  │                                   │
  ▼                                   │
Anaerobic glycolysis                  │
  │                                   │
  ▼                                   │
Pyruvate                             │
  │                                   │
  ▼                                   │
Lactate ──────── Blood ────────► Lactate
                                       │
                                       ▼
                                Pyruvate + NADH → NAD⁺
                                       │
                                       ▼
                                Gluconeogenesis
                                       │
                                       ▼
                                Glucose
                                       │
  Glucose ◄──────── Blood ◄──────────┘
  │
  ▼
Back to muscle
```

### Muscle Side

- Skeletal muscle (especially **fast-twitch fibers**) has limited mitochondria
- Under low O₂ (intense exercise):
  - Rate of pyruvate production by glycolysis **exceeds** rate of oxidation by TCA cycle
  - Less than 10% of pyruvate enters TCA cycle
  - O₂ uptake insufficient for aerobic oxidation of all NADH produced
- Pyruvate is converted to **lactate** (regenerates NAD⁺)
- Lactate diffuses into bloodstream

### Liver Side

- Lactate taken up by liver
- Converted back to pyruvate: Lactate + NAD⁺ → Pyruvate + NADH + H⁺
- Pyruvate channelled into **gluconeogenesis** → glucose
- Glucose released into bloodstream → delivered to muscle and other tissues (RBCs, neurons)

### Importance of the Cori Cycle

- Allows muscle to continue glycolysis under anaerobic conditions
- Liver bears the metabolic cost of glucose regeneration
- Compartmentalizes opposing pathways (glycolysis in muscle, gluconeogenesis in liver)

### Energy Cost

- **6 ATP per glucose** recycled
  - Muscle gains: 2 ATP (from anaerobic glycolysis)
  - Liver spends: 6 ATP (for gluconeogenesis)
  - **Net cost to organism: 4 ATP** per cycle

## Part 2: Alanine Cycle

### Overview

- Similar to Cori cycle but uses **alanine** instead of lactate as the transport molecule
- Also called the **glucose-alanine cycle**

### The Cycle

```
MUSCLE                              LIVER
  │                                   │
  ▼                                   │
Glucose → Pyruvate                    │
  │           │                       │
  │     + amino group                 │
  │     (from amino acid              │
  │      catabolism)                  │
  ▼           ▼                       │
  Transamination                      │
  │                                   │
  ▼                                   │
Alanine ──────── Blood ────────► Alanine
                                       │
                                       ▼
                                Transamination
                                (alanine → pyruvate)
                                       │
                                       ▼
                                Pyruvate
                                       │
                                       ▼
                                Gluconeogenesis
                                       │
                                       ▼
                                Glucose
                                       │
  Glucose ◄──────── Blood ◄──────────┘
  │
  ▼
Back to muscle
```

### Muscle Side

- Pyruvate + amino group → **alanine** (via transamination)
- Alanine may also be produced from:
  - Protein catabolism
  - Catabolism of **glucogenic amino acids** in muscle
- Alanine diffuses into blood → transported to liver

### Liver Side

- Alanine transaminated back to **pyruvate**
- Pyruvate channelled into **gluconeogenesis** → glucose
- Glucose sent back to muscles

### Key Enzymes

| Location | Enzyme | Reaction |
|----------|--------|----------|
| Muscle | **ALT** (Alanine aminotransferase) | Pyruvate + glutamate → Alanine + α-ketoglutarate |
| Liver | **ALT** (Alanine aminotransferase) | Alanine + α-ketoglutarate → Pyruvate + glutamate |

### Comparison with Cori Cycle

| Feature | Cori Cycle | Alanine Cycle |
|---------|-----------|---------------|
| Transport molecule | **Lactate** | **Alanine** |
| Nitrogen transport | No | **Yes** (carries amino groups to liver) |
| Muscle reaction | Pyruvate → Lactate (LDH) | Pyruvate + NH₂ → Alanine (ALT) |
| Liver reaction | Lactate → Pyruvate (LDH) | Alanine → Pyruvate (ALT) |
| Additional benefit | None | Detoxifies ammonia from muscle |
| Energy cost | 6 ATP per glucose | 6 ATP per glucose |

> **Key difference**: Alanine cycle also serves to transport **nitrogen** (amino groups) from muscle to liver safely as non-toxic alanine, rather than as toxic free ammonia.

## Part 3: Gluconeogenesis

### Definition

- The **manufacture of glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors**
- Literally: *neo* (new) + *genesis* (creation) of glucose
- Important when glycogen stores are depleted

### Location

- **Liver** (primary site — ~90%)
- **Kidney cortex** (contributes during prolonged fasting)

### Importance

- Tissues dependent on glucose:
  - **Exercising muscles**
  - **Red blood cells** (no mitochondria)
  - **Neurons** and **brain**
  - **Eyes**
- These tissues must obtain glucose for survival during:
  - Fasting
  - Prolonged exercise
  - Between meals

### Gluconeogenic Precursors

| Precursor | Source | Entry Point |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| **Lactate** | Anaerobic glycolysis (muscle, RBCs) | → Pyruvate |
| **Glucogenic amino acids** | Protein catabolism (muscle) | → Pyruvate, OAA, or TCA intermediates |
| **Glycerol** | Fat (triacylglycerol) catabolism | → DHAP (dihydroxyacetone phosphate) |
| **Propionate** | Odd-chain fatty acid oxidation | → Succinyl-CoA |
| **TCA cycle intermediates** | Various sources | → OAA (must enter Krebs cycle first) |

### Gluconeogenesis is NOT a Complete Reversal of Glycolysis

**7 of 10 glycolytic reactions are reversible** — same enzymes catalyze them in both directions.

**3 irreversible reactions** (catalyzed by kinases) must be **bypassed** using new enzymes:

| Glycolytic Step (Irreversible) | Glycolytic Enzyme | Gluconeogenic Bypass Enzyme |
|-------------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------------|
| Glucose → G6P | **Hexokinase/Glucokinase** | **Glucose-6-phosphatase** |
| F-6-P → F-1,6-BP | **Phosphofructokinase-1** | **Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase** |
| PEP → Pyruvate | **Pyruvate kinase** | **Pyruvate carboxylase** + **PEPCK** |

---

### Bypass Reaction 1: Pyruvate → Phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP)

This bypass requires **two enzymes** because the direct reversal of pyruvate kinase is thermodynamically unfavorable.

#### Step A: Pyruvate → Oxaloacetate

| | |
|---|---|
| **Enzyme** | **Pyruvate carboxylase** |
| **Reaction** | Pyruvate + CO₂ + ATP → **Oxaloacetate** + ADP + Pi |
| **Location** | Mitochondrial matrix |
| **Cofactor** | **Biotin** (vitamin — always involved in CO₂ fixation) |
| **Key Point** | Requires ATP. Fixes CO₂ onto pyruvate. |

#### Step B: Oxaloacetate → PEP

| | |
|---|---|
| **Enzyme** | **PEP carboxykinase (PEPCK)** |
| **Reaction** | Oxaloacetate + GTP → **PEP** + CO₂ + GDP |
| **Location** | Mitochondria or cytosol (species-dependent) |
| **Key Point** | Consumes **GTP** (another high-energy compound) |

#### Oxaloacetate Shuttle to Cytosol

- OAA cannot cross the inner mitochondrial membrane directly
- Converted to **malate** or **aspartate** → transported to cytosol → reconverted to OAA
- Malate shuttle also transfers reducing equivalents (NADH) to cytosol

---

### Bypass Reaction 2: Fructose-1,6-Bisphosphate → Fructose-6-Phosphate

| | |
|---|---|
| **Enzyme** | **Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase** |
| **Reaction** | Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate + H₂O → **Fructose-6-phosphate** + Pi |
| **Key Points** | Reversal of PFK-1. Allosteric enzyme — **regulates gluconeogenesis**. Inhibited by AMP and fructose-2,6-bisphosphate. |

> Fructose-6-phosphate is easily converted to glucose-6-phosphate by phosphoglucose isomerase (same enzyme as in glycolysis).

---

### Bypass Reaction 3: Glucose-6-Phosphate → Glucose

| | |
|---|---|
| **Enzyme** | **Glucose-6-phosphatase** |
| **Reaction** | Glucose-6-phosphate + H₂O → **Glucose** + Pi |
| **Location** | Endoplasmic reticulum membrane (liver, kidney) |
| **Key Points** | Allows liver to release free glucose into blood to maintain homeostasis. G6P is also a precursor for glycogen synthesis. |

---

### Energy Cost of Gluconeogenesis

| Reaction | ATP/GTP Cost |
|----------|-------------|
| 2 × Pyruvate → 2 × OAA | 2 ATP |
| 2 × OAA → 2 × PEP | 2 GTP |
| (Remaining glycolytic steps reversed) | — |
| 2 × F-1,6-BP → 2 × F-6-P | — (hydrolysis) |
| 2 × G6P → 2 × Glucose | — (hydrolysis) |
| **Total** | **6 ATP equivalents per glucose synthesized** |

> **Net**: 6 ATP consumed to synthesize 1 glucose. Glycolysis only yields 2 ATP — so gluconeogenesis is energetically expensive and driven by ATP from other sources (TCA cycle, fatty acid oxidation).

---

### Regulation of Gluconeogenesis

| Hormone | Effect | Mechanism |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| **Glucagon** | **Stimulates** gluconeogenesis | Activates F-1,6-BPase; inhibits PFK-1 (via fructose-2,6-bisphosphate); phosphorylates/inactivates pyruvate kinase |
| **Cortisol** | **Stimulates** gluconeogenesis | Induces gluconeogenic enzymes (PEPCK, G6Pase, amino acid transporters); promotes protein catabolism → amino acids |
| **Insulin** | **Inhibits** gluconeogenesis | Suppresses PEPCK and G6Pase gene expression; activates glycolysis |

### Allosteric Regulation

| Enzyme | Activated By | Inhibited By |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase** | Citrate | AMP, **Fructose-2,6-bisphosphate** |
| **Pyruvate carboxylase** | Acetyl-CoA | — |
| **PEPCK** | Glucagon, cortisol | Insulin |

> **Fructose-2,6-bisphosphate** is the most potent allosteric activator of PFK-1 and inhibitor of F-1,6-BPase — it acts as a reciprocal regulator of glycolysis and gluconeogenesis.

## Summary Table: Cori Cycle vs Alanine Cycle vs Gluconeogenesis

| Feature | Cori Cycle | Alanine Cycle | Gluconeogenesis |
|---------|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Transport molecule | Lactate | Alanine | N/A (pathway) |
| Tissues involved | Muscle ↔ Liver | Muscle ↔ Liver | Liver, kidney cortex |
| Nitrogen transported | No | Yes | N/A |
| Energy cost | 6 ATP/glucose | 6 ATP/glucose | 6 ATP/glucose |
| Purpose | Regenerate glucose for muscle | Regenerate glucose + detoxify NH₃ | Synthesize glucose from non-carb precursors |
| Key bypass enzymes | N/A | N/A | Pyruvate carboxylase, PEPCK, F-1,6-BPase, G6Pase |
