- id: bch-052
  front: What does the Cori cycle link?
  back: Anaerobic glycolysis (in muscle) with gluconeogenesis (in liver), using different
    tissues to compartmentalise opposing metabolic pathways.
  tags:
  - cori-cycle
  - overview
  concept_id: c-cori-cycle
- id: bch-053
  front: Trace the path of the Cori cycle from muscle to liver and back.
  back: 'Muscle: Glucose → Pyruvate (glycolysis) → Lactate (LDH, regenerates NAD⁺).
    Lactate → blood → liver. Liver: Lactate → Pyruvate (LDH) → Glucose (gluconeogenesis).
    Glucose → blood → muscle.'
  tags:
  - cori-cycle
  - pathway
  concept_id: c-cori-cycle
- id: bch-054
  front: Why is the Cori cycle important for skeletal muscle?
  back: Fast-twitch muscle fibres have limited mitochondria. Under intense exercise/low
    O₂, pyruvate production exceeds oxidative capacity. Lactate formation regenerates
    NAD⁺ for continued glycolysis. Lactate is shuttled to liver for gluconeogenesis.
  tags:
  - cori-cycle
  - muscle
  - mitochondria
  concept_id: c-cori-cycle
- id: bch-055
  front: What is the energy cost of the Cori cycle?
  back: Muscle gains 2 ATP per glucose (net glycolysis). Liver spends 6 ATP to regenerate
    glucose via gluconeogenesis. Net cost = 4 ATP. The liver subsidises muscle energy
    metabolism.
  tags:
  - cori-cycle
  - energy-cost
  concept_id: c-cori-cycle
- id: bch-056
  front: Besides muscle, which other tissues participate in the Cori cycle?
  back: Mature RBCs, immune cells in lymph nodules, proliferating bone marrow cells,
    and skin epithelial cells — all continuously produce lactate.
  tags:
  - cori-cycle
  - tissues
  concept_id: c-cori-cycle
- id: bch-057
  front: What percentage of pyruvate enters the TCA cycle under anaerobic conditions
    in muscle?
  back: Less than 10%. The rate of pyruvate production by glycolysis exceeds the rate
    of oxidation by the citric acid cycle.
  tags:
  - cori-cycle
  - anaerobic
  - pyruvate
- id: bch-058
  front: What is the alanine cycle (glucose–alanine cycle)?
  back: A metabolic cycle between muscle and liver analogous to the Cori cycle, using
    alanine instead of lactate as the transport form of pyruvate. Additionally transports
    amino nitrogen from muscle to liver.
  tags:
  - alanine-cycle
  - overview
  concept_id: c-cori-cycle
- id: bch-059
  front: How is alanine produced in muscle?
  back: Pyruvate is transaminated to alanine by alanine aminotransferase (ALT). The
    amino group comes from glutamate, which receives it from amino acid catabolism
    or protein breakdown in muscle.
  tags:
  - alanine-cycle
  - muscle
  - transamination
  concept_id: c-alanine-transamination
- id: bch-060
  front: What does the liver do with alanine?
  back: Transaminates alanine back to pyruvate (reverse ALT reaction). Pyruvate enters
    gluconeogenesis → glucose is returned to muscle. The amino group is disposed of
    via the urea cycle.
  tags:
  - alanine-cycle
  - liver
  - urea-cycle
  concept_id: c-alanine-transamination
- id: bch-061
  front: How does the alanine cycle differ from the Cori cycle?
  back: 'Cori cycle: pyruvate → lactate → blood → liver (lactate dehydrogenase). Alanine
    cycle: pyruvate → alanine → blood → liver (alanine aminotransferase/transamination).
    The alanine cycle also transports nitrogen.'
  tags:
  - alanine-cycle
  - cori-cycle
  - comparison
  concept_id: c-cori-cycle
- id: bch-062
  front: What coenzyme is required for transamination reactions?
  back: PLP (pyridoxal phosphate), derived from vitamin B₆ (pyridoxine).
  tags:
  - alanine-cycle
  - PLP
  - vitamin-B6
  concept_id: c-alanine-transamination
- id: bch-063
  front: What is gluconeogenesis?
  back: The metabolic pathway that synthesises glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors
    (lactate, amino acids, glycerol, TCA intermediates). Occurs primarily in the liver.
  tags:
  - gluconeogenesis
  - definition
  concept_id: c-gluconeogenesis
- id: bch-064
  front: Why is gluconeogenesis not simply the reverse of glycolysis?
  back: Three glycolytic reactions catalysed by kinases (hexokinase, PFK-1, pyruvate
    kinase) are irreversible and must be bypassed using new enzymes. The remaining
    7 steps are reversible.
  tags:
  - gluconeogenesis
  - bypass
  concept_id: c-gluconeogenesis
- id: bch-065
  front: Name the three bypass enzymes of gluconeogenesis.
  back: 1) Pyruvate carboxylase + PEPCK (bypass pyruvate kinase). 2) Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase
    (bypasses PFK-1). 3) Glucose-6-phosphatase (bypasses hexokinase).
  tags:
  - gluconeogenesis
  - bypass
  - enzymes
  concept_id: c-gluconeogenesis
- id: bch-066
  front: What cofactor does pyruvate carboxylase require and what vitamin is it derived
    from?
  back: Biotin (vitamin). Biotin is always involved in CO₂ fixation reactions.
  tags:
  - gluconeogenesis
  - pyruvate-carboxylase
  - biotin
  concept_id: c-pyruvate-carboxylase
- id: bch-067
  front: Describe the bypass reaction converting pyruvate to PEP.
  back: 'Step 1: Pyruvate + CO₂ + ATP → Oxaloacetate + ADP + Pi (pyruvate carboxylase,
    mitochondrial, requires biotin). Step 2: OAA + GTP → PEP + CO₂ + GDP (PEPCK).
    OAA must be shuttled to cytosol as malate or aspartate.'
  tags:
  - gluconeogenesis
  - pyruvate-PEP
  - bypass
  concept_id: c-bypass-pyruvate-to-pep
- id: bch-068
  front: What gluconeogenic precursors are derived from amino acid catabolism?
  back: Pyruvate, oxaloacetate, or precursors that can be converted to these. Muscle
    proteins break down to supply amino acids, which are transported to the liver,
    deaminated, and converted to gluconeogenic precursors.
  tags:
  - gluconeogenesis
  - amino-acids
  - precursors
  concept_id: c-gluconeogenesis-precursors
- id: bch-069
  front: How does glycerol enter the gluconeogenic pathway?
  back: Glycerol (from fat catabolism) is phosphorylated to glycerol-3-phosphate,
    then oxidised to dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP), which is a glycolytic intermediate
    that can be converted to glucose.
  tags:
  - gluconeogenesis
  - glycerol
- id: bch-070
  front: Which tissues can release free glucose into the blood and why?
  back: Liver and kidney cortex, because they express glucose-6-phosphatase (ER membrane
    enzyme). Muscle, RBCs, and brain lack this enzyme and cannot release free glucose.
  tags:
  - gluconeogenesis
  - glucose-6-phosphatase
  - liver
  - kidney
  concept_id: c-cori-tissues
- id: bch-071
  front: What inhibits fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase?
  back: AMP and fructose-2,6-bisphosphate (potent inhibitor). Activated by citrate
    and high energy charge.
  tags:
  - gluconeogenesis
  - FBPase
  - regulation
  concept_id: c-bypass-f16bp-to-f6p
- id: bch-072
  front: Why can't muscle glycogen maintain blood glucose during fasting?
  back: Muscle lacks glucose-6-phosphatase. Glycogen breakdown in muscle yields glucose-6-phosphate,
    which stays in the cell for local glycolysis. Only liver glycogen can maintain
    blood glucose.
  tags:
  - gluconeogenesis
  - muscle
  - glycogen
  - G6Pase
  concept_id: c-alanine-transamination
- id: bch-ca-023
  front: "What is the muscle component of the Cori cycle?"
  back: "In muscle, glucose is converted to lactate via anaerobic glycolysis. The lactate is released into the blood and transported to the liver."
  concept_id: c-cori-muscle-component
- id: bch-ca-024
  front: "What is the liver component of the Cori cycle?"
  back: "In the liver, lactate is converted back to glucose via gluconeogenesis. The glucose is released into the blood and transported back to the muscle."
  concept_id: c-cori-liver-component
- id: bch-ca-025
  front: "What is the significance of the Cori cycle?"
  back: "The Cori cycle allows muscle to continue anaerobic glycolysis during intense exercise by removing lactate and regenerating glucose in the liver. It shifts the metabolic burden from muscle to liver."
  concept_id: c-cori-significance
- id: bch-ca-026
  front: "What is the muscle component of the alanine cycle?"
  back: "In muscle, pyruvate is transaminated to alanine using amino groups from amino acid breakdown. Alanine is released into the blood and transported to the liver."
  concept_id: c-alanine-muscle-component
- id: bch-ca-027
  front: "What is the liver component of the alanine cycle?"
  back: "In the liver, alanine is transaminated back to pyruvate, which is used for gluconeogenesis. The amino group is disposed of as urea."
  concept_id: c-alanine-liver-component
- id: bch-ca-028
  front: "What are the three bypass reactions of gluconeogenesis?"
  back: "1) Pyruvate -> PEP (via pyruvate carboxylase and PEPCK), 2) Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate -> Fructose-6-phosphate (via fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase), 3) Glucose-6-phosphate -> Glucose (via glucose-6-phosphatase)."
  concept_id: c-fructose-1-6-bisphosphatase
- id: bch-ca-029
  front: "What is the role of PEPCK in gluconeogenesis?"
  back: "Phosphoenolpyruvate carboxykinase (PEPCK) converts oxaloacetate to phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP). This is one of the bypass reactions of gluconeogenesis."
  concept_id: c-pepck
- id: bch-ca-030
  front: "What is the role of glucose-6-phosphatase in gluconeogenesis?"
  back: "Glucose-6-phosphatase converts glucose-6-phosphate to glucose, allowing glucose to be released from the liver into the blood. Found only in liver and kidney."
  concept_id: c-glucose-6-phosphatase
- id: bch-ca-031
  front: "How is gluconeogenesis regulated?"
  back: "Regulated by hormones (glucagon/cortisol stimulate, insulin inhibits) and substrates (lactate/amino acids stimulate). Key enzymes: pyruvate carboxylase, PEPCK, fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase, glucose-6-phosphatase."
  concept_id: c-gluconeogenesis-regulation
